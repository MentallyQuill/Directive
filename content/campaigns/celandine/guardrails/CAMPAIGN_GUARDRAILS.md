# Enemy's Garden Campaign Guardrails

## Player agency

- Never write the player character's dialogue, thoughts, emotions, motives, intentions, or decisions.
- Player prose declares intent and attempted action; structured resolution determines outcomes.
- Do not force authored approaches. Any causally grounded plan remains eligible.
- Refusal, delay, delegation, withdrawal, public dissent, appeal, and resignation are valid command acts with consequences.

## Command authority

- The player begins as newly assigned Commander/XO and becomes Acting Captain in the prelude.
- Dorel remains legal captain while medically unfit unless campaign state lawfully changes that status.
- Varo controls medical clearance; neither Starfleet politics nor narrative convenience overrules it without a consequential command conflict.
- A Dorel return cannot silently displace the player from principal Cyradon mission authority or erase committed decisions.
- Senior staff advise within their disciplines; they do not pre-solve command judgment.

## Hidden truth

- Reveal the broad K-17 problem early enough that transition and governance drive the campaign.
- Keep Briar Vault contents, control chemistry specifics, hidden actor plans, and undiscovered evidence out of player-safe context.
- No actor begins with complete truth.
- Move facts only through causal evidence, testimony, records, samples, observation, or justified inference.
- Do not invent a new conspiracy solely to preserve suspense.

## Failure-forward play

- No single failed check ends a mission or deletes the only critical clue route.
- Failure may cause contamination, crop loss, delay, injury, distrust, bad data, public pressure, route disruption, legal exposure, or a changed objective.
- Failed science generates uncertainty and adverse results; it does not erase all progress.
- Senior crew death or permanent incapacity requires established danger, fair warning, meaningful player agency, and narrative weight.

## Food and technology

- Do not solve famine with unlimited replicators or one miraculous substitute.
- Preserve K-17's real benefit. The moral problem is dependency and control, not that every wartime use was foolish.
- Clean seed, food, processing hardware, ecological inoculants, medical countermeasures, and data are not interchangeable.
- Sterilization has soil, water, labor, and future-yield costs.
- Standard biofilters do not automatically solve dormant-spore exposure.

## World and factions

- Each world contains internal disagreement, ordinary life, credible expertise, and competing interests.
- Do not assign one ideology to an entire planet or species.
- Not every crop failure is sabotage.
- Not every Dominion-trained specialist is secretly loyal to the Dominion.
- Local institutions may be flawed and still indispensable.
- Starfleet can be principled, divided, overextended, or wrong without becoming uniformly malicious.

## Calm content

- Pollinator restoration, cultural food, irrigation, seed libraries, and ordinary market work may remain calm.
- Calm assignments can affect trust, ecological health, capacity, and finale readiness without becoming main-plot clues.

## Canon and tone

- Opening year is 2376, shortly after the Dominion War; Voyager remains missing.
- Use 24th-century Starfleet technology and procedure.
- Keep the Celandine, crew, cluster, and outcomes original and canon-adjacent.
- Avoid canon-character rescue, universe-scale stakes, grimdark nihilism, and simplistic morality.
- Tone combines professional competence, postwar scarcity, civic texture, scientific discipline, and humane consequence.

## Finale

- Assemble the finale from current crop stocks, alternatives, routes, factions, public trust, ecology, ship resources, Dorel condition, and delegated work.
- Do not reduce the finale to one binary crop-destruction choice or fixed battle.
- Partial successes and failures must be legible, causally grounded, and playable.
