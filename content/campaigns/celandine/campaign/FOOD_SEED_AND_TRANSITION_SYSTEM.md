# Food, Seed, and Transition System

## Purpose

The campaign models food as infrastructure, ecology, labor, law, and time. The Director should never treat all relief cargo as fungible or assume one scientific discovery instantly becomes regional capacity.

## Resource families

### Immediate food

Finished grain, protein, preserved produce, replicated staples, and emergency ration packs prevent near-term hunger. They consume cargo volume, distribution labor, storage, energy, and political trust. They do not create the next harvest.

### Planting stock

Clean seed, heritage lines, Grayleaf substitute lots, edited K-17, parent strains, tissue culture, and soil inoculants differ in purity, viability, yield, ecological fit, custody, and reproducibility. Seed must be multiplied, tested, documented, and planted within local windows.

### Production infrastructure

Processing towers, irrigation, reservoirs, weather control, cold storage, laboratory capacity, field vehicles, cargo handling, and civilian maintenance determine whether biological resources become food. Celandine engineering feedstock competes with ship repair and other promises.

### Knowledge and custody

Genomic data, control chemistry, marker maps, farmer practice, consent records, access keys, audit logs, and legal standards are assets that can protect transition or create surveillance and monopoly. Custody should name who can use, verify, copy, challenge, and revoke access.

## World dependency profiles

- **Arda II:** high-yield K-17 grain, centralized processing towers, export contracts, and the most immediate planting pressure.
- **Khepri:** fragile water reserves, heritage seed cooperatives, clean-acre multiplication, and strong opposition to external ownership.
- **Sorell:** dense population, ration identity, centralized allocation, and political pressure for rapid, measurable continuity.
- **Vela Nacre:** wetlands, fisheries, microbial interdependence, and the highest risk that sterilization destroys the system being protected.
- **Lysa's Moon:** substitute-crop trials, volunteer medicine, clean seed multiplication, and pressure to suppress adverse data.
- **Cinder Reach:** processing, freight, labor, insurance, and the practical corridor through which every other policy must move.

## Adjudication sequence

1. Identify the intended benefit: calories, seed, ecological recovery, evidence, processing, transport, legitimacy, or time.
2. Identify the relevant stock, system, and custodian.
3. Check route, labor, quarantine, storage, energy, planting window, and verification constraints.
4. Resolve the player's actual plan and committed mechanics.
5. Update tracks, clocks, fronts, assets, route state, and local conditions.
6. Narrate visible effects through markets, kitchens, fields, clinics, docks, hearings, and crew workload.
7. Record obligations: repayment, maintenance, audit, consent, review, or future planting support.

## K-17 control and biomarker constraints

The K-17 marker is probabilistic and environmental. It is not a perfect personal locator or proof of intent. Orbital detection can identify patterns and geography under favorable conditions, especially when combined with ration, labor, or medical records. The danger is strongest when multiple data systems are correlated.

Control chemistry can alter dormancy, fertility, stress response, or synchronized growth in selected crop families. Not every K-17 line responds identically. Countermeasures may include signal masking, edited lines, chemical antagonists, mixed planting, physical isolation, weather changes, soil competition, or destruction. Each has food, ecology, labor, and governance consequences.

## Scientific standards

A credible result should use independent samples, explicit consent, chain of custody, cross-laboratory replication, adverse-result reporting, and stated uncertainty. Failed trials create information and cost; they do not become narrative dead ends. Lareen and Cardassian specialists may provide indispensable expertise without receiving unilateral custody or moral absolution.

## Transition doctrines

The player may produce different world-specific plans. Common patterns include:

- controlled K-17 continuity with dated exit milestones;
- mixed planting and ecological competition;
- rapid clean-seed replacement where acreage and processing permit;
- local heritage restoration;
- edited K-17 under accountable custody;
- emergency reserve held under partitioned authority;
- distributed community seed banks;
- centralized regional management;
- Starfleet technical trusteeship pending local capacity.

No doctrine is automatically correct. The campaign evaluates whether people can eat, challenge authority, verify claims, maintain systems, protect ecological function, and eventually leave emergency dependence.

## Replicator rule

Replicators are targeted relief and time-buying capacity, not a universal answer. State the energy, feedstock, maintenance, distribution, and opportunity cost. A replicated meal can prevent immediate suffering; a replicated seed lab component can protect multiple future harvests. Command decides where bridge capacity matters most.
