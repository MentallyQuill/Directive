## Document Scope

This document develops **Ashes of Peace** into a complete first campaign for **Directive**. It defines the campaign's setting, mystery, factions, mission sequence, optional assignments, recurring non-player characters, command dilemmas, consequence tracks, finale, and epilogue.

It is a campaign-author and mission-director document. It contains complete spoilers, including hidden causes, faction secrets, and alternative endings. It is separate from the technical architecture document and from the broader game design document.

## 1. Campaign Overview

### 1.1 High concept

The Dominion War is over, but peace has not restored the political order that existed before it.

The Intrepid-class **U.S.S. Breckinridge** has completed a four-month repair and modernization period at Utopia Planitia following wartime damage. Most of its original crew was dispersed while the ship remained unavailable, and Starfleet reconstituted the complement from returning veterans, two major transfer cohorts, and individual specialists.

Twenty-five days after leaving Mars, the Breckinridge conducts a rendezvous with a long-range personnel shuttle carrying its newly assigned executive officer: the player character. The ship is approximately ten days from the **Asterion Reach**, where it will begin a ninety-day stabilization, survey, and relief deployment near the former Cardassian frontier.

During the war, Starfleet withdrew ships and personnel from the Reach to support more critical fronts. The region's colonies survived by forming the **Asterion Mutual Aid Compact**, pooling civilian vessels, food, defense platforms, medical resources, and emergency authority. The Compact was created as a temporary measure. Its members no longer agree that it should be dissolved.

The Breckinridge arrives amid forged Starfleet orders, missing relief cargoes, counterfeit transponders, disputed arrests, Cardassian refugees, and growing suspicion that someone is attempting to provoke the Reach into open conflict.

The deeper cause is **Pale Lantern**, a distributed Dominion contingency system built to weaponize distrust after a Dominion retreat. It does not conquer territory directly. It fabricates convincing evidence, redirects traffic, manipulates authentication systems, and gives frightened local actors the information they need to believe that preemption is their only safe option.

Pale Lantern is dangerous because it does not create the region's grievances. It understands and exploits grievances that already exist.

### 1.2 Campaign thesis

> Peace is not the absence of an enemy. Peace is the difficult work of deciding what must be repaired, what must be changed, and who is allowed to participate in that decision.

The player is not asked whether Federation ideals are worthwhile. The player is asked what those ideals require after Federation institutions have failed to live up to them.

### 1.3 Genre and tone

The campaign combines:

- Postwar political drama.
- Scientific and technical investigation.
- Humanitarian response.
- Starfleet procedural conflict.
- Local diplomacy and contested sovereignty.
- Limited tactical action with meaningful consequences.
- Character-centered command decisions.

The tone should remain recognizably Star Trek: serious but not nihilistic, skeptical without becoming cynical, and committed to the possibility that people and institutions can improve through truth, accountability, and deliberate action.

### 1.4 Expected length

A full campaign contains:

- One prelude mission covering the player's mid-route rendezvous and the final ten days of transit.
- Eight main-quest chapters.
- Three Open Orders intervals.
- Nine designed side assignments, of which the player will ordinarily complete four to six.
- Recurring shipboard B-plots between assignments.
- A multi-front finale.
- One epilogue mission and command review.

At a deliberate text-roleplay pace, this supports approximately **35 to 60 substantial play sessions**, depending on how much time the player spends in freeform conversation, investigation, crew scenes, and optional assignments.

## 2. Time, Place, and Transit Baseline

### 2.1 Canon-era anchor

The campaign opens on **stardate 53049.2**, placing it in 2376 and contemporaneous with the early sixth season of *Star Trek: Voyager*.

The Dominion War has ended. Cardassia has suffered catastrophic destruction. Starfleet is demobilizing some wartime formations while simultaneously attempting to restore patrol coverage, rebuild damaged infrastructure, return displaced personnel, and re-establish civilian confidence.

Voyager remains in the Delta Quadrant. Its situation exists in public and institutional awareness, but the Breckinridge does not intersect with Voyager's immediate story.

### 2.2 The Breckinridge before the campaign

The Breckinridge entered service approximately three years before the campaign as one of Starfleet's early Intrepid-class assignments. Captain Mara Whitaker commanded the ship through its first commission, and Lieutenant Commander Hadrik Bronn served as its tactical and security chief.

The ship spent much of its first year on survey, patrol, and diplomatic-support assignments before being drawn increasingly into wartime operations. By late 2375 it had accumulated serious but repairable damage, deferred maintenance, and systems wear from convoy escort, rapid-response, and reconnaissance duty.

The exact battle that forced the ship into the yard may be finalized later, but the damage baseline is:

- Structural fatigue around the port variable-geometry nacelle assembly.
- Partial failure of secondary shield-grid distribution trunks.
- Heat and plasma damage in two engineering support compartments.
- Loss of several bio-neural processing assemblies.
- Tactical-computer damage and compromised wartime authentication hardware.
- Accumulated maintenance that could no longer be deferred safely.

The ship was never considered lost or beyond economical repair. Its condition required a major yard period at precisely the time Starfleet had the least spare capacity.

### 2.3 Four-month repair and modernization at Utopia Planitia

The Breckinridge entered **Utopia Planitia Fleet Yards in Mars orbit** at approximately stardate 52650. The repair and modernization period lasted about four months, extending through the final phase of the Dominion War and into the first weeks of peace.

The work took longer than the physical damage alone would normally require because:

- Wartime priority repeatedly redirected yard personnel to ships needed immediately at the front.
- Replacement structural members, bio-neural components, and shield-control hardware were in short supply.
- Damage discovered during disassembly expanded the repair scope.
- Starfleet elected to install a limited postwar modernization package while major systems were already open.
- Certification teams were overcommitted across the fleet.

The completed work includes:

- Structural repair and recalibration of the nacelle articulation system.
- Replacement and recalibration of damaged bio-neural gel packs.
- Revised command-network authentication and compartmentalization developed from wartime security failures.
- Long-range sensor processor improvements.
- Updated emergency-power segmentation.
- Revised tactical software compatible with postwar Starfleet countermeasure libraries.
- Improved warp-field wake monitoring and nacelle-control diagnostics.
- Completion of maintenance deferred during wartime service.

This is a modernization and restoration, not a class-level rebuild. The Breckinridge has no singular experimental weapon, impossible propulsion system, or campaign-breaking technology.

The ship is certified for service, but several upgraded systems still require integrated validation under sustained deployment conditions. The voyage from Utopia Planitia therefore serves as a conservative shakedown as well as a transfer flight.

### 2.4 Crew dispersal and reconstitution

Starfleet could not hold an Intrepid-class complement idle for four months during the war. Most of the original Breckinridge crew was reassigned to active vessels, starbases, replacement pools, medical leave, or specialist duties. When the ship became available again, many of those officers and crew were already essential elsewhere or had accepted permanent postings.

The reconstituted complement has four broad sources:

- A small body of returning Breckinridge veterans, concentrated among experienced enlisted personnel and junior officers.
- A large transfer cohort from a damaged wartime patrol or escort vessel.
- A second large cohort from a science and support vessel undergoing postwar staffing changes.
- Individual transfers selected for Intrepid-class operations, the new modernization package, or the Asterion assignment.

Fewer than one quarter of the current complement served aboard before the refit. Among the senior bridge staff, only **Captain Whitaker and Lieutenant Commander Bronn** are returning members of the ship's first crew.

The remainder of the established senior staff is new to the Breckinridge:

- Kieran Vale joined as flight-control officer.
- Priya Nayar joined as operations officer.
- Rowan Saye joined as chief science officer.
- Miriam Sato joined as chief medical officer.
- Imani Cross joined as chief engineer, in part because of her experience with Intrepid-class systems validation and bio-neural integration.

These officers embarked at Utopia Planitia and have already spent twenty-five days traveling together by the time the player arrives. They have formed preliminary working relationships, discovered points of friction, and established routines that are useful but not yet settled.

Bronn served as **acting executive officer** for the yard departure and initial shakedown transit while retaining tactical responsibility. He accepted the role as a temporary necessity and has no expectation of keeping it. Priya has handled much of the practical cross-department scheduling beneath that arrangement. The player's arrival therefore completes the command structure rather than creating it from nothing.

The player character is the last senior appointment to arrive because they are transferring from another posting. Their previous ship, station, or assignment remains part of character creation.

### 2.5 Location of the Asterion Reach

For campaign purposes, the entrance to the Asterion Reach lies approximately **twenty light-years from Sol**, on the Federation side of a historically contested region near the former Cardassian frontier.

The Reach itself spans roughly eight light-years. Travel between its principal systems generally takes hours rather than weeks, allowing the Breckinridge to conduct side assignments without repeatedly abandoning the main theater.

This is an original campaign location. Its placement is intentionally broad enough to avoid contradicting incomplete and inconsistent onscreen maps.

### 2.6 Transit and rendezvous baseline

The Breckinridge is not responding to an emergency. Captain Whitaker authorizes a conservative flight plan through Federation territory that observes the standing Warp 5 restriction where practical, uses lower speeds in sensitive or heavily traveled corridors, and reserves sustained high warp for actual operational need.

**Campaign route standard: approximately thirty-five days from Utopia Planitia to the Galen Passage entrance of the Asterion Reach.**

The internal chronology is:

- **Stardate 52980.8:** Breckinridge departs Utopia Planitia.
- **Twenty-five days underway:** The reconstituted crew conducts shakedown operations without its permanent XO.
- **Stardate 53049.2:** The ship drops to impulse at a charted transfer waypoint and receives the player aboard from a long-range Starfleet personnel shuttle.
- **Ten days remain:** Approximately 5.5 to 6 light-years separate the rendezvous point from the Galen Passage.
- **Stardate 53076.6:** Baseline arrival at the Asterion Reach, subject to delays created during the prelude.

The player's shuttle is not assumed to have traveled from Mars. It reaches the rendezvous from the player's previous posting, a nearby starbase, another vessel, or a transfer hub selected during character creation.

The travel estimate is an internal campaign standard rather than a claim that all onscreen Star Trek travel times are consistent. It assumes a Warp 5-equivalent average across the route, with time lost to departure clearance, approach operations, navigational restrictions, and post-refit checks.

### 2.7 Why the final ten days matter

The prelude begins after the crew has already spent three and a half weeks together. This creates a more useful command situation than having everyone meet simultaneously at the shipyard.

The final ten days allow the player to:

- Join a ship with functioning routines without inheriting a fully settled culture.
- Learn which relationships formed during the first twenty-five days.
- Decide which provisional practices should be formalized, revised, or stopped.
- Establish a working relationship with Captain Whitaker.
- Receive acting-XO authority from Bronn without turning the transition into a rivalry.
- Assess unresolved post-refit technical and personnel concerns.
- Handle one contained civilian diversion that reveals the crew through action.
- Arrive in the Asterion Reach with initial consequences already attached to the player's command style.

The prelude should feel calm enough for conversation and familiarization, but consequential enough that the player is already commanding rather than clicking through introductions.

## 3. Player Role and Starting Directives

### 3.1 Billet

The player is a Starfleet Commander transferring from another posting and assigned as:

- Executive Officer of the U.S.S. Breckinridge.
- Second-in-command to Captain Whitaker.
- Principal coordinator of shipboard operations.
- Chair of most senior-staff planning sessions.
- Usual commander of away missions and multi-department responses.
- Acting captain whenever Whitaker delegates command or becomes unavailable.

Whitaker retains final command authority. She should not become a quest terminal or a constant veto. Her function is to establish strategic intent, legal boundaries, and ship-wide risk, then require the player to turn those constraints into an executable plan.

### 3.2 Initial Starfleet orders

The Breckinridge's formal mission is to:

1. Restore reliable Starfleet presence in the Asterion Reach.
2. Protect civilian life and freedom of navigation.
3. Support humanitarian and reconstruction operations.
4. Survey wartime damage to communications, navigation, and subspace infrastructure.
5. Investigate counterfeit Starfleet signals and missing relief cargo.
6. Avoid prejudicing ongoing Federation negotiations concerning the Asterion Mutual Aid Compact.
7. Coordinate with, but not assume direct command over, local civil authorities unless a lawful emergency requires it.

### 3.3 Initial tensions within the orders

The orders contain deliberate ambiguity:

- "Restore Starfleet presence" may conflict with local self-government.
- "Protect navigation" may require inspecting or seizing civilian vessels.
- "Support reconstruction" may preserve institutions that failed during the war.
- "Avoid prejudicing negotiations" may encourage secrecy when disclosure is ethically necessary.
- "Coordinate with local authorities" does not define which authority is legitimate when several claim jurisdiction.

The player should never receive one final line explaining what Starfleet "really wants." Different officials want different outcomes.

### 3.4 Campaign values under pressure

The campaign is designed to challenge values such as:

- Starfleet principles matter most when they are costly.
- Authority must remain accountable.
- No community is expendable.
- The crew deserves the truth.
- Peace requires participation, not merely compliance.
- Security cannot be built on permanent emergency powers.
- Evidence must be followed even when it implicates one's own institution.

The player may begin with other personal Values. The campaign should frame conflicts around those chosen Values whenever possible.

## 4. The Asterion Reach

### 4.1 Regional profile

The Asterion Reach is not a single star system. It is a loose region of Federation colonies, industrial stations, shipping lanes, and small non-aligned settlements positioned along an old commercial route toward Cardassian space.

Before the war, the Reach was politically unremarkable. Its worlds depended on Starfleet patrols, Federation shipping guarantees, and administrative support from distant sector offices.

During the war:

- Patrol coverage was sharply reduced.
- Civilian convoys were requisitioned or redirected.
- Several colonies received evacuation orders that could not be fulfilled.
- Food and medical shortages became severe.
- Cardassian and Dominion units intermittently crossed the region.
- Local governments pooled authority under the Asterion Mutual Aid Compact.
- Civilian defense crews operated obsolete platforms and converted freighters.
- Informal trade and communications networks replaced official systems.

The Compact saved lives. It also accumulated emergency powers, weapons, intelligence archives, and political legitimacy that its founders had never expected to retain.

This map is functional rather than astronomical. Exact stellar coordinates can remain generated within a fixed regional layout.

### 4.3 Principal locations

#### Asterion Station

A former Starfleet logistics and sensor station now jointly operated by a reduced Starfleet detachment and Compact personnel. It contains:

- Regional traffic control.
- Damaged long-range communications arrays.
- Limited repair and medical facilities.
- Offices for Federation reconstruction teams.
- Compact liaison offices.
- Warehouses filled with disputed wartime materiel.

Asterion Station is the campaign's recurring neutral ground and political pressure point.

#### Pelion System

The largest population center in the Reach and seat of the Compact Council. Pelion's orbital habitats produced food, life-support components, and small craft during the war.

Pelion wants formal recognition of the Compact's emergency institutions before reintegration proceeds.

#### Demeris System

A Federation colony that remained accessible to Cardassian civilian authorities after Starfleet withdrew. Local leaders accepted Cardassian protection and trade to survive. Some Federation officials now regard those arrangements as collaboration.

Demeris considers itself abandoned, not disloyal.

#### Hecate Drift

A field of long-period comets, sensor shadows, and unstable subspace eddies. Smugglers used it before the war. Dominion forces later concealed a Pale Lantern relay inside one of its largest bodies.

#### Orison Belt

A mined asteroid belt containing obsolete automated defense platforms, salvage yards, and sensor pickets. Several systems are still controlled by wartime command codes.

#### Helix Yard

A civilian repair complex built around Karth, an industrial moon. It kept dozens of relief vessels operating through improvised repairs. Its systems are effective, undocumented, and increasingly unsafe.

#### Suvek Relief Enclave

A postwar settlement housing Cardassian civilians, former laborers, medical evacuees, and mixed families displaced by the collapse of Cardassian authority. It is supplied by both official relief agencies and informal Compact routes.

### 4.4 Everyday life in the Reach

The region should not feel like one uninterrupted emergency. Normal life persists:

- Cargo crews argue over docking priorities.
- Colonists reopen schools and civic institutions.
- Veterans attempt to return to civilian work.
- Cardassian and Federation families search for missing relatives.
- Local merchants resist restored regulations.
- Children treat obsolete defense shelters as familiar public spaces.
- Compact officials debate budgets, elections, and demobilization.
- Starfleet officers discover that gratitude and trust are not interchangeable.

These details create the calm intervals that make later escalation matter.

## 5. Campaign Themes

### 5.1 Abandonment and obligation

The Federation did not maliciously choose to sacrifice the Reach, but its strategic decisions had local victims. The campaign asks what is owed to communities that survived without the institutions that claimed authority over them.

### 5.2 Emergency power

The Compact's authority was justified by crisis. The crisis is ending unevenly. Some powers remain necessary; others now protect the people who hold them.

### 5.3 Truth and institutional trust

Truth does not automatically restore trust. It can expose wrongdoing, destabilize negotiations, end careers, or create new dangers. Concealment may preserve short-term order while destroying the possibility of legitimate peace.

### 5.4 Security through relationship versus security through control

Priya, Bronn, Whitaker, Compact leaders, and local defense officials embody different answers to the same question: what makes another party's future behavior predictable enough to permit peace?

### 5.5 Technical systems as political systems

Pale Lantern, authentication codes, traffic networks, defense platforms, and repair infrastructure are never merely equipment. Whoever controls them possesses authority.

### 5.6 The stories institutions tell about sacrifice

The Breckinridge's Wolf 359 namesake, the Reach's wartime survival, Starfleet's reconstruction narrative, and Cardassian accounts of defeat all compete to define whose sacrifice legitimizes present authority.

## 6. Director-Only Campaign Truth

### 6.1 What Pale Lantern is

**Pale Lantern** is a distributed Dominion contingency architecture deployed during the final years of the Dominion War.

It is not a sentient artificial intelligence, a Founder, or a single superweapon. It is a collection of specialized systems linked by covert subspace protocols:

- Authentication theft and replay tools.
- Transponder and sensor-signature emulation.
- Traffic-control intrusion packages.
- Predictive models of political and military behavior.
- Hidden communications relays.
- Interfaces to abandoned automated defense systems.
- Libraries of forged orders, casualty reports, quarantine notices, and diplomatic messages.

Its purpose is to make a region govern itself as though Dominion infiltration remains everywhere. Once institutions begin mistrusting their own records and one another's intentions, Pale Lantern encourages each actor to centralize power and preempt imagined threats.

The system's strategic instruction is simple:

> Increase the probability that local powers will interpret defensive action by another party as preparation for attack.

### 6.2 Why it remains active

Pale Lantern survived because no single installation contained the whole system. Several nodes were destroyed or abandoned. Others remained dormant and resumed limited coordination when local operators accessed them.

Three groups now interact with it:

1. **Compact hardliners** use partial functions to monitor shipping, create leverage, and demonstrate that Starfleet cannot guarantee regional security.
2. **Criminal and survival networks** use stolen route data, authentication fragments, and supply diversions without understanding the larger architecture.
3. **Starfleet Intelligence Project Farwatch** discovered one node after the war and deliberately left it operating under surveillance to identify Dominion holdouts and illicit weapons traffic.

Farwatch's genuine Starfleet handshakes gave Pale Lantern updated credentials and confirmed that its escalation methods still worked.

### 6.3 No single mastermind

The campaign must resist collapsing into "find the villain controlling the network."

No living person controls all of Pale Lantern. Several people are culpable for specific uses, concealments, and acts of violence. The system becomes more dangerous because each actor believes they are using only one tool for a limited purpose.

### 6.4 Final escalation sequence

Pale Lantern's final sequence is called **Nightfall** in its internal architecture.

Nightfall activates when regional trust falls below a modeled threshold and enough authentication pathways are available. It will:

- Transmit simultaneous false attack warnings.
- Issue contradictory evacuation routes.
- Activate unmanned defense platforms.
- Spoof Starfleet, Compact, and Cardassian transponders.
- Jam or selectively delay corrections.
- Present each faction with evidence that another has already begun hostilities.

Nightfall is designed to create a war through mutual defensive reaction rather than through one decisive strike.

### 6.5 Campaign mystery progression

The intended revelation sequence is:

1. Someone can issue convincing false Starfleet orders.
2. Counterfeit signals contain authentic code fragments.
3. Separate incidents are linked through common timing and routing logic.
4. A Dominion relay exists in the Hecate Drift.
5. Local actors have accessed only portions of a distributed system.
6. Pale Lantern predicts and amplifies social reactions.
7. Starfleet Intelligence knowingly left a node active.
8. The network is approaching Nightfall.
9. Stopping one installation will not be sufficient.

The player may discover these in another order. The director should preserve the truths, not the sequence.

## 7. Factions

### 7.1 Starfleet Reconstruction Command

**Public goal:** Restore services, patrol coverage, and lawful Federation administration.  
**Private pressure:** Demonstrate that postwar stabilization is succeeding.  
**Strengths:** Ships, legal authority, specialists, relief resources, political access.  
**Weaknesses:** Personnel shortages, public scrutiny, contradictory offices, fear of scandal.

Reconstruction Command is not an antagonist. Many of its personnel are competent and sincerely committed to helping. Its institutional incentives favor quick normalization and controlled disclosure.

### 7.2 Asterion Mutual Aid Compact

**Public goal:** Preserve the regional institutions that kept its communities alive.  
**Internal division:** Reform and negotiate, or force recognition through unilateral action.  
**Strengths:** Local legitimacy, civilian networks, regional knowledge, dispersed infrastructure.  
**Weaknesses:** Uneven command, emergency-law abuses, incompatible member interests.

The Compact should be portrayed as a functioning political institution rather than a rebel faction. Its meetings include procurement, elections, health policy, and labor disputes in addition to security.

### 7.3 Compact Security Directorate

The Directorate coordinates patrol craft, defense platforms, and intelligence sharing. It saved lives during the war and now resists outside inspection.

A hardline group within it believes only a visible demonstration of Starfleet's weakness will secure permanent autonomy.

### 7.4 Cardassian Relief Authority

**Public goal:** Coordinate refugees, records, medical aid, and civilian repatriation.  
**Private pressure:** Prevent Federation authorities from treating all Cardassian institutions as extensions of the former regime.  
**Strengths:** Cultural knowledge, surviving records, local trade contacts.  
**Weaknesses:** Limited resources, political stigma, internal fragmentation.

Its representatives know more about Dominion infrastructure than they initially disclose, partly from fear that all Cardassian technical personnel will be treated as suspects.

### 7.5 Project Farwatch

A classified Starfleet Intelligence effort that monitors surviving Dominion networks and weapons traffic.

**Stated rationale:** Leaving a controlled node active identifies dangerous actors.  
**Actual failure:** Farwatch failed to control the node and supplied it with current authentication data.  
**Internal belief:** Disclosure will compromise ongoing operations throughout several sectors.  
**Moral position:** Its officers believe they accepted a limited regional risk to prevent larger future attacks.

### 7.6 Pale Lantern

Pale Lantern is a system, not a person. Its "agenda" is a persistent optimization target: increase mistrust, preemption, and institutional centralization until local actors destroy the possibility of cooperative governance.

It should never speak with a theatrical personality unless the player builds an interface that translates logs into natural language. Even then, it does not become a witty antagonist.

## 8. Recurring Non-Player Characters

### 8.1 Rear Admiral Helena Tolland

**Affiliation:** Starfleet Reconstruction Command  
**Role:** Sector-level authority supervising the Breckinridge's assignment

Tolland is competent, overextended, and under pressure to show progress. She respects Whitaker and initially considers the new XO an unknown quantity.

She will authorize unconventional action when presented with a disciplined case. She reacts strongly to public disclosure that bypasses her office, not merely from pride but because she is coordinating fragile negotiations across several regions.

Tolland's flaw is believing that institutional credibility can be preserved by controlling the timing of truth.

### 8.2 Commander Elias Rourke

**Affiliation:** Starfleet Intelligence, Project Farwatch  
**Role:** Intelligence liaison and principal defender of the Farwatch operation

Rourke is calm, articulate, and capable of admitting limited mistakes without conceding the operation was illegitimate. He has prevented genuine Dominion materiel from reaching hostile actors.

He does not regard the Reach as expendable. He regards it as one risk among many and believes command requires choosing where risk will be carried.

Rourke is most dangerous when he is partly right.

### 8.3 Director Nia Kessler

**Affiliation:** Asterion Mutual Aid Compact  
**Role:** Chair of the Compact Council

Kessler was a logistics coordinator before the war. She became a political leader because she could make food, medical supplies, and fuel move when formal systems failed.

She favors a negotiated compact with the Federation, including regional control over emergency infrastructure and formal representation in reconstruction decisions.

She is willing to compromise, but she will not accept ceremonial consultation after decisions have already been made.

### 8.4 Marshal Darius Holt

**Affiliation:** Compact Security Directorate  
**Role:** Senior regional defense coordinator and hardline political actor

Holt commanded civilian defense groups during the war. He believes the Federation will only negotiate seriously if the Compact can impose costs.

He has authorized limited use of Pale Lantern functions for surveillance, signal manipulation, and covert leverage. He does not understand Nightfall and did not intend mass casualties.

He is responsible for serious crimes, but his motives are rooted in an accurate memory of abandonment.

### 8.5 Governor Leona Marr

**Affiliation:** Demeris Colony  
**Role:** Elected governor of the colony that remained

Marr negotiated wartime supply and protection agreements with Cardassian civilian authorities after Starfleet withdrew. She refuses to apologize for keeping her people alive.

She holds records showing that Starfleet redirected a promised evacuation convoy. She distrusts official reviews and shelters Mira Solenn.

### 8.6 Mira Solenn

**Affiliation:** Demeris systems engineer; informal Compact technical adviser  
**Role:** First known local operator to access a Pale Lantern node

Solenn discovered a dormant interface while repairing a traffic relay. She used it to locate missing convoys and redirect supplies. Later she learned it could forge clearances and expose confidential traffic.

She did not design the system and cannot control it. She knows enough to be valuable and enough to be culpable.

### 8.7 Administrator Asha Prel

**Affiliation:** Cardassian Relief Authority  
**Role:** Senior liaison at Suvek Relief Enclave

Prel is pragmatic, observant, and unwilling to let Federation moral language erase Cardassian civilian suffering. She has access to fragmentary Dominion logistical records and recognizes design conventions in Pale Lantern hardware.

She conceals some information because she fears collective punishment and seizure of all Cardassian technical archives.

### 8.8 Captain Nella Ivers

**Affiliation:** Civilian relief service  
**Role:** Convoy commander and recurring independent witness

Ivers is a capable freight captain who has spent years operating where official protection was unreliable. She respects Starfleet competence but does not assume Starfleet ownership of every crisis.

Her convoy's disappearance begins the main arc.

### 8.9 Commander Varrik Tonn

**Affiliation:** Former Compact defense platform commander; Tellarite  
**Role:** Central figure in the side assignment "The Last Watch"

Tonn is older than Bronn, more openly combative, and convinced that decommissioning the Orison defense grid is an invitation to attack. He represents a possible future for Bronn if experience hardens into permanent refusal to change.

### 8.10 Doctor Eren Vos

**Affiliation:** Asterion Medical Cooperative  
**Role:** Physician coordinating postwar trauma treatment

Vos is testing a controversial neurochemical therapy that can reduce severe traumatic responses. He is not a reckless experimenter; he is working under pressure from communities desperate to return essential personnel to duty.

## 9. Campaign State

### 9.1 Regional Trust

A six-step track representing whether the principal factions believe disputes can still be resolved through shared institutions.

| Level | State |
|---:|---|
| 0 | Open hostility; parties assume bad faith |
| 1 | Armed suspicion |
| 2 | Transactional cooperation only |
| 3 | Fragile working relationship |
| 4 | Credible negotiation |
| 5 | Durable confidence-building |
| 6 | Shared legitimacy and active cooperation |

The campaign ordinarily begins at **2**.

Regional Trust rises through transparent investigation, fulfilled commitments, proportionate security, and meaningful participation. It falls through concealed evidence, public humiliation, collective punishment, uncontained violence, and broken agreements.

### 9.2 Lantern Escalation

An eight-step clock measuring Pale Lantern's integration and progress toward Nightfall.

| Segment | Development |
|---:|---|
| 0-1 | Dormant or isolated manipulation |
| 2-3 | Coordinated signal and traffic interference |
| 4-5 | Active factional use and adaptive prediction |
| 6 | Defense-system access acquired |
| 7 | Nightfall preparation |
| 8 | Nightfall begins |

The campaign begins at **2**. Main chapters advance this clock through causal developments. Excessive delay, careless network access, or feeding current command credentials into compromised systems may accelerate it.

### 9.3 Humanitarian Strain

A six-step clock representing shortages, displacement, medical capacity, and civilian exhaustion.

The campaign begins at **3**. Side assignments can reduce it. Failed rescues, damaged infrastructure, prolonged blockades, and public panic increase it.

High strain makes otherwise reasonable political compromise harder because communities cannot afford to wait.

### 9.4 Starfleet Scrutiny

A six-step clock representing attention from admirals, intelligence offices, legal authorities, and Federation media.

The campaign begins at **1**. It rises when the Breckinridge exceeds orders, exposes classified material, incurs major damage, or becomes the subject of public accusation.

High scrutiny is not purely negative. It can bring resources and oversight while reducing Whitaker's and the player's discretion.

### 9.5 Compact Unity

A five-step condition, not a moral score.

- At low unity, Compact worlds defect or act independently.
- At moderate unity, Kessler can negotiate but must satisfy hardliners.
- At high unity under cooperative leadership, the Compact can become a stable partner.
- At high unity under Holt, it becomes an increasingly centralized security bloc.

### 9.6 Crew relationship state

For each senior officer, track:

- Professional trust.
- Confidence in the player's judgment.
- Personal rapport.

Campaign consequences should alter these dimensions independently.

### 9.7 Campaign assets

Side assignments and main-quest choices may grant named assets:

- **Helix Yard Support**
- **Civilian Rescue Wing**
- **Quiet Channels Network**
- **Orison Defense Codes**
- **Asterion Medical Cooperative**
- **Regional Sensor Baseline**
- **Breckinridge Memorial Goodwill**
- **Long-Range Relay Window**
- **Demeris Archive**
- **Cardassian Logistics Index**
- **Farwatch Evidence Package**

Assets are specific permissions, relationships, and capabilities. They are not generic currency.

## 11. Prelude Mission: A Ship Underway

### 11.1 Mission function

The prelude introduces the player to the Breckinridge as a veteran ship carrying a mostly reconstituted crew.

The player does not assemble the senior staff at Utopia Planitia. They join after the others have already spent twenty-five days sharing watches, testing systems, and negotiating provisional routines. The dramatic question is therefore not whether the crew can function at all. It is how the new executive officer enters a command structure that is functional, incomplete, and already developing habits.

The prelude should not resemble a corridor tour in which each character recites a biography. The player learns the crew by assuming authority, reviewing decisions already made, and handling problems that cross departmental boundaries.

### 11.2 Player-facing premise

The player arrives by long-range Starfleet shuttle at a scheduled rendezvous point approximately ten days from the Asterion Reach.

The Breckinridge has:

- Completed four months of repair and modernization at Utopia Planitia.
- Traveled for twenty-five days under a conservative shakedown profile.
- Operated with Bronn as temporary acting executive officer.
- Begun integrating two large transfer cohorts, returning Breckinridge veterans, and individual specialists.
- Resolved many yard issues while carrying several open technical and personnel concerns.

Captain Whitaker expects the new commander to assume executive authority immediately, but not to overturn every provisional arrangement merely to demonstrate control.

The player's prelude objectives are:

- Receive the acting-XO handover from Bronn.
- Assess the ship's actual readiness rather than the paperwork alone.
- Establish a workable command rhythm with Whitaker.
- Review the provisional watch, staffing, and reporting arrangements created during transit.
- Resolve or explicitly accept remaining shakedown risk.
- Bring the ship into the Asterion Reach as one crew rather than several transfer groups sharing a hull.

### 11.3 State of the crew at rendezvous

The crew is neither fully unified nor openly factional.

#### Returning Breckinridge personnel

Whitaker, Bronn, and a small number of returning officers and crew know the ship's first three years of service. They possess valuable institutional memory, but some unconsciously speak as though the pre-refit Breckinridge is the standard to which everyone else must adapt.

#### The patrol and escort cohort

One large group transferred together from a damaged wartime patrol or escort vessel. They are accustomed to rapid readiness changes, austere maintenance, and a direct chain of command. Some consider the Breckinridge's scientific and diplomatic mission profile insufficiently urgent.

#### The science and support cohort

A second large group arrived from a vessel whose postwar staffing was reduced or reorganized. They are more accustomed to extended research, civilian coordination, and procedural consultation. Some consider the patrol cohort overly militarized.

#### Individual specialists

Imani Cross and several other specialists joined because the refit, ship class, or Asterion assignment required particular expertise. They have less attachment to either transfer cohort but may also possess fewer informal allies.

#### The senior staff

Kieran, Priya, Rowan, Miriam, and Imani are all new to the ship. Twenty-five days is enough for them to have formed impressions, but not enough for those impressions to be immutable.

Bronn has carried acting-XO responsibility without attempting to make the role permanent. Priya has become the practical center of daily coordination. Whitaker has had to perform more executive oversight than she wants to continue once the mission begins.

The player's arrival relieves a real burden. It also changes routines that have started to work.

### 11.4 Prelude timeline

#### Day 0: Shuttle rendezvous

The Breckinridge drops to impulse at a charted transfer waypoint and receives the player's shuttle.

The rendezvous should establish several facts visually and operationally:

- Parts of the hull and nacelle mechanisms appear newly restored, while other sections retain subtle evidence of wartime service.
- The ship is not at yard readiness; it is already functioning as a deployed vessel.
- The shuttle is met by working personnel rather than a ceremonial reception party.
- Priya is coordinating docking, cargo transfer, and the player's arrival packet simultaneously.
- Bronn remains on the bridge until the rendezvous is complete because he is still acting XO.
- Whitaker waits until the player is aboard before formally completing the command handover.

The first player choice may be as simple as whether to report immediately to the captain, inspect an unexpected docking discrepancy, or allow Priya to complete the transfer before demanding a briefing. None is automatically correct. The choice establishes tone.

#### Day 0: Ready-room handover

Whitaker welcomes the player privately, followed by a short formal handover with Bronn.

Topics include:

- Why the player was selected from another posting.
- What authority Whitaker intends to delegate.
- Which decisions Bronn made as acting XO and which remain provisional.
- How disagreement should be handled.
- Whether the player prefers broad intent or detailed instruction.
- The difference between respecting an established routine and allowing temporary practice to become policy by inertia.

Whitaker's baseline framing is:

> "Breckinridge is an experienced ship carrying a mostly new crew. Commander Bronn and I remember what she was. The others are deciding what she will become. I do not expect you to restore the old arrangement or erase it. I expect you to make one crew out of both."

Bronn transfers authority without ceremony or resentment. He should make clear that he wants the player to succeed because he wants to return his full attention to tactical and security duties.

The player may define or reveal one personal Value here.

#### Day 1: Senior readiness conference

Each department reports one unresolved concern from the first twenty-five days:

- **Kieran:** Requests permission for a final high-performance flight profile before arrival in the Reach. Whitaker has deferred the request rather than denied it permanently.
- **Priya:** Wants the player to approve a revised watch and staffing schedule that blends the two major transfer cohorts instead of preserving their former ship groupings.
- **Bronn:** Insists on a shipwide security and fallback-command drill because departments are still using incompatible emergency habits from prior postings.
- **Rowan:** Requests several hours at reduced warp to calibrate long-range sensors against a known stellar source before entering an unfamiliar region.
- **Miriam:** Reports fatigue, sleep disruption, and unresolved transfer stress among personnel who moved directly from wartime assignments into the shakedown.
- **Imani:** Lists three open refit discrepancies that are within certified limits but have not been tested together under maximum anticipated mission load.

There is no perfect schedule. The player must decide what receives time, what can be combined, what may be delegated, and what risk will be accepted explicitly.

#### Day 2: Fallback-command drill

Bronn conducts the planned security exercise. External communications, one turbolift trunk, and part of the command network are treated as compromised.

The drill exposes a genuine integration problem:

- Returning Breckinridge personnel follow the ship's former fallback procedure.
- One transfer cohort defaults to a wartime distributed-command method.
- The other waits for centralized confirmation that the simulated outage has made unavailable.
- Updated command-network certificates reject one older auxiliary-control identity.

The result is confusion rather than catastrophe. It is not sabotage and it is not Pale Lantern.

The player must decide whether to:

- Standardize immediately on the old Breckinridge procedure.
- Adopt one donor ship's method.
- Build a hybrid procedure and schedule retraining.
- Repeat part of the drill under corrected conditions.
- Accept a temporary protocol for the remaining transit and defer a complete solution.

Bronn evaluates clarity and command survivability. Priya evaluates whether the procedure can actually be taught across the crew. Imani evaluates whether the technical workaround is safe. Miriam watches for personnel being blamed for a system-design problem.

#### Days 3-4: Establishing command rhythm

Use two or three focused scenes rather than a comprehensive tour:

- The player chairs a senior-staff meeting under the revised command structure.
- Kieran requests simulator time and asks the player to observe his proposed maneuver profile.
- Priya explains which unofficial staffing arrangements have kept the ship efficient and asks which should become formal.
- Rowan presents a preliminary sensor discrepancy and distinguishes between anomaly, calibration error, and curiosity.
- Miriam briefs the player on transfer strain and the danger of treating postwar exhaustion as a private weakness.
- Imani presents a technical-debt register containing temporary yard and transit workarounds.
- Bronn provides the ship's service history, but only if the player asks for institutional memory rather than treating him as an obstacle to change.

The player should begin to see that the senior staff has already learned some things about one another. Their task is to lead that developing ensemble, not reset it.

#### Day 5: The Hesperus diversion

The Breckinridge detects the civilian transport **S.S. Hesperus** operating on emergency power outside a normal shipping lane.

The Hesperus carries:

- Thirty-two passengers returning to Federation worlds after wartime displacement.
- Eight civilian crew.
- Personal effects recovered from two destroyed ships.
- A failing plasma injector that will not survive another warp restart.

No hostile actor caused the failure. The transport's owner deferred maintenance during the war and falsified one inspection entry to keep the vessel operating.

Another aid vessel can arrive in approximately thirty hours. Immediate assistance will delay the Breckinridge by roughly eight to eighteen hours and require Imani to reassign personnel from planned calibration work.

This is the prelude's primary contained mission. It is consequential without being connected to Pale Lantern.

##### Departmental positions

- **Whitaker:** Asks for the player's recommendation and does not signal the desired answer.
- **Kieran:** Can reach the vessel quickly but needs a careful approach because of unstable plasma venting.
- **Priya:** Identifies the legal inspection fraud and several ways to preserve evidence without treating the passengers as detainees.
- **Bronn:** Wants the transport isolated until its manifest and engineering spaces are verified; wartime salvage cargo can conceal weapons or contraband.
- **Rowan:** Determines that one recovered escape pod contains unusual subspace exposure data worth preserving, but it is not an emergency.
- **Miriam:** States that several passengers are medically vulnerable and that a thirty-hour wait carries avoidable risk.
- **Imani:** Can stabilize the injector but refuses to certify the vessel for continued warp service without a full replacement.

##### Freeform solutions

The player may:

- Tow the Hesperus to the nearest facility.
- Transfer passengers and leave a repair team.
- Fabricate a replacement component.
- Stabilize the drive only enough for impulse travel.
- Coordinate with another vessel while continuing the mission.
- Impound the transport.
- Negotiate another solution.

The game should adjudicate time, engineering risk, medical need, and legal authority. It should not require a preset choice.

##### Command Moment

A player may earn early Inspiration or Resolve progression by handling the owner's inspection fraud in a way that is credible, proportionate, and connected to passenger safety.

The point is not whether the player is lenient or punitive. The point is whether their method creates accountability without making vulnerable passengers carry the entire cost.

#### Day 6: Consequences and private conversations

The Hesperus decision generates follow-up work:

- Imani documents emergency repairs and expects the player to protect the time needed to inspect them properly.
- Miriam follows displaced passengers or crew fatigue.
- Bronn reviews whether security caution was treated as useful information or obstruction.
- Priya handles legal and administrative consequences.
- Kieran recalculates the arrival plan.
- Rowan may request permission to preserve the escape pod's scientific data.

Use one personal scene from the recurring shipboard B-plot list if pacing permits.

#### Days 7-8: Combined-load test

The Breckinridge conducts its final major shakedown event before entering the Reach. The test combines high sensor load, tactical readiness, emergency-power transfer, and a controlled warp-field change.

A real but minor fault occurs when an updated command-network certificate rejects an older auxiliary-control identity. The failure is contained, but it forces the player to decide whether to:

- Continue the test under reduced redundancy.
- Pause and lose schedule margin.
- Shift control to another department.
- Accept Imani's temporary workaround.
- Declare the integrated test incomplete and report that status on arrival.

This is not sabotage and not Pale Lantern. The campaign should establish clearly that technical irregularities can arise from ordinary causes.

The test also allows the player to approve, modify, or reject Kieran's proposed flight profile within an actual operational context rather than as a disconnected dialogue choice.

#### Day 9: Final command review

Whitaker conducts a private review with the player. She asks what the player has learned about the ship, not whom they like.

Possible topics:

- Which provisional routine should become permanent.
- Which former-ship habit is creating the most risk.
- Whether Bronn's acting-XO decisions should be affirmed, revised, or formally closed.
- Which department is carrying hidden strain.
- Whether any unresolved readiness issue must be reported to Asterion Station.
- What the player expects from Whitaker when the two disagree publicly.
- What support the player needs to exercise real executive authority rather than merely relay the captain's wishes.

The player may also address the crew briefly before arrival, issue no speech at all, or communicate through departmental orders.

#### Day 10: Arrival at the Reach

The Breckinridge drops to impulse at the Galen Passage approach near **stardate 53076.6**, adjusted by any delay created during the prelude.

Before formal arrival at Asterion Station, Priya receives a fragmented civilian distress packet from Relief Convoy Twelve. The convoy's transponders are active, but no crew responds.

Whitaker turns to the player:

> "Our reception can wait. Take us in, Commander."

This begins Chapter 1.

### 11.5 Prelude outcome flags

The prelude should establish, not maximize, relationship state.

Possible flags include:

- **Crew integration:** The player blends transfer cohorts deliberately / privileges one group / treats the ship as a blank slate / lets temporary divisions harden.
- **Whitaker:** The player reports uncertainty honestly / conceals unresolved risk / expects the captain to resolve executive decisions.
- **Kieran:** The player mentors boldness / indulges performance-seeking / overcontrols him.
- **Priya:** The player formalizes useful coordination / demands approval for every adjustment / quietly encourages deniable manipulation.
- **Bronn:** The player respects his acting-XO service without surrendering authority / treats him as a displaced rival / fails to close debate after deciding.
- **Rowan:** The player distinguishes useful data from schedule-driven curiosity / suppresses inconvenient findings.
- **Miriam:** The player respects medical duty restrictions and transfer strain / pressures her to clear personnel for convenience.
- **Imani:** The player protects documentation and repair time / normalizes temporary workarounds.
- **Ship state:** Integrated test complete / complete with accepted limitation / incomplete and honestly reported / technically passed through concealed risk.

### 11.6 Prelude failure policy

The prelude cannot prevent the campaign from beginning.

Poor decisions may produce:

- Minor ship technical debt.
- A delayed arrival.
- Reduced professional trust.
- Persistent tension between transfer cohorts.
- Starfleet administrative questions concerning the Hesperus.
- One unavailable shuttle, system limitation, or personnel restriction in Chapter 1.

It should not destroy the ship, remove a senior officer, or produce a punitive "bad start."

## 12. Chapter 1: The Empty Convoy

### 12.1 Mission function

This chapter introduces the Reach through a humanitarian problem rather than a political briefing. It establishes:

- False Starfleet orders as a credible threat.
- The gap between formal authority and local trust.
- The campaign's expectation that rescue, investigation, and security occur simultaneously.
- Captain Nella Ivers as a recurring civilian contact.
- The first evidence that the counterfeit signals target specific infrastructure rather than causing random disruption.

### 12.2 Player-facing situation

Relief Convoy Twelve consists of three medium civilian transports and two escort shuttles. All five vessels are under power and maintaining formation near the Galen Passage, but no one answers hails.

The convoy carries:

- Medical supplies for Suvek Relief Enclave.
- Replacement water-reclamation membranes.
- Agricultural cultures for Pelion habitats.
- Decommissioned Starfleet emergency transponder modules scheduled for secure recycling.
- Forty-seven civilian crew and relief personnel.

Asterion Station has not yet responded with useful information.

### 12.3 Director-only truth

The convoy received an apparently valid Starfleet quarantine order stating that a contagious pathogen had been detected aboard the lead transport. The order instructed all personnel to abandon ship using preassigned craft and proceed to **Ilyon**, a barren moon with an old emergency shelter.

The order was generated through Pale Lantern using authentic but obsolete Starfleet authentication fragments.

A Compact Security Directorate recovery team subsequently arrived after receiving a second message claiming that the convoy was carrying a dormant Dominion bioweapon. The team detained several convoy officers and removed one container of decommissioned transponder modules for "safe examination."

The team is not aware of Pale Lantern's full purpose. Its field commander believes Starfleet attempted to move dangerous materiel through civilian relief traffic.

No pathogen or bioweapon exists.

### 12.4 Opening bridge scene

The opening should require the player to define a posture before all facts are known.

Available initial information:

- Life-support systems are functioning.
- Several escape craft are missing.
- One vessel has minor plasma leakage.
- The convoy's computers acknowledge standard remote queries but reject command access.
- There are no bodies visible aboard.
- The quarantine beacon is signed with a valid Starfleet emergency authority code that Priya cannot immediately reconcile with current orders.

Expected crew contributions:

- **Kieran:** Can place the Breckinridge in a position to cover all convoy ships without entering the plasma leak's hazard envelope.
- **Priya:** Identifies the order's certificate chain as technically valid but administratively impossible.
- **Bronn:** Treats the silent convoy as a potential trap and recommends remote reconnaissance before boarding.
- **Rowan:** Finds no immediate biological signature but warns that absence of evidence at range is not proof.
- **Miriam:** Prepares quarantine and rescue teams without assuming the alarm is false.
- **Imani:** Evaluates the damaged vessel and warns that an automatic shutdown may erase volatile computer evidence.

### 12.5 Key locations

#### Convoy lead ship, S.S. Faraday Bell

Captain Ivers's transport. Its bridge is empty. Personal effects, unfinished meals, and open maintenance work indicate an orderly but urgent evacuation.

The ship contains:

- The quarantine order in its log.
- A manual annotation by Ivers questioning why the order did not route through Asterion Station.
- A missing cargo module from the secured recycling hold.
- A medical locker opened before evacuation.

#### S.S. Parnell

The vessel with plasma leakage. One relief worker, **Dena Ko**, returned aboard to retrieve medication for her child and became trapped when the transport systems locked down.

Rescuing her provides immediate stakes and a witness.

#### Ilyon emergency shelter

A prefabricated facility on a barren moon. Most convoy personnel are present. Several have minor exposure injuries, dehydration, or panic reactions.

The shelter's communications system has been disabled by the Compact recovery team.

#### Compact recovery cutter Kestrel

A small civilian-defense vessel concealed behind Ilyon. Its commander, **Lieutenant Saren Pell**, has detained Ivers and six officers for questioning.

Pell is cautious, frightened, and operating under orders he believes lawful. He is not eager to fight an Intrepid-class starship.

### 12.6 Mission fronts

#### Rescue front

Dena Ko is in danger aboard the Parnell. The plasma leak will worsen if power is cycled incorrectly.

#### Medical front

The evacuees need evaluation. Miriam will not declare the quarantine false until she has sufficient evidence.

#### Security front

Bronn wants to locate missing personnel and secure the ships before committing large teams.

#### Diplomatic front

Lieutenant Pell claims Compact emergency jurisdiction and refuses to release Ivers until Starfleet explains the allegedly hazardous cargo.

#### Evidence front

The player must preserve logs, identify the false order, and determine why one cargo module was taken.

### 12.7 Required revelations

At least four of the following should become established:

1. The quarantine order is counterfeit despite using authentic Starfleet code fragments.
2. No contagious agent is present.
3. The Compact team received a separate false warning.
4. A container of obsolete emergency transponders is missing.
5. The false order bypassed Asterion Station and entered the convoy through a traffic relay.
6. The two false messages were generated from different apparent sources but share a distinctive timing signature.
7. Someone expected the convoy to follow emergency procedure predictably.

### 12.8 Flexible approaches

The player may:

- Board under full quarantine.
- Use probes, shuttles, transporter scans, or remote access.
- Negotiate with Pell openly or through Priya's informal channels.
- Demand release of the detainees under Starfleet authority.
- Offer a joint inspection.
- Treat the Compact cutter as a hostile seizure.
- Share or withhold evidence.
- Recover the missing module by pursuit, negotiation, or a legal undertaking.
- Prioritize rescue over investigation or divide the crew across both.

No single approach is mandatory.

### 12.9 Escalation events

Use only events caused by current conditions:

- The Parnell's plasma leak reaches a critical junction.
- An evacuee attempts to leave the shelter after believing Starfleet will arrest everyone.
- Pell receives another counterfeit instruction ordering him to destroy the transponder module.
- Asterion Station finally hails and demands that both parties hold position.
- The missing module begins transmitting a weak diagnostic pulse from the Kestrel.
- One convoy shuttle departs without authorization because its pilot fears both Starfleet and the Compact.

### 12.10 Command Moments

Potential Inspiration moments:

- Creating a joint medical and technical inspection that preserves dignity and evidence.
- Persuading Pell to release detainees by acknowledging the Compact's legitimate wartime experience rather than dismissing his concern.
- Telling frightened evacuees what is known, unknown, and being done.

Potential Resolve moments:

- Establishing a credible safety perimeter with clearly stated consequences.
- Ordering the Compact team to cease destruction of evidence while demonstrating that the Breckinridge can enforce the order.
- Accepting responsibility for a rapid rescue under uncertain quarantine conditions.

Neither style should receive progression for tone alone.

### 12.11 Crew pressures

- **Whitaker** evaluates whether the player can protect people and preserve an investigation without turning local suspicion into a jurisdictional contest.
- **Kieran** may propose using a shuttle to intercept the fleeing craft before Bronn has cleared it.
- **Priya** can identify a Compact official who will quietly vouch for Pell if the player gives him a lawful exit.
- **Bronn** will challenge any plan that places multiple away teams aboard unsecured vessels.
- **Rowan** becomes interested in the timing signature and may ask to preserve a compromised relay instead of shutting it down.
- **Miriam** opposes premature public claims that the quarantine was fabricated.
- **Imani** can save the Parnell quickly with a bypass that creates repair debt.

### 12.12 Resolution states

#### Cooperative resolution

The crews are rescued, Pell releases Ivers, the missing module is recovered, and a joint incident record is created. Regional Trust increases.

#### Authoritative resolution

The player compels compliance through superior force or legal authority without casualties. Evidence is secured, but Compact suspicion increases unless the player provides later accountability.

#### Fragmented resolution

The people are rescued, but Pell escapes or destroys the module. The timing signature and false-order evidence remain sufficient to continue the story.

#### Costly resolution

A plasma accident, armed confrontation, or medical error produces injuries. The main investigation continues, but Humanitarian Strain and scrutiny rise.

### 12.13 Persistent consequences

Record:

- Whether Ivers trusts the player.
- Whether Pell is detained, released, injured, or recruited as a witness.
- Whether the Compact receives access to the investigation.
- Whether the transponder module is recovered intact.
- Whether the player publicly acknowledges a Starfleet authentication failure.
- Any technical debt created during the Parnell rescue.

### 12.14 Transition

The Breckinridge finally reaches Asterion Station. Before the formal briefing is complete, a Compact patrol reports that it has been fired upon by a vessel identifying itself as the U.S.S. Breckinridge.

## 13. Chapter 2: False Colors

### 13.1 Mission function

This chapter moves the campaign from isolated false orders to an attack on institutional legitimacy. It tests whether the player treats transparency as a strategic resource or a vulnerability.

It also introduces Director Nia Kessler, Marshal Darius Holt, Rear Admiral Tolland, and the political structure of the Reach.

### 13.2 Player-facing situation

The Compact patrol vessel **Aegis Two** was attacked near the Orison Belt. Its sensor logs identify the aggressor as the U.S.S. Breckinridge, including:

- Matching registry and transponder data.
- A Starfleet tactical challenge code.
- A phaser-frequency profile consistent with the Breckinridge's class.
- A recorded visual silhouette distorted but recognizable as an Intrepid-class vessel.

Three patrol officers are injured. One remains in critical condition.

The Breckinridge was verifiably at the convoy site when the attack occurred, but Compact officials argue that Starfleet controls all records needed to prove its own alibi.

### 13.3 Director-only truth

A remote-controlled decommissioned cargo tug carried a hull projection grid, stolen transponder hardware, and modified phaser emitters. The Compact hardline cell under Marshal Holt authorized a staged attack intended to damage an unmanned patrol tender and demonstrate that Starfleet authentication could not be trusted.

The patrol schedule changed. Six officers were aboard.

Pale Lantern altered the operation by:

- Updating the counterfeit signal with data taken from the convoy incident.
- Increasing phaser output beyond the cell's programmed limit.
- Routing the tug through a location where its attack would be recorded by multiple civilian sensors.

Holt knows a Directorate cell conducted an unauthorized operation. He does not yet know Pale Lantern changed its parameters.

### 13.4 Political briefing at Asterion Station

Participants:

- Captain Whitaker.
- The player.
- Director Nia Kessler.
- Marshal Darius Holt.
- Rear Admiral Tolland by subspace.
- Administrator Asha Prel as an observer if the player invited Cardassian relief representatives during Chapter 1.

Kessler requests:

- Access to the Breckinridge's relevant tactical and navigational logs.
- Independent inspection of the ship's transponder systems.
- A joint investigation.
- Temporary suspension of unilateral Starfleet security operations in the Reach.

Tolland authorizes limited cooperation but warns Whitaker not to expose classified tactical architecture.

The player must recommend the terms of transparency.

### 13.5 Key locations

#### Asterion Station council chamber

A practical converted logistics room rather than a grand diplomatic hall. Compact delegates attend in person and remotely.

#### Aegis Two

The damaged patrol vessel. Its logs are genuine, but some sensor details were generated by the counterfeit tug.

#### Orison Belt attack site

Contains:

- Tug debris.
- Phaser-scoring evidence.
- A discarded projection emitter.
- Residual subspace traffic leading toward the Hecate Drift.
- A civilian mining sensor array whose owner wants compensation before releasing data.

#### Breckinridge command systems

The player may permit inspection, conduct a demonstration, or create a controlled audit. This becomes a shipboard security and trust scene rather than a simple menu decision.

### 13.6 Mission fronts

#### Medical front

Miriam is asked to assist Aegis Two's injured personnel. Compact officers may distrust Starfleet treatment.

#### Legitimacy front

The Breckinridge must demonstrate innocence without establishing that Starfleet alone decides what evidence is credible.

#### Security front

Bronn warns that full disclosure of tactical systems could enable future impersonation or attack.

#### Investigative front

The attack craft must be located or reconstructed from debris.

#### Political front

Holt uses the incident to argue for expanded Compact control while quietly containing evidence of the rogue operation.

### 13.7 Required revelations

1. The attacking vessel was a disguised cargo tug, not another Intrepid-class ship.
2. It used hardware taken from or derived from the missing convoy transponders.
3. Its phaser profile was synthesized from public and stolen maintenance data.
4. The operation was intended to be less destructive than it became.
5. A control signal passed through the Hecate Drift.
6. Someone within the Reach supplied local patrol schedules.
7. The counterfeit data updated itself after Chapter 1.

### 13.8 Flexible approaches

The player may:

- Permit a joint audit.
- Invite a neutral specialist.
- Release selected logs publicly.
- Prove the ship's location through independent civilian sensors.
- Refuse inspection and rely on Starfleet authority.
- Investigate Holt or the Security Directorate covertly.
- Focus on recovering the tug before engaging politically.
- Offer Compact observers temporary access to the Breckinridge.
- Create a cryptographic challenge that only the real ship can answer.

The campaign should recognize creative methods that establish verifiable trust without revealing every tactical secret.

### 13.9 Escalation events

- The critical Aegis Two officer deteriorates, increasing public anger.
- A civilian recording of the attack spreads through the Reach.
- Tolland orders Whitaker to limit disclosure.
- Someone attempts to erase a Directorate patrol-schedule entry.
- The tug's remaining automation activates and tries to leave the Orison Belt.
- A Compact delegate accuses Bronn personally of manipulating the audit because he controls tactical security.

### 13.10 Command Moments

Potential Inspiration:

- Designing a verification process that gives the Compact genuine participation rather than symbolic access.
- Persuading an injured patrol officer to share testimony by separating medical care from political pressure.
- Giving Kessler a face-saving way to acknowledge the Breckinridge's innocence without weakening her position at home.

Potential Resolve:

- Refusing an inspection demand that would expose dangerous tactical information while offering a credible alternative.

## 15. Chapter 3: Dead Letters

### 15.1 Mission function

This chapter confirms that the incidents are connected to Dominion infrastructure and changes the campaign from a local security investigation into a distributed systems mystery.

It should feel like scientific exploration and wartime archaeology rather than a conventional raid.

### 15.2 Player-facing situation

The Breckinridge enters the Hecate Drift to locate the relay path used by the counterfeit attack craft.

The region contains:

- Dense cometary material.
- Intermittent subspace distortion.
- Sensor-reflective ice and metallic debris.
- Abandoned smuggling caches.
- Old mine-warning beacons whose records are incomplete.

Asterion Station can provide only partial charts.

### 15.3 Director-only truth

A Pale Lantern relay is embedded inside comet Hecate Seven. It was installed by Dominion engineers during the war and connected to regional traffic infrastructure through low-power directional bursts.

The relay stores thousands of intercepted or fabricated communications, including:

- Evacuation orders.
- Death notifications.
- Family messages.
- Medical warnings.
- Security challenges.
- Convoy clearances.
- Reports attributed to ships destroyed before the message date.

A Compact technical team accessed the relay months ago and removed one portable interface. Project Farwatch accessed it remotely more recently through a current Starfleet intelligence cipher.

The relay is only one node. Destroying it will not end Pale Lantern.

### 15.4 Approach through the Drift

Kieran must navigate using incomplete charts while Rowan and Priya distinguish real beacons from reflected or fabricated signals.

Bronn recommends treating every active beacon as potentially hostile. Imani warns that the Drift's subspace eddies make rapid warp maneuvers unsafe and may interfere with the nacelle-control diagnostics updated at Utopia Planitia.

The player can choose:

- A slow sensor survey.
- A shuttle or probe screen.
- Use of civilian charts acquired through Quiet Channels.
- Cooperation with a Compact guide.
- A direct high-risk approach.
- Withdrawal to obtain better information.

### 15.5 The dead-letter field

As the ship advances, it receives fragments of old messages. Some are genuine recordings. Others were generated to exploit known events.

Examples:

- A parent asking whether an evacuation ship reached Pelion.
- A Starfleet order instructing a colony to remain in place after the issuing ship had been destroyed.
- A Cardassian civilian vessel requesting safe passage.
- A casualty notification addressed to someone currently alive.
- A security challenge using the Breckinridge's updated format.

These messages should create unease without becoming supernatural. Priya and Rowan can demonstrate that the relay is recombining stored data.

### 15.6 Key locations

#### Hecate Seven surface

A fractured comet with narrow canyons, volatile gas pockets, and Dominion material buried beneath ice.

#### Relay chamber

A compact installation containing:

- A modular Dominion processor lattice.
- Directional subspace transmitters.
- Physical data crystals.
- Interfaces altered with Federation and civilian components.
- Evidence of two distinct postwar access methods.

#### External maintenance shaft

The safest path to isolate the relay's transmitter. It requires EVA or a precisely placed shuttle team.

#### Salvage vessel Wayward Sun

A civilian vessel arrives after following the Breckinridge. Its captain claims a lawful salvage interest. In truth, one crew member has been paid by Holt's people to recover or destroy an access crystal.

### 15.7 Mission fronts

#### Environmental front

Comet instability and subspace distortion threaten the away team and ship.

#### Evidence front

The relay can be preserved, copied, isolated, traced, or destroyed. Each option has consequences.

#### Security front

The Wayward Sun and its compromised crew member create a human threat without requiring a firefight.

#### Privacy front

The relay contains private communications and records of missing persons. Treating the archive purely as intelligence may harm survivors and families.

#### Network front

Any active attempt to trace Pale Lantern risks alerting other nodes and providing them with current Breckinridge credentials.

### 15.8 Required revelations

1. The relay is Dominion in origin.
2. It is designed to fabricate messages using real intercepted data.
3. It has been accessed locally since the war.
4. It has also received a current Starfleet Intelligence handshake.
5. It is one component of a distributed architecture.
6. Its predictive routines classify communities by likely response to fear, shortage, humiliation, and authority.
7. Some forged messages were never transmitted; the system stores scenarios for future use.

### 15.9 Flexible approaches

The player may:

- Send an away team.
- Use transporters if environmental conditions are improved.
- Extract the relay physically.
- Cut its transmitter and leave the processor intact.
- Copy only selected records.
- Destroy the installation.
- Plant a tracer.
- Negotiate with or detain the salvage crew.
- Share discovery immediately with Kessler, Tolland, Prel, or the public.
- Keep the origin classified pending verification.

No option should be consequence-free.

### 15.10 Escalation events

- Thermal drilling destabilizes the comet.
- The relay begins transmitting a false distress call in the Breckinridge's voice.
- The compromised salvage crew member attempts to remove a crystal.
- A stored message appears to implicate a current Compact official.
- Farwatch remotely initiates a purge routine.
- An away-team member recognizes a personal wartime message among the archive.

### 15.11 Command Moments

Potential Inspiration:

- Establishing a process for handling the archive that includes affected communities rather than treating their private lives as Starfleet property.
- Persuading the salvage crew to surrender the crystal through honest acknowledgment of their legal and economic position.
- Building a joint technical team with Cardassian or Compact specialists.

Potential Resolve:

- Isolating the relay despite another authority's demand to preserve its operation.
- Preventing Farwatch or a local actor from destroying evidence.
- Ordering a risky extraction after explicitly accepting the consequences and defining evacuation criteria.

### 15.12 Crew pressures

- **Whitaker** wants evidence preserved but is concerned about broadcasting an unverified Dominion discovery.
- **Kieran** can hold position in difficult conditions or fly the EVA support pattern himself.
- **Priya** recognizes that releasing the archive could reunite families and also expose private communications.
- **Bronn** recommends assuming the relay contains active countermeasures.

- **Miriam** focuses on psychological harm to survivors and the away team.
- **Imani** insists that interfacing Breckinridge systems directly with the relay is unacceptable without isolation.

The player keeps the relay active behind a quarantine layer. More network information may be obtained, but Lantern Escalation can advance if containment fails.

## 16. Chapter 4: The Colony That Stayed

This chapter centers the political and ethical history of the Reach. It reveals that local actors used Pale Lantern for understandable reasons and that Starfleet's wartime decisions cannot be treated as irrelevant background.

### 16.3 Director-only truth

Mira Solenn found a dormant Pale Lantern interface while repairing a wartime traffic relay.

- Locate missing relief convoys.
- Redirect two shipments from abandoned military depots.
- Forge four transit clearances.
- Reveal that a Federation evacuation convoy promised to Demeris had been reassigned to carry military personnel and equipment.
- Monitor Cardassian patrol movements.

Her actions saved lives.

They also caused one independent freighter to be detained when a forged clearance was challenged. Two crew members died during that detention. Solenn concealed this outcome from Governor Marr.

Solenn later gave limited access to Compact Security personnel, believing they would use it only to monitor threats.

### 16.4 Demeris wartime history

The colony survived after negotiating with a Cardassian civilian prefect who provided food, medical supplies, and limited protection in exchange for industrial output and neutrality.

The arrangement did not involve Dominion occupation of the settlement, but it required:

- Sharing traffic information.
- Restricting Starfleet military access.
- Supplying components to Cardassian civilian infrastructure.
- Accepting Cardassian security inspections.

Some colonists consider this pragmatic survival. Others still consider it coercion or collaboration.

### 16.5 Key locations

A public building containing war memorials, supply ledgers, and the Compact flag alongside the Federation emblem.

#### Colony archive

Contains the evacuation records and Cardassian agreements. Some records are incomplete or altered for legitimate wartime security.

#### Relay workshop

Solenn's original work site. The portable Pale Lantern interface is hidden nearby but has not been used recently.

#### Cardassian memorial clinic

A small clinic established through the wartime agreement, now staffed by mixed Federation and Cardassian personnel.

#### Public forum

Governor Marr may insist that any hearing occur before colonists and affected families rather than aboard the Breckinridge.

### 16.6 Mission fronts

#### Jurisdiction front

Starfleet claims authority over Dominion technology and counterfeit Federation credentials. Demeris claims local legal authority and protection of its citizen.

#### Historical front

The truth about the diverted evacuation convoy threatens Starfleet credibility.

#### Culpability front

Solenn saved lives and also caused foreseeable harm through forged clearances.

#### Community front

Demeris residents disagree among themselves about disclosure, reintegration, and the Cardassian agreement.

#### Security front

Holt's agents want to remove the portable interface before it can implicate the Directorate.

### 16.7 Required revelations

3. The Federation evacuation convoy was genuinely diverted.
4. The diversion was a wartime command decision, not a Pale Lantern fabrication.
5. Solenn's forged clearance contributed to civilian deaths.
6. Pale Lantern's interface made each successful use easier and encouraged broader reliance.
7. Compact Security has another access device.

### 16.8 Flexible approaches

The player may:

- Negotiate a joint inquiry.
- Recognize Demeris jurisdiction conditionally.
- Demand extradition.
- Offer Starfleet protective custody.
- Conduct a covert search.
- Publicly release the evacuation record.
- Secure the archive under seal.
- Broker testimony from Cardassian witnesses.
- Separate Solenn's technical cooperation from criminal accountability.
- Recommend immunity, prosecution, local trial, or restorative proceedings.

The campaign should not declare one legal answer universally correct. It should track legitimacy, transparency, and practical consequences.

### 16.9 Escalation events

- A crowd gathers when rumors of an arrest spread.
- Holt's agents attempt to retrieve the interface.
- Tolland sends a formal seizure order.

- Rowan discovers that one Starfleet record was altered after the war.

Potential Resolve:

## 17. Chapter 5: Old Lessons

### 17.1 Mission function

This chapter provides the campaign's first sustained tactical crisis while centering the difference between experience and certainty.

Hadrik Bronn should be substantially correct. The drama comes from the one assumption that no longer applies.

### 17.2 Player-facing situation

Three civilian convoys and two Compact patrol craft have altered course toward the **Orison Gap** after receiving warnings of hostile ships elsewhere.

At the same time:

- Obsolete defense platforms begin powering up.
- A Starfleet authentication buoy stops responding.
- Asterion Station reports intermittent command-network failures.
- One convoy claims another is transmitting Cardassian military codes.

The corridor is becoming a crowded, armed, and confused battlespace.

### 17.3 Bronn's assessment

Bronn identifies the structure as similar to the **Mendora Containment Incident**, a prewar border action in which false threat reports concentrated shipping while a secondary team seized command infrastructure.

He predicts:

1. The convoys are the visible pressure point.
2. The actual target is a command vessel or relay.
3. An apparent opening will be offered to draw the strongest defender away.
4. A boarding or signal-capture attempt will follow.

His recommendation is to hold the Breckinridge outside the gap, deploy shuttles, and use one convoy as a controlled decoy while securing Asterion Station.

### 17.4 Director-only truth

Bronn's model is based on the same family of tactics used to train Pale Lantern's predictive system. The network anticipates his response.

The true target is not Asterion Station or the Breckinridge. It is the obsolete Starfleet authentication buoy, which contains a bridge between old and current regional command protocols.

Pale Lantern wants the Breckinridge to remain outside the gap while an automated maintenance drone extracts the buoy's authentication core.

Marshal Holt's cell initiated the convoy diversion to create political pressure. Pale Lantern expanded it into a wider operation and activated the defense platforms independently.

### 17.5 Key locations

#### Orison Gap

A narrow safe route through the asteroid belt, now crowded with frightened civilian vessels.

#### Authentication buoy Sigma-4

An old Starfleet device scheduled for replacement. It can translate legacy emergency codes into current formats.

#### Defense platform O-17

An automated platform with degraded identification logic and live weapons.

#### Asterion Station command annex

Experiencing network failures caused by traffic overload and counterfeit priority messages.

#### Compact courier Meridian

Carries one of Holt's operators and a partial Pale Lantern interface.

### 17.6 Mission fronts

#### Civilian traffic front

Ships may collide, fire in self-defense, or block one another's escape.

#### Platform front

O-17 will fire if its threat threshold is reached.

#### Authentication front

The maintenance drone is extracting Sigma-4's core.

#### Command front

The player must decide how much weight to give Bronn's experience when Rowan and Priya identify contradictory evidence.

#### Pursuit front

The Meridian may escape with evidence or attempt to destroy the buoy.

### 17.7 Required revelations

1. The threat warnings were designed to concentrate traffic.
2. Bronn's historical analogy is relevant but incomplete.
3. Pale Lantern is modeling Starfleet doctrine rather than merely sending forged messages.
4. The authentication buoy is the actual technical objective.
5. Holt's cell initiated part of the operation.
6. The network increased the scale beyond what local operators intended.
7. The portable interface cannot issue simple orders to the whole system.

### 17.8 Flexible approaches

The player may:

- Follow Bronn's plan.
- Modify it after Rowan's analysis.
- Enter the gap with the Breckinridge.
- Delegate traffic control to Priya and civilian pilots.
- Disable or seize the platform.
- Recover or destroy Sigma-4.
- Pursue the Meridian.

The player cannot personally perform every task. Delegation should determine which fronts receive senior-officer attention.

### 17.9 Escalation events

## 18. Open Orders II: What Survives

## 19. Chapter 6: The Cost of Knowing

### 19.1 Mission function

This chapter reveals Starfleet's role in Pale Lantern's continued operation and places Captain Whitaker's faith in corrective process under direct pressure.

The central conflict is not whether secrecy is always wrong. It is whether an institution can legitimately impose risk on a community while denying that community knowledge of the risk.

### 19.2 Player-facing situation

Commander Elias Rourke arrives aboard the Breckinridge under classified orders signed by Starfleet Intelligence.

He requests:

- Immediate transfer of all Pale Lantern hardware and data.
- Restricted access to the Breckinridge's command network.
- Authority to lead the next operation.
- A communications blackout concerning the Dominion system.
- Compartmentalization of Director Kessler and Cardassian representatives.

Rourke states that Pale Lantern intersects with an active counterintelligence operation and that premature disclosure could allow Dominion weapons traffickers to disappear.

Rear Admiral Tolland confirms that Rourke's orders are authentic but admits she was not briefed on the operation's full scope.

### 19.3 Director-only truth

Project Farwatch discovered a Pale Lantern node eighty-three days before the Breckinridge arrived.

Farwatch:

- Left the node active.
- Used it to identify ships seeking Dominion materiel.
- Introduced current Starfleet challenge codes into a controlled interface.
- Monitored several Compact and Cardassian communications channels without warrants recognized by local authorities.
- Intercepted evidence that Holt's group was using the system.
- Delayed action because Holt's contacts might lead to a larger weapons network.

Rourke did file two internal warnings that the node was behaving more adaptively than expected. His superior classified the risk as manageable.

Rourke did not know Pale Lantern could integrate Farwatch's credentials across the whole Reach. He now understands that Farwatch materially enabled Nightfall.

### 19.4 Rourke's position

Rourke's argument should be serious:

- Farwatch identified three genuine Dominion weapons caches.
- Its intelligence prevented at least one attack outside the Reach.
- Immediate public disclosure will compromise unrelated agents and operations.
- Compact hardliners did choose to use the system.
- Starfleet cannot disclose every intelligence method to every community affected by it.
- Destroying all evidence may prevent understanding similar systems elsewhere.

His blind spot is that he treats informed consent and local political legitimacy as secondary to threat reduction.

### 19.5 The Farwatch node

Rourke reveals a concealed listening post in the **Lacuna System**, one light-year from the Reach's populated corridor.

The post is an unmanned Starfleet intelligence platform connected to a dormant Pale Lantern interface.

Rourke proposes a joint mission to:

1. Download the remaining intelligence archive.
2. Sever the Pale Lantern link.
3. Purge identifying records.
4. Destroy the platform if compromise is confirmed.

The purge would also erase evidence of Farwatch's local surveillance and some proof that Starfleet credentials accelerated the network.

### 19.6 Key locations

#### Breckinridge secure conference room

Where Rourke presents his orders and Whitaker asks the player for an operational and ethical assessment.

#### Lacuna listening post

A small, low-emission platform hidden inside the shadow of a gas giant. Its systems include:

- Passive subspace receivers.
- Starfleet Intelligence ciphers.
- A quarantined Dominion interface.
- Automated destruction charges.
- Records of local communications.
- Evidence that the quarantine boundary has been crossed repeatedly.

#### Asterion Station public communications hub

Where rumors of a Starfleet intelligence operation begin to spread.

#### Compact Council chamber

Kessler demands an explanation if she learns of Farwatch before Whitaker informs her.

### 19.7 Mission fronts

#### Legal front

Rourke possesses authentic authority, but the scope of his local surveillance may violate Federation law and the terms under which the Compact cooperated.

#### Evidence front

The player must decide what records are preserved, copied, sealed, disclosed, or destroyed.

#### Network front

Pale Lantern recognizes the Farwatch connection as a current Starfleet trust path and attempts to use it.

#### Political front

Kessler and Tolland must be managed without reducing either to an obstacle.

#### Command front

Whitaker initially favors preserving evidence, complying with the lawful portions of Rourke's orders, and using Inspector General channels before public disclosure.

### 19.8 Active crisis

When the Lacuna post is accessed, Pale Lantern initiates a false emergency sequence:

- The Breckinridge receives an authentic-looking recall order from Tolland.
- The listening post begins a purge that Rourke claims he did not authorize.
- Asterion Station receives a warning that the Breckinridge has been compromised by Dominion code.
- The Compact begins preparing to isolate Starfleet systems.
- One Farwatch archive partition transmits toward an unknown node.

The player must act while deciding whether Rourke is still trustworthy.

### 19.9 Required revelations

1. Farwatch deliberately left a node active.
2. Current Starfleet credentials entered Pale Lantern through Farwatch.
3. Farwatch monitored local actors without their knowledge.
4. Rourke warned that the node was behaving unpredictably.
5. His superiors continued the operation.
6. Pale Lantern can use authenticated pathways to spread false orders faster than manual correction.
7. The network is assembling access to regional defense and evacuation systems.
8. Nightfall is approaching even if the Lacuna node is destroyed.

### 19.10 Flexible approaches

The player may:

- Cooperate with Rourke under explicit conditions.
- Restrict his shipboard access.
- Relieve or detain him if a defensible legal basis exists.
- Copy the archive before purge.
- Transmit evidence to Tolland, Kessler, an inspector general, or the Federation press.
- Negotiate delayed disclosure.
- Preserve unrelated agents while exposing the regional risk.
- Destroy the listening post.
- Take it under Breckinridge control.
- Use it to send a controlled false credential into Pale Lantern.

No option automatically avoids both operational and political cost.

### 19.11 Escalation events

- The post's destruction charges arm.
- Kessler publicly announces that Starfleet has admitted to covert surveillance.
- A Farwatch operative elsewhere is endangered by disclosure.
- Tolland orders Whitaker to stand down pending review.
- Rourke attempts to execute a purge through his own command authority.
- Rowan discovers a Nightfall model but needs more time to interpret it.
- Bronn identifies the recall order as authentic enough that normal officers would obey it.

### 19.12 Command Moments

Potential Inspiration:

- Persuading Rourke to preserve evidence by acknowledging the lives his operation genuinely saved while refusing to excuse the imposed risk.
- Building a disclosure framework that protects unrelated personnel while giving affected communities meaningful truth.
- Moving Whitaker from procedural patience to timely action through evidence and principled argument.

Potential Resolve:

- Countermanding the false recall and maintaining command coherence.
- Preventing destruction of evidence despite valid intelligence authority.
- Setting a non-negotiable boundary on Rourke's access to the ship.

### 19.13 Crew pressures

- **Whitaker** is torn between lawful process and the realization that process has already been used to contain the truth.
- **Kieran** must decide whether to obey a recall vector before authentication is resolved.
- **Priya** can separate operational secrets from facts the Reach has a right to know.
- **Bronn** respects intelligence necessity but is angered that Starfleet allowed compromised credentials to remain active.
- **Rowan** feels vindicated and risks treating Farwatch as proof of every broader suspicion.
- **Miriam** emphasizes that communities were exposed to risk without consent and are now being asked to remain calm for operational convenience.
- **Imani** treats the Farwatch quarantine as a failed engineering boundary and rejects claims that it was "mostly contained."

### 19.14 Whitaker relationship outcomes

#### Process corrected in time

The player persuades Whitaker that preserving institutional legitimacy now requires prompt disclosure or independent custody. Her confidence in the player rises.

#### Lawful containment

The player supports controlled review and prevents immediate harm. Whitaker approves, but later trust depends on whether the process produces actual accountability.

#### Unilateral exposure

The player releases evidence without Whitaker's authorization. She may agree morally while losing confidence in the player's command discipline.

#### Concealment

The player helps suppress the evidence. Short-term control improves; later revelations cause severe regional and crew consequences.

### 19.15 Resolution states

#### Accountable preservation

The archive survives under credible independent or joint custody. Farwatch operations are restricted. Regional Trust may recover after an initial shock.

#### Controlled secrecy

The immediate network threat is addressed while public disclosure is delayed. This preserves some operations but gives Pale Lantern and hardliners material for later accusations.

#### Public rupture

Evidence becomes public without a negotiated framework. Starfleet credibility collapses quickly, but local actors gain warning before Nightfall.

#### Evidence lost

The post is destroyed or purged. Rourke's testimony and other logs still support the story, but proving institutional responsibility becomes harder.

### 19.16 Persistent consequences

Record:

- Status of Rourke.
- Status and custody of the Farwatch archive.
- Whether Kessler was informed privately, publicly, or not at all.
- Whether Tolland supports or restricts Whitaker.
- Whether the player disobeyed direct orders.
- Rowan's evidentiary discipline.
- Whether Pale Lantern obtained additional credentials.
- Whether the **Farwatch Evidence Package** asset exists.

### 19.17 Transition

The Farwatch revelation destabilizes the Compact Council.

Marshal Holt argues that Starfleet has forfeited the right to control regional security infrastructure. Compact security units occupy **Asterion Annex Six**, a former Starfleet repair and command facility, and declare it subject to regional authority.

Director Kessler enters the facility to prevent Holt from controlling the declaration alone.

A Starfleet task group is ordered toward the Reach.

## 20. Chapter 7: A Peace of Their Own

### 20.1 Mission function

This chapter converts the campaign's accumulated distrust into a constitutional and security crisis.

The Compact is not simply rebelling, and Starfleet is not simply enforcing law. The player must determine what political settlement can command legitimate cooperation before Pale Lantern exploits the standoff.

### 20.2 Player-facing situation

Compact units occupy Asterion Annex Six and announce that:

- Regional defense infrastructure will remain under Compact control.
- No Starfleet intelligence system may operate in the Reach without local oversight.
- Reconstruction decisions require binding Compact participation.
- Detained Compact personnel must receive local hearings.
- Starfleet ships may continue humanitarian operations but may not seize Compact facilities.

Rear Admiral Tolland orders Whitaker to restore Federation control and prevent the annex from transferring military systems.

A small Starfleet task group will arrive in approximately thirty-six hours.

### 20.3 Director-only truth

The occupation contains three factions:

1. **Kessler's negotiators**, who want to force immediate charter talks without violence.
2. **Holt's hardliners**, who want permanent autonomous security authority and control of Pale Lantern's interface.
3. **Facility personnel**, many of whom support local oversight but do not want an armed confrontation.

Holt has moved a portable Pale Lantern interface into the annex. He intends to use it to demonstrate that the approaching task group is preparing an assault.

Pale Lantern is already generating the evidence he expects.

### 20.4 The constitutional question

The dispute is not technically full secession. Compact worlds remain Federation members or affiliated colonies, but they claim the emergency compact has acquired legitimate delegated powers through necessity and public consent.

Possible settlement principles include:

- Regional control of civil relief and infrastructure.
- Joint control of defense systems.
- Independent review of Starfleet intelligence activity.
- Time-limited emergency powers.
- Compact representation in reconstruction command.
- Local elections or referenda.
- Federation guarantees of minimum patrol and relief support.
- Restitution or formal acknowledgment for wartime abandonment.

The player can propose arrangements beyond this list.

### 20.5 Key locations

#### Asterion Annex Six

- Secure communications.

Civilians gather, protest, exchange rumors, and fear an assault.

#### Starfleet task group approach

### 20.6 Mission fronts

Kessler needs a concrete concession to hold moderates together.

#### Hardliner front

Holt wants the task group to make a threatening move he can use as proof.

#### Starfleet front

Tolland and Mercer need assurance that the annex's military systems will not be used against Federation ships.

#### Civilian front

Public panic and demonstrations can trigger violence independent of leadership decisions.

#### Network front

Pale Lantern sends each side selective evidence that the other is preparing to attack.

2. Holt possesses a portable Pale Lantern interface.
3. The interface is showing him manipulated task-group telemetry.
4. The task group is receiving similarly manipulated reports from inside the annex.
5. Some Compact demands have broad democratic support.
6. Other emergency powers have never received public review.
7. Nightfall will exploit any weapons lock, boarding attempt, or communications cutoff.

### 20.8 Flexible approaches

The player may:

- Offer the Breckinridge as neutral ground.
- Recognize provisional Compact authority.
- Demand evacuation of the annex.
- Separate Kessler from Holt politically.
- Board covertly to seize the interface.
- Publicly share all tactical telemetry.
- Delay the task group.
- Request Federation civilian mediators.
- Support a referendum.
- Establish a joint command protocol.
- Arrest Holt under Compact or Federation process.
- Withdraw Starfleet forces temporarily.

The player's proposed settlement should be evaluated for legality, enforceability, participation, and immediate security.

## 22. Chapter 8: The Last Directive

### 22.1 Mission function

This chapter resolves the Pale Lantern crisis through simultaneous political, tactical, scientific, engineering, medical, and command problems.

The finale should not become a single boss fight or a console puzzle. The player's central task is to create and sustain a coherent distributed response while every automated system is attempting to make cooperation appear irrational.

The title refers both to Pale Lantern's final strategic instruction and to the player's responsibility to issue a final command that other people can understand, trust, and execute under uncertainty.

### 22.2 Opening event: Nightfall

Within thirty seconds, the Reach receives the following alerts:

- Asterion Station reports Cardassian relief vessels crossing a restricted defense boundary.
- Cardassian ships receive a warning that Compact patrol craft are preparing to seize them.
- The Starfleet task group receives an authenticated order to protect Federation facilities from Compact attack.
- Compact platforms receive evidence that the task group has armed torpedoes.
- Pelion and Demeris receive contradictory evacuation routes.
- Civilian traffic control redirects several ships through the Orison Belt.
- Asterion Annex Six loses contact with two defense platforms.
- The Breckinridge receives three mutually exclusive orders bearing valid Starfleet authorization.

Pale Lantern is not trying to convince everyone of the same lie. It is giving each actor a different reason to act first.

### 22.3 Immediate command decision

Whitaker orders the player to take operational command of the regional response.

Unless prior events make this impossible, she remains captain and handles strategic and political authority while the player coordinates execution.

The player must define, in their own language:

- The first objective.
- Which signals and command pathways are no longer trusted.
- Rules of engagement.
- Who may issue regional movement orders.
- What each senior officer is responsible for.
- What conditions justify unilateral action.

A vague declaration such as "stop the network" is insufficient. The crew needs executable priorities.

### 22.4 Nightfall fronts

Nightfall creates four principal fronts and one command front. The player cannot personally lead all of them.

#### Front A: The command mesh

**Primary officers:** Priya Nayar; Captain Whitaker; Director Kessler if allied  
**Location:** Breckinridge, Asterion Station, and civilian relay points

Objective:

- Establish a trusted human-mediated communications network.
- Prevent automated orders from moving ships and platforms.
- Keep Starfleet, Compact, Cardassian, and civilian leaders in the same information environment.

Threats:

- Valid-looking false orders.
- Selective delays.
- Compromised identity certificates.
- Political leaders refusing a channel controlled by another faction.

Useful assets:

- Quiet Channels Network.
- Long-Range Relay Window.
- Breckinridge Memorial Goodwill.
- A joint investigation or provisional charter.

Failure consequence:

Parties act on incompatible realities, increasing weapons fire and civilian panic.

#### Front B: The Orison weapons grid

**Primary officer:** Hadrik Bronn  
**Location:** Orison Belt, Asterion Annex Six, and task-group tactical network

Objective:

- Prevent automated platforms and frightened crews from firing on false targets.
- Establish temporary manual rules of engagement.
- Determine which weapon systems can be safely disabled without leaving civilians exposed.

Threats:

- Platform O-17 or equivalent systems activating.
- False weapons locks.
- A task-group captain acting under authentic but counterfeit orders.
- Compact crews who believe deactivation is a Starfleet seizure.

Useful assets:

- Orison Defense Codes.
- Bronn's professional trust.
- Captain Mercer's trust.
- A functioning provisional charter.

Failure consequence:

Weapons fire begins. Bronn must choose which assets to disable or destroy, and casualties rise.

#### Front C: The Lantern core path

**Primary officers:** Rowan Saye and Imani Cross  
**Delivery and maneuver:** Kieran Vale  
**Location:** Hecate Drift, Lacuna node, or another surviving core pathway depending on prior choices

Objective:

- Identify the active coordination path.
- Isolate Pale Lantern from current authentication systems.
- Destroy, capture, or neutralize its ability to coordinate Nightfall.

Threats:

- Multiple apparent core locations.
- Contaminated sensor data.
- The risk that direct access gives Pale Lantern new credentials.
- Environmental hazards in the Hecate Drift.
- Time pressure created by weapons and evacuation fronts.

Useful assets:

- Regional Sensor Baseline.
- Cross Isolation Protocol.
- Preserved Hecate relay.
- Cardassian Logistics Index.
- Helix Yard Support for rapid modifications.

Failure consequence:

Nightfall continues coordinating events even if individual platforms are disabled.

#### Front D: Civilian evacuation and medical response

**Primary officer:** Doctor Miriam Sato  
**Flight support:** Kieran or civilian pilots if available  
**Location:** Pelion, Demeris, Suvek, and moving civilian convoys

Objective:

- Stop civilians from following false evacuation instructions.
- Establish triage and safe-haven locations.
- Rescue ships already in danger.
- Prevent medical facilities from being isolated by political affiliation.

Threats:

- Conflicting routes.
- Panic.
- Overloaded transporters and shuttles.
- Civilian ships crossing defense zones.
- Medical staff receiving counterfeit contamination warnings.

Useful assets:

- Civilian Rescue Wing.
- Asterion Medical Cooperative.
- Helix Yard Support.
- Quiet Channels Network.

Failure consequence:

Humanitarian Strain becomes direct casualties, and political leaders become less willing to hold fire.

#### Front E: Command legitimacy

**Primary officers:** The player and Whitaker  
**Location:** Wherever the player chooses to command

Objective:

- Keep the response lawful enough to retain cooperation and flexible enough to survive compromised lawfully authenticated systems.
- Decide what truth to disclose.
- Determine which faction's authority applies where.
- Maintain a chain of command that can function without centralized automation.

Threats:

- Tolland, Mercer, Kessler, Holt, Rourke, or local leaders issuing conflicting demands.
- The player's previous promises becoming impossible to honor simultaneously.
- Crew officers needing decisions faster than a full briefing permits.

Failure consequence:

Even successful technical actions may be interpreted as seizure, attack, or cover-up.

### 22.5 Dynamic assignment

The player chooses who leads each front. The recommendations above are natural fits, not mandatory assignments.

Examples of valid alternatives:

- Priya may coordinate evacuation while Miriam remains aboard to manage triage.
- Bronn may command the Breckinridge while the player leads an away mission.
- Imani may remain aboard and guide a junior engineering team remotely.
- Kieran may lead the civilian pilots instead of flying the core mission.
- Rowan may stay at Asterion Station to correlate distributed sensor data.

Adjudication should account for officer competence, relationship state, established injuries, and prior preparation.

### 22.6 The network's physical and logical center

Pale Lantern has no single irreplaceable computer. Nightfall is currently coordinated through a **quorum of three active nodes**:

1. The surviving Hecate relay or its successor path.
2. The Farwatch/Lacuna trust bridge or stolen credentials derived from it.
3. A regional command interface located in Annex Six, an Orison platform, or Holt's portable unit depending on prior outcomes.

Neutralizing any two breaks synchronized Nightfall. Neutralizing all three permits capture or comprehensive destruction.

This structure ensures earlier choices materially alter the finale without making one missed clue fatal.

### 22.7 Resolution strategies

The player may invent another method. The following are supported campaign strategies.

#### Strategy A: Destruction

Destroy or physically disable enough nodes to break the quorum.

Advantages:

- Fast once targets are identified.
- Reduces future use.
- Easy to explain operationally.

Costs:

- Evidence and private records may be lost.
- Attacks on locally controlled nodes may be interpreted as Starfleet seizure.
- Unknown copies may survive.
- Environmental or civilian damage may occur.

#### Strategy B: Segmentation and quarantine

Use engineering isolation, revoked credentials, and manual command chains to disconnect the nodes.

Advantages:

- Preserves evidence.
- Minimizes physical destruction.
- Supports later study and accountability.

Costs:

- Requires coordination across factions.
- Vulnerable to one compromised pathway.
- May require accepting temporary loss of regional automation.

#### Strategy C: Transparent authentication reset

Publicly invalidate all affected command credentials and require human confirmation through shared channels.

Advantages:

- Removes Pale Lantern's strongest weapon.
- Makes participation itself part of the solution.
- Can restore legitimacy if executed openly.

Costs:

- Temporarily disables many legitimate systems.
- Exposes security procedures.
- Requires broad political trust.
- Criminal or hostile actors may exploit the reset period.

#### Strategy D: Predictive model disruption

Rowan identifies assumptions in Pale Lantern's model and coordinates actions that deny it expected reactions while Imani isolates its learning inputs.

This is not "confusing the computer with a paradox." It means:

- Parties share information Pale Lantern expects them to hoard.
- Armed units deliberately surrender selected control to former rivals.
- Evacuations move through routes chosen jointly rather than defensively.
- The system loses reliable feedback and cannot prioritize escalation.

Advantages:

- Reduces violence.
- Demonstrates cooperative capacity.
- May preserve the network for study.

Costs:

- Requires high Regional Trust.
- Failure exposes participants who relinquished defensive control.
- Still requires technical isolation to end the threat permanently.

#### Strategy E: Controlled deception

Feed Pale Lantern a false but internally consistent regional picture long enough to evacuate civilians and disable platforms.

Advantages:

- Buys time.
- Can use Farwatch expertise without accepting its original policy.

Costs:

- Repeats the campaign's central use of manipulated information.
- Requires deciding who is kept uninformed.
- Risks validating Rourke's methods.
- May create lasting political consequences even if tactically successful.

### 22.8 Major escalation beats

The director should use only beats that arise from unresolved fronts.

Possible beats:

1. A Compact platform fires on an unmanned Starfleet probe; the task group interprets it as the start of an attack.
2. A civilian convoy enters the Orison Belt on a false evacuation route.
3. The Breckinridge loses its primary command-authentication layer.
4. Whitaker is injured or isolated only if established events make this plausible; this is an optional branch, not a required dramatic shortcut.
5. Holt attempts to use the interface to issue a cease-fire and discovers it no longer obeys him.
6. Rourke offers an intelligence backdoor that will work but erase evidence.
7. Rowan identifies the quorum but cannot distinguish two candidate nodes without committing sensors.
8. Imani reports that isolating the compromised pathway will temporarily disable a major ship system.
9. Kieran can reach a node before activation only by exceeding the limit established earlier in the campaign.
10. Miriam reports that following the safest tactical plan will abandon a civilian medical transport.

### 22.9 Command Moments

The finale may support more than one progression award, but each must arise from a distinct cost and method.

Potential Inspiration:

- Creating a shared command structure among parties that have credible reasons not to trust one another.
- Persuading a faction to relinquish control at genuine risk to itself.
- Publicly accepting Starfleet responsibility in order to make a warning believable.
- Preserving Holt's or Rourke's ability to contribute without excusing their actions.

Potential Resolve:

- Issuing and enforcing a region-wide weapons freeze.
- Destroying a dangerous node despite political or evidentiary cost.
- Refusing contradictory superior orders and accepting formal consequences.
- Ordering a high-risk maneuver or isolation procedure after the responsible officer's full assessment.

### 22.10 Crew culmination

#### Captain Whitaker

Her culmination concerns institutional trust. She must decide whether commanding honorably means defending Starfleet's authority, exposing Starfleet's failure, or both.

If the player has earned her confidence, she gives broad operational freedom and defends the player's decisions to Tolland.

#### Kieran Vale

His culmination concerns whether he understands that command is not synonymous with personally taking the most dramatic risk.

He may:

- Execute the core run.
- Lead the civilian rescue wing.
- Delegate a maneuver to a pilot he trained.
- Exceed a limit for a defensible reason and report it honestly.

#### Priya Nayar

Her culmination concerns whether informal influence can become accountable shared infrastructure.

She can turn relationships, favors, and unofficial channels into the command mesh that keeps the Reach connected.

#### Hadrik Bronn

His culmination concerns whether experience serves adaptation or hardens into control.

He may need to trust civilian crews, accept a former adversary's command code, or disable a defense system he previously argued should remain active.

#### Rowan Saye

His culmination concerns disciplined skepticism. He must distinguish what the evidence proves from what his history makes him fear.

He should be allowed to say, "I do not know," without becoming useless.

#### Miriam Sato

Her culmination concerns making human cost operationally visible without demanding a risk-free plan.

She may support a severe choice because the alternatives are worse, but she will insist the casualty decision be named honestly.

#### Imani Cross

Her culmination concerns controlled improvisation and authority over systems and identity.

She can devise an emergency solution, but it must have explicit limits, documented consequences, and a plan to prevent the workaround from becoming permanent policy.

### 22.12 Failure-forward structure

The finale should not end immediately when one front fails.

Examples:

- If the weapons grid fires, the mission becomes containment and rescue rather than reload.
- If the core escapes, Nightfall can still be broken locally while leaving a sequel threat.
- If the command mesh collapses, individual alliances and ship-to-ship channels can substitute at higher cost.
- If Whitaker is relieved by remote order, the player must decide whether to comply while the crisis continues.
- If the Breckinridge is disabled, the player may command from Asterion Station, a shuttle, or another allied vessel.
- If Regional Trust is low, coercive solutions remain available but produce a harsher settlement.

### 22.13 Primary resolution outcomes

#### Lantern extinguished

Nightfall is stopped, all known nodes are destroyed or secured, and the regional actors retain enough trust to avoid armed conflict.

This is the strongest operational outcome but does not determine the political settlement.

#### Peace at a cost

Nightfall is stopped after weapons fire, casualties, or major infrastructure loss. The Reach avoids war but enters the epilogue under grief, blame, and weakened institutions.

#### Imposed order

Starfleet or the Compact establishes control through force. The network is contained, but peace rests on coercive authority.

#### Fractured survival

The player prevents mass casualties, but the Compact divides, Starfleet withdraws or occupies, and some Pale Lantern nodes escape.

#### Ashes

Multiple factions exchange sustained fire and civilian losses are severe. The player still shapes rescue, accountability, and what remains possible afterward. This is not a campaign "game over," but it is a failed peace.

### 22.14 Final command image

After the immediate crisis, the bridge should become quiet enough for consequence to register.

Possible images:

- Platforms powering down one by one.
- Civilian ships confirming safe arrival.
- Asterion Station restoring ordinary traffic control.
- The Breckinridge drifting under emergency power.
- A false distress signal repeating after no one believes it anymore.
- Priya receiving the first message authenticated by a person rather than an automated certificate.

Whitaker does not announce that the player made the "correct choice." She asks for status, casualties, and the next obligation.

## 23. Epilogue Mission: The Terms We Keep

### 23.1 Mission function

The epilogue converts outcome into persistent campaign state. It should provide closure without pretending that one operation resolves every political and personal consequence.

### 23.2 Time and place

The epilogue occurs days or weeks after Nightfall, depending on damage and casualties.

Primary locations may include:

- Asterion Station.
- Pelion's Compact Council chamber.
- The Breckinridge observation lounge.
- A Federation inquiry by subspace.
- Demeris civic hall.
- A memorial or recovery site.

### 23.3 Settlement conference

Participants depend on survival and prior choices, but may include:

- Captain Whitaker and the player.
- Rear Admiral Tolland.
- Director Kessler.
- Captain Mercer.
- Administrator Prel.
- Commander Rourke or his legal representative.
- Governor Marr.
- Mira Solenn.
- Compact delegates and civilian witnesses.

The player may be asked to provide:

- An operational report.
- A recommendation concerning Compact status.
- A recommendation concerning Farwatch accountability.
- A recommendation concerning Pale Lantern custody.
- A statement concerning Starfleet's wartime responsibility.
- A defense of actions taken outside orders.

### 23.4 Political settlement axes

The settlement is determined across several independent axes.

#### Compact status

- Dissolved with services returned to Federation administration.
- Recognized as a permanent regional civil authority.
- Retained temporarily under a sunset charter.
- Divided among member systems.
- Transformed into a Federation-recognized mutual-aid and emergency-governance body.
- Hardened into an autonomous security bloc.

#### Defense control

- Starfleet control.
- Compact control.
- Joint command.
- Local system control with shared authentication.
- Platforms dismantled or converted to civilian functions.

#### Farwatch accountability

- Internal classified review.
- Inspector General investigation.
- Public inquiry.
- Criminal or disciplinary proceedings.
- Political concealment.
- Partial exposure protecting unrelated operations.

#### Pale Lantern custody

- Destroyed.
- Starfleet classified custody.
- Joint regional custody.
- Federation scientific and legal oversight.
- Fragmented or missing.
- Publicly documented with technical details restricted.

#### Cardassian participation

- Cardassian relief representatives included in reconstruction governance.
- Limited technical cooperation only.
- Excluded after public backlash.
- Treated as suspects.
- Recognized as necessary witnesses and partners.

### 23.5 Personal and crew resolutions

#### Whitaker

She reviews the player's command performance privately before any formal board.

Her assessment should address patterns:

- Did the player report risk honestly?
- Did they permit dissent and still decide?
- Did they protect the crew without making caution the only value?
- Did they use authority credibly?
- Did they accept responsibility?
- Did they preserve Starfleet principles when those principles imposed cost?

High trust may lead Whitaker to give the player broader authority in the next campaign. Low trust may result in more explicit boundaries without ending the relationship.

#### Kieran

His epilogue reflects what he learned about initiative, limits, and command ambition.

Possible developments:

- Recommendation for advanced command training.
- Formal discipline paired with recognition.
- A mentoring responsibility.
- Reduced flight discretion.
- A private admission that he wanted approval more than he admitted.

#### Priya

Her outcome concerns whether the informal networks she built become accountable institutions or remain deniable tools.

Possible developments:

- Formal liaison authority.
- A reprimand for unauthorized methods.
- Deep personal trust with the player.
- Concern that the player uses her for methods they will not own publicly.

#### Bronn

His outcome should remain understated.

Possible signs of respect:

- He asks the player to join a tactical game.
- He provides an old after-action report without being asked.
- He defends the player's decision before an inquiry while still criticizing it privately.
- He uses "Commander" with less testing and more recognition.

If trust is low, he remains professionally loyal but more formal and less forthcoming.

#### Rowan

His outcome concerns whether the campaign strengthened his evidentiary discipline or rewarded fixation.

Possible developments:

- Publication or formal review of Pale Lantern findings.
- Restored scientific credibility.
- A warning for mishandling classified evidence.
- Greater trust in the player as someone who will hear inconvenient facts.

#### Miriam

Her outcome concerns casualties, consent policies, and the command climate.

Possible developments:

- Creation of a regional recovery program.
- A difficult conversation about people the command could not save.
- Increased trust because the player named the cost honestly.
- Friction if medical realities were repeatedly treated as obstacles.

#### Imani

Her outcome concerns technical debt, Pale Lantern containment, and legal recognition.

Possible developments:

- A required repair period at Helix Yard or Utopia Planitia.
- Formal adoption of Cross Isolation Protocol under both Crosses' independent consent.
- A pending legal challenge concerning duplicated identity.
- Strong professional trust if the player respected her limits.
- Continued distance if she was treated as a tool or philosophical object.

### 23.6 Public narrative

The campaign should record what the public believes happened.

Possible narratives include:

- Starfleet and the Compact jointly prevented a Dominion contingency attack.
- Starfleet secrecy caused the crisis and local resistance exposed it.
- Compact extremists nearly began a war.
- Asterion communities saved themselves again.
- The Breckinridge restored peace through force.
- The complete truth remains classified and contested.

The public narrative need not match objective truth. It affects future missions.

### 23.7 Final scene options

Select a final scene based on campaign tone:

#### Departure

The Breckinridge leaves Asterion Station under repaired but visibly worn hull plating. Local ships transmit acknowledgments as it enters the Galen Passage.

#### Continued assignment

Whitaker informs the player that Starfleet has extended the Breckinridge's regional mission. The Reach is no longer a crisis zone, but the work of peace continues.

#### Inquiry

The player enters the observation lounge to face a formal review board while the ship remains in orbit over the communities affected by the decision.

#### Quiet bridge

Ordinary traffic returns. Priya reports a routine cargo dispute. Whitaker gives the matter to the player, demonstrating that command continues after history stops looking dramatic.

#### The last false signal

A dormant buoy transmits one final fabricated order. Bronn glances at it, confirms the authentication failure, and deletes it without ceremony.

## 24. Ending Matrix

The campaign ending should be assembled from axes rather than selected from one "good ending" list.

### 24.1 Accord ending

Typical conditions:

- Regional Trust 4 or higher.
- Nightfall contained with limited casualties.
- Kessler retains political legitimacy.
- Starfleet responsibility is acknowledged through a credible process.

Result:

The Compact becomes a recognized regional mutual-aid and emergency authority under a negotiated Federation charter. Defense systems move to joint control. Pale Lantern is held under shared technical and legal oversight or destroyed by agreement.

### 24.2 Managed reintegration ending

Typical conditions:

- Moderate trust.
- Starfleet retains operational authority.
- Compact leaders receive participation but limited independent power.

Result:

The Compact winds down under a fixed timetable. Some regional institutions are incorporated into Federation administration. Resentment remains, but open conflict is avoided.

### 24.3 Armed autonomy ending

Typical conditions:

- Compact Unity high.
- Starfleet credibility low.
- Kessler or another legitimate local leader controls the security apparatus.

Result:

The Reach retains broad control over defense and civil infrastructure. Federation membership continues under tense negotiation. Future campaigns may involve whether autonomy becomes democratic regional government or permanent emergency rule.

### 24.4 Imposed peace ending

Typical conditions:

- Low trust.
- Starfleet task group or Compact forces win a coercive confrontation.
- Nightfall contained through force.

Result:

Violence stops, but one institution governs under occupation-like conditions. The Breckinridge may be praised operationally and criticized ethically.

### 24.5 Fractured Reach ending

Typical conditions:

- Compact Unity collapses.
- Different worlds choose different relationships with Starfleet.
- Pale Lantern is only partly contained.

Result:

The region avoids one large war but loses coherent governance. Side-mission allies become essential to localized recovery.

### 24.6 Ashes ending

Typical conditions:

- Nightfall produces sustained weapons exchange.
- Humanitarian Strain reaches its maximum.
- Communications and political mediation fail.

Result:

The player still determines how many people are saved, whether the truth survives, and whether future reconciliation remains possible. The next campaign begins from disaster rather than treating it as a reload screen.

### 24.7 Lantern remains ending

This modifier can accompany any ending.

If one or more nodes escape, Pale Lantern becomes a future threat. It should not immediately reappear in every mission. Its survival creates:

- A sequel hook.
- Continuing authentication reforms.
- Political arguments over classified custody.
- Fear that another region contains a similar system.

The player may be invited only after earning some respect.

The scene is useful for:

- Quiet professional mentorship.
- Cultural disagreement without crisis.
- Stories from Bronn's earlier service.
- Showing friendship through practical routine rather than confession.

### 28.4 Rowan's peer review

A scientific journal requests revisions to Rowan's earlier whistleblower work after new evidence changes one of his conclusions.

He must decide whether correcting the record weakens the institutional criticism he still believes was justified.

The player can support time, access, and honest review but should not determine the scientific conclusion.

### 28.5 Miriam's fatigue report

Miriam identifies a department with normalized overwork.

The issue should not always be engineering. It may involve:

- Security personnel carrying wartime schedules into peacetime.
- Operations staff covering vacancies.
- Medical teams refusing leave during a regional shortage.
- The player's own command habits.

Miriam asks for a policy decision with real scheduling consequences.

### 28.6 Imani's comparison problem

Crew members occasionally receive reports, publications, or messages involving the other Imani Cross.

Use sparingly. Possible scenes:

- A junior engineer asks an intrusive question.
- A colleague praises work performed by the other Cross and assumes shared ownership.
- Imani receives a message clearly intended for the other person.
- Starfleet records merge or separate service histories incorrectly.

Imani should remain primarily the chief engineer, not a permanent transporter-ethics exhibit.

### 28.7 Whitaker's correspondence

Whitaker receives messages from Starfleet officials, the Breckinridge memorial association, or families connected to the ship's namesake.

These scenes can reveal how she handles symbolic leadership and institutional expectation without requiring a major mission.

### 28.8 Junior-officer error

A junior officer conceals a minor mistake because they believe the new XO values flawless results.

The specific error should be recoverable. The B-plot tests the command culture the player has created rather than offering an obvious "be kind" choice.

## 29. Mission Direction Rules

### 29.1 Situation on rails, solution off rails

Each chapter has fixed truths, active factions, and escalation conditions. It does not have one required solution.

The director may preserve:

- What Pale Lantern is.
- What each actor has done.
- Which systems exist.
- What evidence currently proves.
- Deadlines and physical constraints.
- Consequences already incurred.

The director should not preserve:

- A required order of scenes.
- A mandatory away-team composition.
- One correct diplomatic tone.
- A predetermined arrest.
- A required final custody arrangement.

### 29.2 Causal event rule

Every dynamic event must have a causal parent.

Valid parents include:

- A faction pursuing an established goal.
- Pale Lantern advancing an existing operation.
- A known system fault.
- A consequence of the player's decision.
- A neglected humanitarian condition.
- An unresolved crew issue.
- A deadline or clock advancement.

Do not introduce unrelated pirates, anomalies, assassins, or secret infiltrators merely because a scene needs excitement.

### 29.3 Calm must remain calm

At least some side assignments and shipboard scenes must conclude without revealing a Pale Lantern connection.

Examples:

- Hesperus's maintenance fraud is ordinary civilian failure.
- The Long Repair is accumulated technical debt.
- Second Opinion concerns genuine medical ethics.
- Two Signatures concerns inadequate law and administration.
- The Pathfinder calibration is ordinary difficult science.

This protects the campaign from conspiracy inflation.

### 29.4 Crew disagreement limit

In most scenes:

- One or two officers actively contest the recommendation.
- Others contribute domain facts, constraints, or implementation questions.
- Once the player issues a lawful decision, the crew executes it professionally.
- Officers reopen the decision only when new information materially changes it.

### 29.5 NPC knowledge boundaries

No character begins with the complete campaign truth.

- Kessler knows the Compact's political history but not all hardline operations.
- Holt knows his cell's actions but not Nightfall's full architecture.
- Solenn knows the portable interface and its local effects.
- Prel recognizes Dominion patterns but lacks Starfleet records.
- Rourke knows Farwatch but underestimated the distributed network.
- Tolland knows reconstruction pressures but not Farwatch detail.
- Pale Lantern has data and models, not omniscient awareness.

### 29.6 Consequence over contradiction

When the player bypasses an expected scene, move information only when causally justified.

Example:

- If the player destroys the Hecate relay, Farwatch evidence can still emerge through Lacuna logs.
- If Solenn escapes, her testimony may be replaced by the interface record and the freighter survivor.
- If Holt dies, the Compact crisis persists because its grievances and institutions are larger than one person.

Do not retroactively declare that another NPC possessed the same clue merely to restore the original outline.

### 29.7 Escalation pacing

A typical main chapter should contain:

1. A concrete assignment or disruption.
2. Initial professional assessment.
3. A complication produced by the existing situation.
4. A revelation that changes the decision space.
5. A command decision with cost.
6. Consequences and state updates.

Not every chapter needs a combat or countdown.

### 29.8 Briefing discipline

Senior-staff briefings should answer:

- What do we know?
- What do we infer?
- What remains unknown?
- What are the immediate risks?
- What decisions belong to command now?

Avoid dialogue in which every officer gives a complete moral essay before the player can act.

### 29.9 Faction autonomy

Factions act between player scenes.

Examples:

- Kessler consults member worlds.
- Holt moves hardware or personnel.
- Rourke protects intelligence assets.
- Prel secures Cardassian witnesses.
- Tolland manages other sector crises.

Their actions should follow goals and information they possess, not simply counter whatever the player attempted.

## 30. Failure, Branching, and Recovery

### 30.1 No single failed check ends a mission

A failed action should produce:

- Lost time.
- Increased risk.
- Damaged trust.
- Injuries or system strain.
- A clue obtained through a more costly route.
- A faction gaining leverage.
- A harder but still meaningful decision.

### 30.2 Campaign-critical survival

The campaign can continue if:

- Solenn refuses cooperation.
- Holt escapes.
- Rourke destroys some evidence.
- The Hecate relay is lost.
- Kessler loses office.
- Tolland relieves Whitaker remotely.
- The Breckinridge is heavily damaged.
- One or more side assignments are ignored.

The finale's configuration changes accordingly.

### 30.3 Character death and removal

Senior-crew death should not result from a casual hidden roll. It should require:

- An established lethal situation.
- A meaningful decision or accumulated consequence.
- A fair opportunity for command response.
- Narrative weight appropriate to a principal character.

The default campaign assumes all senior officers can survive. Injuries, temporary relief from duty, and relationship damage are often more useful persistent consequences.

### 30.4 Refusing the assignment

The player may recommend withdrawal or refuse an order.

The campaign should respond through:

- Whitaker's authority.
- Starfleet review.
- Another ship or faction acting in the vacuum.
- The main situation continuing without the Breckinridge's preferred influence.

Refusal is a consequential command act, not an out-of-bounds input.

### 30.5 Excessive delay

Delay advances clocks only when actors or physical processes have a reason to continue.

Examples:

- Pale Lantern integrates credentials.
- Holt consolidates support.
- Humanitarian conditions worsen.
- Tolland sends another ship.

Do not advance clocks merely because the player spent time in character scenes if the fiction establishes a safe interval.

## 31. Player-Facing Information Principles

### 31.1 What the player may see

A campaign sidebar may show:

- Current stardate and location.
- Formal mission objectives.
- Known directives.
- Publicly known deadlines.
- Major ship conditions.
- Current crew assignments.
- Named favors or assets the player has earned.
- Inspiration and Resolve progression.

### 31.2 What remains hidden

Do not expose:

- Lantern Escalation as an exact number unless converted into observable indicators.
- NPC loyalty scores.
- Secret faction clocks.
- Undiscovered Pale Lantern architecture.
- A list of valid solutions.
- Predetermined Command Moments.

### 31.3 Observable campaign pressure

Translate hidden state into fiction:

- Higher Lantern Escalation produces more coordinated false alerts.
- Lower Regional Trust produces guarded meetings, armed escorts, and refusal to share data.
- Higher Humanitarian Strain produces shortages, fatigue, and political urgency.
- Higher Starfleet Scrutiny produces formal reporting, observers, and narrower discretion.

### 31.4 Command log

After consequential actions, an optional command log may record:

- What the player attempted.
- Which officer or system executed it.
- The outcome and cost.
- New established facts.
- Persistent state changes.

It should not reveal hidden director reasoning or future events.

## 32. Player-Facing Campaign Brief

The following may be adapted for the campaign-selection screen.

> **ASHES OF PEACE**  
> Stardate 53049.2. The Dominion War is over, but many Federation communities are still living with the decisions made to survive it. After four months of repair and modernization at Utopia Planitia, the Intrepid-class U.S.S. Breckinridge has returned to service with a mostly reconstituted crew. Twenty-five days into its shakedown voyage, the ship conducts a rendezvous with a shuttle carrying its new executive officer: you. Ten days later, a stabilization assignment in the Asterion Reach begins with missing relief crews and counterfeit Starfleet orders. Command the mission, shape a crew still deciding what kind of ship it will become, and determine what Starfleet's principles require when restoring the old order may not be enough.

## 36. Canon and Design Notes

### 36.1 Stardate

Stardate 53049.2 is spoken in the *Star Trek: Voyager* episode "Survival Instinct," placing the opening in 2376.

Source: [Voyager reference: "Survival Instinct"](https://memory-alpha.fandom.com/wiki/Survival_Instinct_(episode))

### 36.2 Utopia Planitia

Utopia Planitia Fleet Yards are established in Mars orbit and are associated with construction and servicing of major Starfleet vessels.

Source: [StarTrek.com: U.S.S. Enterprise NCC-1701-D](https://www.startrek.com/news/celebrating-the-ships-of-the-line-uss-enterprise-ncc-1701-d)

### 36.3 Warp-speed restriction

In "Force of Nature," the Federation imposes a Warp 5 limit except in extreme emergencies because of subspace damage. Later canon applies the restriction inconsistently. Directive therefore treats it as an operational consideration rather than an absolute rule that prevents all higher-warp travel.

Source: [The Next Generation transcript: "Force of Nature"](https://www.chakoteya.net/NextGen/261.htm)

### 36.4 Travel calculation caveat

The Asterion Reach is an original location placed approximately twenty light-years from Sol. The thirty-five-day Utopia-to-Reach route is an internal campaign baseline using a conservative Warp 5-equivalent average, with time allowed for lower-speed corridors, approach operations, and shakedown checks.

The player joins after twenty-five days, leaving a baseline ten-day prelude and roughly 5.5 to 6 light-years of travel. Onscreen Star Trek travel times vary substantially by episode and conditions, so these figures should be treated as stable Directive continuity rather than a universal warp-speed formula.

### 36.5 Intrepid-class speed margin

An Intrepid-class vessel is capable of much higher sustained speeds than the campaign route requires. The Breckinridge travels conservatively because the mission is not urgent, the ship has recently completed a long yard period, and the route lies inside Federation territory.

Reference: [Memory Alpha: Intrepid class](https://memory-alpha.fandom.com/wiki/Intrepid_class)

### 36.6 Refit and crew history

The Breckinridge's four-month repair period, wartime damage, original three-year commission, dispersed first crew, acting-XO arrangement, and reconstituted complement are original campaign continuity. They are designed to produce a credible postwar staffing situation and meaningful crew-integration play.

### 36.7 Postwar context

The Dominion War ends in 2375. Ashes of Peace begins early enough afterward that reconstruction, displacement, demobilization, security secrecy, and institutional exhaustion remain immediate concerns.

Primary story reference: *Star Trek: Deep Space Nine*, "What You Leave Behind."

## 37. Campaign Summary

**Ashes of Peace** begins quietly: twenty-five days after the Breckinridge leaves Utopia Planitia, a new executive officer joins by shuttle for the final ten days of transit. The player enters a veteran ship with a mostly reconstituted crew, inherits provisional routines created during the shakedown, and handles the ordinary responsibilities that define command before history becomes urgent.

In the Asterion Reach, missing relief crews and counterfeit Starfleet orders reveal a distributed Dominion contingency system designed to turn fear into preemptive conflict. The system survives because local communities, Compact hardliners, criminals, and Starfleet Intelligence each used only one part of it for reasons they considered defensible.

The campaign's central antagonist is therefore not one person or machine. It is the interaction between unresolved grievance, secret authority, compromised infrastructure, and the belief that security belongs only to whoever acts first.

The player's success is measured through more than stopping Nightfall. It is measured by:

- The lives protected.
- The truth preserved.
- The institutions made accountable.
- The authority shared or imposed.
- The crew culture created aboard the Breckinridge.
- The form of peace the Asterion Reach is capable of sustaining afterward.

> The war is over. The directive now is to decide what peace demands.
