# Directive

## Intrepid-Class Starship Bible

**Document status:** Ship location and portrayal design baseline  
**Version:** 0.1  
**Campaign era:** Late 2370s baseline, adaptable by campaign  
**Primary campaign use:** Intrepid-class campaigns modeled after *Star Trek: Voyager*, including the U.S.S. Breckenridge  
**Companion documents:** *Directive Breckenridge Senior Staff Character Bible*, *Directive: Ashes of Peace Campaign*, and *Character Bible Shaping Guide*

---

## Document Purpose

This document defines the Intrepid-class starship as a story environment rather than a list of technical trivia.

It should guide:

- Director decisions about which ship facts matter in a scene.
- Narrator descriptions of exterior approach, internal movement, damage, repair, and daily shipboard life.
- Ship Director and Mission Director packet construction.
- Future ship-area datasets, retrieval cards, and continuity facts.
- Contradiction guards for hard physical details, such as shuttlebay placement.

This bible is the deep source. It should not be injected wholesale. Runtime packets should lift only the facts relevant to the current scene: one hard anchor, two sensory or operational textures, and one constraint or pressure. The goal is to stop the model from guessing generic starship details while still leaving room for scene-specific prose.

The Intrepid-class baseline here is modeled after the U.S.S. Voyager. The U.S.S. Breckenridge is not Voyager. It may share class layout, design language, and operational assumptions, but it should not inherit Voyager's crew history, Delta Quadrant events, or one-off modifications unless a campaign explicitly authors those details.

# 1. Reference Grounding

## 1.1 Source posture

Use canon and reputable franchise references for hard class facts, then translate those facts into Directive-authored scene guidance. Do not copy episode dialogue or encyclopedia prose into generated content. The useful layer for Directive is not "say what Memory Alpha says"; it is "make the ship behave like an Intrepid-class vessel when a scene touches the ship."

Primary hard anchors for this bible:

- The Intrepid class is a compact long-range Starfleet explorer with 15 decks, a normal crew around 140-150, and advanced 2370s systems.
- Deck 1 contains the bridge, ready room, and briefing room.
- Deck 2 includes the mess hall and captain's private dining room.
- Deck 3 contains the captain's quarters.
- Deck 4 contains transporter rooms and Cargo Bay 2 references.
- Deck 5 contains sickbay and the CMO office.
- Deck 6 contains the standard holodecks and armory references.
- Deck 8 includes astrometrics, science lab, Cargo Bay 2 references, and crew quarters.
- Deck 10 contains the primary shuttlebay complex in the aft dorsal secondary hull.
- Deck 11 contains main engineering, deflector control references, and major support spaces.
- Lower decks and Jefferies tubes are important maintenance, damage-control, and isolation spaces.

These are scene constraints, not excuses to recite deck numbers. If the player says "I head to sickbay," the runtime should know Deck 5 and the physical layout. The prose should usually show the lift ride, the transition into medical light, the CMO's office proximity, or the triage pressure rather than announce a reference entry.

## 1.2 Canon and adaptation boundary

Treat the following as hard class truth unless a package intentionally overrides it:

- The main shuttle approach is aft, toward the Deck 10 shuttlebay area in the secondary hull. Do not depict a routine shuttle docking into a wide mouth in the underside of the saucer.
- The ship is compact compared with Galaxy-class scale. It can feel advanced and capable, but it should not feel like an endless hotel or city.
- The bridge is a working command space with three-level verticality, not a flat generic cockpit.
- The ship has advanced systems, but they create dependencies. Bio-neural systems, variable-geometry pylons, holodeck power, transporters, and EMH infrastructure are all usable story pressures.
- An Intrepid-class ship can land on a planetary surface under blue alert conditions, but landing is a major operational event, not a casual parking maneuver.

Treat the following as Directive-authored adaptation choices:

- Exact Breckenridge room names, department cultures, postwar refit changes, and any wartime repairs.
- Which lounge, lab, workshop, or enlisted commons recurs in a campaign.
- Whether shuttlebay 2 is called "shuttlebay two," "the maintenance bay," "the aft hangar," or a ship-specific nickname in dialogue.
- How much of Voyager's later Delta Quadrant improvisation exists aboard a Federation-side Intrepid-class ship in 2376. Default answer: none, unless authored.

# 2. Class Personality

## 2.1 At a glance

| Field | Directive baseline |
|---|---|
| Class role | Fast, advanced, long-range Starfleet explorer and response vessel |
| Story scale | Small enough for recurring spaces and familiar crew rhythms; capable enough for deep-space autonomy |
| Visual identity | Arrowhead saucer, compact secondary hull, aft shuttlebay, variable-geometry nacelles |
| Social identity | Professional, close-quarters, departmentally interdependent |
| Dramatic engine | Independence under constraints |
| Central contradiction | Built for self-sufficient long-range exploration, but emotionally and operationally dependent on a small crew's routines |
| Default tone | Quietly capable, efficient, modern, compact, lived-in |
| Avoid | Galaxy-class grandeur, Defiant-class militarism, generic saucer-underbelly shuttle doors, infinite labs, endless starbase corridors |

## 2.2 Core engine

An Intrepid-class ship turns distance into procedure. It is designed to make Starfleet policy, scientific inquiry, medical capacity, diplomacy, tactical response, and family-level daily life function inside a hull smaller than the heroic scale of earlier flagship designs.

The ship's engine for play is not "the ship can do anything." It is "the ship can attempt almost anything if the crew understands the tradeoffs." Its systems are fast, integrated, and sophisticated. That makes improvisation possible. It also means an apparently local failure can ripple through navigation, sensors, replicators, medical support, holodecks, or command control faster than a looser, older architecture would.

In scene terms, the Intrepid class is a pressure vessel for competence. The ship rewards officers who can name dependencies: if they route auxiliary power to shields, what does that cost the transporters; if they isolate a gel-pack grid, what else goes blind; if they launch a shuttle during combat, which arcs and approach lanes remain safe.

## 2.3 Central contradiction

The ship is both intimate and enormous.

From outside, it is a 344-meter Starfleet vessel with nacelles, shield grids, torpedo launchers, landing systems, shuttle capacity, science labs, transporter rooms, holodecks, and a warp core. From inside, it is a workplace where the same faces pass through the same turbolifts, where the mess hall becomes the social center because the ship is not large enough for anonymity, and where a damaged corridor on Deck 11 matters because half the engineering department has walked it for years.

This contradiction should shape narration. The ship can cross light-years. It can also trap two officers in a five-meter maintenance crawlway while a gel pack overheats behind a wall panel.

## 2.4 Storytelling values

**Compact capability.** The ship should feel dense with function. A door opens and there is a lab, a junction, a locker, a transporter control booth, a handrail around the warp core, or a replicator alcove whose paneling hides part of the nervous system.

**Procedural intimacy.** Crew members know small things: which turbolift doors hesitate after a refit, which Jefferies access is faster than the lift during night watch, which mess table catches the best forward view, which engineering catwalk conversation can be heard below.

**Advanced systems with human ownership.** The ship has automation, EMH support, bio-neural processing, and high-speed calculation. It still needs people to decide priorities, own risk, and notice when an apparently correct reading feels wrong.

**Exterior truth.** Scenes should respect the hull. Shuttles approach the aft shuttlebay, windows mean rooms have plausible exterior placement, the bridge is dorsal on Deck 1, and the deflector, nacelles, pylons, landing gear, and saucer windows carry physical meaning.

**Recoverable damage.** The ship can be hurt in specific ways. "The ship shook" is weaker than "Deck 11 lost inertial compensation along the starboard catwalk," or "the aft tractor emitter dropped out of the shuttle's approach solution."

# 3. Exterior Bible

## 3.1 Silhouette and first read

An Intrepid-class ship reads as fast before it moves. The primary hull is a long, arrowhead saucer rather than a wide circular disk. The secondary hull is tucked aft and compact, with the main deflector set forward in the engineering section. The nacelles sit on swept pylons and change posture for warp, giving the vessel a mechanical tell visible from outside.

For narration, the exterior should usually be described through operational perspective:

- A shuttle pilot sees the aft shuttlebay doors, pylon geometry, approach lights, tractor targeting marks, and the saucer's long back curving away above.
- A station controller sees the ship's compact mass, raised nacelles, forward sensor structures, and narrow warp profile.
- A damage-control scene sees scorched phaser arrays, venting thrusters, shield-grid flicker, landing strut stress, or a nacelle frozen out of proper articulation.
- A diplomatic arrival sees a ship that is clearly Starfleet but not a ceremonial flagship: sleek, modern, efficient, and serious.

## 3.2 Exterior zones

| Exterior zone | Story function | Scene detail |
|---|---|---|
| Dorsal saucer and bridge crown | Command identity and sensor profile | Bridge dome, dorsal arrays, forward windows, the sense that command sits visibly atop the ship |
| Forward saucer windows | Social and command-life signal | Mess hall and forward-facing rooms can look out over the bow |
| Main deflector and forward secondary hull | Science, navigation, and hazard work | Deflector pulses, graviton beams, torpedo apertures above, nearby engineering support |
| Aft dorsal secondary hull | Small-craft operations | Shuttlebay doors, approach lighting, arresting fields, maintenance hangar behind the flight deck |
| Variable-geometry nacelle pylons | Warp identity and vulnerability | Nacelles angle into warp posture; pylon status can telegraph readiness or damage |
| Ventral secondary hull | Core ejection, landing, support systems | Antimatter loading, intermix access, landing stress points, core ejection path |
| Aeroshuttle cradle | Rare auxiliary craft option | Integrated underside craft; do not use routinely unless campaign authors it |
| Landing mechanisms | Planetfall spectacle and risk | Blue alert, support struts, landing thrusters, deck-plate tension under gravity |

## 3.3 Shuttle approach truth

The main shuttlebay complex is aft, on Deck 10, in the dorsal aft secondary hull. In visual terms, a shuttle approaches from astern into the aft structure framed by the swept nacelle pylons and the compact engineering hull. This is a hard continuity anchor.

Correct approach shape:

- The small craft falls in behind the Intrepid-class ship.
- Helm or shuttle control aligns it with the aft bay and approach beacon.
- Tractor guidance or an arresting field may assist the final approach.
- The shuttle passes beneath or between the visual language of the nacelle pylon frame, depending on angle.
- The bay interior opens into a working flight deck, with maintenance access and the larger hangar/shuttlebay 2 behind it.

Incorrect approach shape:

- A wide mouth in the underside of the saucer.
- A primary-hull ventral docking bay for normal shuttle entry.
- A generic belly hangar under the bridge or forward saucer.
- A shuttlebay whose exterior placement ignores the aft secondary hull.

If prose needs a dramatic external view, use stern geometry: the ship's aft lights, raised nacelles, bay threshold, pylon shadows, and the shuttle's relative size against the secondary hull.

## 3.4 Planetary landing and blue alert

An Intrepid-class ship can enter atmosphere and land on a planetary surface. That ability should feel extraordinary because it exposes a starship to stresses it normally avoids. Blue alert is not a mood light. It is a shipwide posture: crew to assigned stations, landing systems verified, structural integrity watched, inertial damping and lifters coordinated, nonessential movement reduced, small craft secured, and department heads aware that the hull is about to become a load-bearing object in gravity.

Landing scenes should use:

- The nacelles and impulse systems adjusting for atmospheric handling.
- Support struts or landing mechanisms taking weight.
- Decks gaining a subtle sense of "down" that is more literal than artificial gravity normally makes it.
- Sickbay, engineering, and ops preparing for impact injuries, system strain, and power-grid spikes.
- Exterior windows showing atmosphere, dust, cloud, or terrain rather than starfield.

Do not use landing casually when a shuttle, transporter, orbit, or station docking is simpler. Landing is a mission choice with consequences.

# 4. Interior Design Language

## 4.1 Overall feel

The interior is late-24th-century Starfleet without flagship spaciousness. Corridors are clean and curved, with LCARS panels, ribbed wall segments, access hatches, and lighting that can shift quickly from calm to alert. Rooms are efficient and multi-use. Even comfortable spaces are work-adjacent.

Aboard an Intrepid-class ship, the player should feel the crew's proximity. Senior officers can run into each other between the bridge and mess hall. A junior engineer knows the sickbay nurse by face. The same turbolift routes, corridor junctions, and observation windows recur often enough to become familiar landmarks.

## 4.2 Sensory palette

Use sensory detail as operational information.

| Sense | Baseline detail | Under pressure |
|---|---|---|
| Sound | Doors, LCARS chirps, turbolift hum, environmental systems, distant deck vibration | Alert tones, forced-air surges, deck flex, muffled repair teams, relays cycling |
| Light | Warm Starfleet interior light, console color, sickbay white, engineering glow | Red or blue alert wash, emergency strips, pulsing diagnostic panels, blackout pockets |
| Touch | Smooth consoles, carpeted deck, handrails, warm panel edges near active conduits | Static discharge, deck tilt, hot access plates, vibration through ladder rungs |
| Smell | Neutral recycled air, replicator food, medical antiseptic, engineering ozone | Smoke, overheated insulation, sterilization, coolant, scorched circuitry |
| Movement | Turbolift transitions, short corridor walks, curved turns, lift delays | Sealed bulkheads, rerouted access, ladders, Jefferies tube crawls, emergency force fields |

## 4.3 Room-to-hull logic

Voyager production design cared about matching important interior spaces to exterior windows and ship placement. Directive should preserve that discipline. If a room has forward windows, use a plausible forward hull location. If a scene needs an exterior view from the mess hall or captain's quarters, remember those spaces face forward. If a scene needs a view aft, use aft lounge, shuttlebay control, certain engineering-adjacent windows, or an explicitly authored shipboard space.

This rule matters because it prevents the LLM from turning rooms into floating theater sets. The ship has a body. Scenes should know where they are inside that body.

# 5. Key Area Profiles

Each area profile uses the character-bible idea of engine, contradiction, tells, and reveal pressure, adapted for locations. A ship location should be playable from four angles:

- as a competent professional workspace;
- as a physical room with sensory and movement identity;
- as a source of social pressure and routine;
- as a continuity anchor with constraints.

## 5.1 Bridge, Deck 1

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Deck 1, dorsal command center |
| Function | Command, flight control, tactical, ops, science, shipwide prioritization |
| Adjacent spaces | Captain's ready room, briefing room, turbolift access, aft command/control area |
| Retrieval keywords | bridge, conn, helm, ops, tactical, science station, ready room, briefing room, captain's chair |
| Runtime anchor | Ovoid, multi-level command space with central command chairs and forward conn |

### Location engine

The bridge turns distributed ship activity into command decisions. It is not merely where the captain sits. It is where ops allocates resources, conn keeps the ship alive in motion, tactical understands threat arcs, science turns sensor returns into usable meaning, and command decides whose concern becomes the ship's priority.

### Central contradiction

The bridge is both public and private. Every officer can see command posture. Every hesitation, closed expression, changed alert status, or sudden order becomes shipwide weather. Yet the ready room and briefing room sit only a few steps away, holding the private work that command cannot conduct in front of everyone.

### Physical identity

The command area is central, with the captain and first officer positioned together. Conn sits forward and lower, giving flight control a cockpit-like immediacy inside a larger command room. Aft stations and the master systems display create a constant visual reminder that the bridge is reading the whole ship, not just the viewscreen.

### Working life

Normal watches should not feel empty. Officers rotate through stations, department reports come up through ops, a science officer may linger near a display to clarify a sensor ambiguity, and the duty officer's personal habits show in small ways: a mug at tactical during long patrol, a quiet systems check at shift change, a standby PADD passed to the XO before a briefing.

### Pressure behavior

Under red alert, the bridge becomes clipped and specific. Under blue alert, command sounds procedural and physical: landing stations, inertial stress, thruster balance, structural-integrity margins. Under diplomatic pressure, it becomes a listening room where the bridge crew's silence matters as much as orders.

### Scene shapes

- The player enters from the turbolift as the forward viewscreen shifts from starfield to tactical overlay, and ops has already prioritized three shipboard demands.
- A private disagreement begins in the ready room, but the bridge is close enough that both officers feel the cost of returning with unresolved tension.
- Conn reports the aft shuttlebay beacon has acquired the incoming craft; tactical quietly verifies no one else is riding the approach vector.
- The master systems display flags a gel-pack grid irregularity, and engineering's answer makes the bridge decide whether to trust automation or stop the maneuver.

## 5.2 Captain's ready room and briefing room, Deck 1

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Deck 1, adjacent to the bridge |
| Function | Private command work, classified calls, senior-staff briefings, command correction |
| Retrieval keywords | ready room, briefing room, private command, senior staff, captain's office |
| Runtime anchor | Immediate proximity to bridge; command can move from public order to private judgment in seconds |

### Location engine

These rooms convert bridge urgency into considered decision. The ready room lets command speak without performing for the bridge. The briefing room lets a problem become collective without making the whole bridge crew sit inside uncertainty.

### Physical identity

The ready room should feel like a working office, not a throne room. It has a desk, view, seating, replicator access, and enough personal objects to reveal the captain's command style. The briefing room is collaborative and sharper: table, wall display, windows or screens, PADDs, and officers arranging themselves according to relationship as much as rank.

### Pressure behavior

Private scenes here should carry the awareness that the bridge is one door away. A command argument cannot sprawl indefinitely. At any moment, an alert tone, a bridge call, or the captain's decision to step back out ends the private register.

### Scene shapes

- The captain keeps one hand on the back of a chair instead of sitting because the conversation is not yet a meeting.
- The briefing room display shows the Asterion Reach, but half the staff is really watching which risk the player names first.
- A classified subspace exchange ends, and the ready room door opens directly into a bridge that has no idea the captain's next order just changed.

## 5.3 Mess hall and galley, Deck 2

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Deck 2, forward section |
| Function | Food, informal diplomacy, crew morale, memorials, celebrations, overflow care |
| Adjacent spaces | Captain's private dining room, galley or reconfigured food-prep area where authored |
| Retrieval keywords | mess hall, galley, dining room, morale, crew meal, reception, memorial |
| Runtime anchor | Forward-facing social center with windows and limited anonymity |

### Location engine

The mess hall turns a starship into a community. It is where command policy becomes rumor, where junior officers decide whether senior officers are frightened, where a diplomatic reception becomes a logistical problem, and where the ship remembers people who are not present.

### Central contradiction

The room is ordinary until it is not. Breakfast can become triage. A wedding toast can become a casualty announcement. A private conversation can become public because there is nowhere truly separate inside a small ship.

### Physical identity

Use forward windows, replicators, tables that develop social geography, a serving area or galley if the campaign has one, and the subtle background of off-duty uniforms, duty shifts, PADDs, and half-finished meals. It is a lived-in commons, not a restaurant.

### Working life

The mess hall should remember recurring habits: who eats alone, which table night shift claims, which department comes in dusty from maintenance, who uses a replicated drink as a ritual, who notices when a senior officer does not appear for a meal.

### Pressure behavior

Under shortage, replicator limits, quarantine, or diplomatic crowding, the mess hall becomes a resource argument. Under grief, it becomes a place where Starfleet ceremony has to fit inside small tables and tired faces.

### Scene shapes

- A junior officer falls silent when the XO enters, and the silence tells more about ship morale than the morning report.
- A diplomatic guest mistakes the mess hall's modest scale for lack of importance, then realizes every department passes through it.
- During sickbay overflow, tables become treatment areas and the social center becomes a reminder that the ship has no spare space.

## 5.4 Captain's quarters, Deck 3

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Deck 3, forward primary hull |
| Function | Command privacy, off-duty continuity, late-night decisions |
| Retrieval keywords | captain's quarters, private quarters, command privacy, forward windows |
| Runtime anchor | Larger than standard quarters but still part of a compact ship |

### Location engine

The captain's quarters show what command keeps when it stops performing. They are useful for scenes where institutional authority meets personal cost: unsent letters, old charts, a private dinner that becomes a command conversation, or a silence after casualty reports.

### Physical identity

Use forward windows, a desk, living space, sleeping area, washroom references when needed, and a narrow line between comfort and readiness. These quarters are not palatial. Their value is privacy, not scale.

### Pressure behavior

If command is using quarters as a second ready room, something is wrong. Late-night calls, a PADD stack by the bed, or a meal left unfinished should imply stress without telling the player how to feel.

### Scene shapes

- The ship drops from warp, and the windows change before the captain reaches the comm panel.
- A private dinner becomes a working meal when ops asks for a decision that cannot wait for morning.
- A personal object from before the current mission catches another officer's eye, but asking about it would cross a line.

## 5.5 Transporter rooms, Deck 4

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Deck 4 |
| Function | Personnel and cargo transport, away-team deployment, medical emergency movement |
| Standard feel | Six-pad platform, raised control booth, wall panels, operator focus |
| Retrieval keywords | transporter, transport room, beam-out, pattern buffer, emergency transport |
| Runtime anchor | Three transporter facilities; bio-neural processing links transporters into shipwide system risk |

### Location engine

Transporter rooms turn mission choice into physical commitment. They are the threshold where the ship gives people to a planet, station, debris field, or hostile environment and tries to bring them back unchanged.

### Central contradiction

Transporters are routine until a person vanishes. The room's calm procedure should make failures more frightening, not less. A transporter chief's ordinary checklist carries moral weight because the machine is handling identity, injury, quarantine, and trust.

### Physical identity

Use the platform, control booth, wall panels, low hum, confinement beam shimmer, operator hand movements, and the formal posture of away teams waiting to step onto the pads. Transporter scenes should have staging: who steps up first, who hesitates, who watches from the door, who stays at the console.

### Pressure behavior

Transporter pressure can come from interference, biofilters, emergency site-to-site transport, medical routing to sickbay, pattern degradation, shield status, or uncertainty about what is being brought aboard.

### Scene shapes

- The away team materializes wet, coughing, and one person short, while the transporter chief is already replaying the pattern trace.
- Sickbay requests direct emergency transport and the room becomes a medical corridor without moving.
- The biofilter flags an ambiguity and command must decide whether urgency outweighs quarantine discipline.

## 5.6 Sickbay, Deck 5

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Deck 5 |
| Function | Medical care, surgery, quarantine, medical authority, EMH operations |
| Adjacent spaces | CMO office, medical lab, ICU/critical care, storage |
| Retrieval keywords | sickbay, CMO office, EMH, biobed, surgery, ICU, quarantine, medical lab |
| Runtime anchor | One major sickbay facility with three standard biobeds, surgical bed, CMO office, and EMH support |

### Location engine

Sickbay turns harm into decisions. It is where command cost becomes physical, where optimism must wait for scans, and where medical authority can stop a mission plan that looks acceptable from the bridge.

### Central contradiction

Sickbay is both compassionate and cold. It is built to save lives, but its monitors, force fields, surgical lighting, and morgue storage make the facts visible. The CMO's office being near the ward means private conversations occur within earshot of consequences.

### Physical identity

Use the main ward, biobeds, surgical bed, ceiling sensor cluster, CMO office, medical lab, isolation fields, EMH emitters, and the contrast between bright medical light and the rest of the ship. The room can be efficient without becoming sterile prose.

### Working life

Normal sickbay scenes include routine exams, crew avoiding checkups, medical staff managing privacy, EMH availability, instrument sterilization, and the emotional difference between "cleared for duty" and "well."

### Pressure behavior

Under casualties, sickbay runs out of privacy before it runs out of competence. Overflow may move to the mess hall or cargo spaces. The medical officer becomes a command actor when they set return-to-duty limits, quarantine conditions, or evacuation priorities.

### Scene shapes

- The CMO steps out of the office before the door fully closes because the patient on the surgical bed has started to crash.
- The EMH appears before anyone asks, which means the ship has already classified the injury as above routine threshold.
- A command briefing relocates to sickbay because the person with the answer cannot leave the biobed.

## 5.7 Holodecks, Deck 6

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Standard baseline Deck 6 |
| Function | Recreation, training, therapy, simulation, controlled fantasy |
| Retrieval keywords | holodeck, simulation, training program, recreation, safeties |
| Runtime anchor | Two holodecks with separate power assumptions; not infinite free space |

### Location engine

Holodecks let the ship create what it lacks: wilderness, distance, training hazards, cultural ritual, personal memory, or a controlled version of an uncontrolled problem. They are not merely amusement rooms. They are pressure valves and rehearsal chambers.

### Central contradiction

The holodeck is the most artificial place aboard and often the most honest. Officers may reveal what they miss, fear, rehearse, or cannot say in an ordinary room.

### Physical identity

Use the arch, grid reveal, program transition, safeties, power isolation, scheduling, and the moment when a simulated world collapses back into a black-and-yellow room. Do not make every holodeck scene a novelty episode. The setting should expose a character or solve a mission problem.

### Pressure behavior

Holodeck pressure can involve program bleed, safeties, emotional dependence, training ethics, limited scheduling, power-grid debate, or the awkwardness of discovering what someone uses the room for.

### Scene shapes

- A tactical drill ends and the grid returns, leaving officers embarrassed by how real their fear was.
- A grieving crew member keeps restarting the same landscape until a friend notices the program never advances past sunset.
- Engineering uses the holodeck to model a damaged chamber because walking into the real one would kill the repair team.

## 5.8 Armory and security suite, Deck 6 baseline

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Deck 6 baseline for armory; brig/security office ship-specific as authored |
| Function | Weapons storage, security response, evidence handling, prisoner control |
| Retrieval keywords | armory, brig, security office, tactical locker, phaser, evidence |
| Runtime anchor | Security is procedural; weapons and detention require accountable handling |

### Location engine

Security spaces turn trust into inventory. Who has weapons, who is confined, what evidence is sealed, who can override a lock, and who records the chain of custody all become story facts.

### Central contradiction

Starfleet security wants to be unobtrusive until it suddenly cannot be. The armory and brig are signs that a peaceful explorer still expects coercion, danger, and the need to restrain people.

### Physical identity

Use lockers, access codes, sign-out records, force fields, compact workstations, and the formal discomfort of officers standing in a room built around the possibility that conversation has failed.

### Scene shapes

- A phaser rifle sign-out becomes a moral argument because the mission profile says rescue and the threat profile says boarding action.
- A prisoner in the brig knows the ship's walls better than the officer asking the questions.
- Security locks down a corridor and suddenly every ordinary door on the ship has become a boundary.

## 5.9 Astrometrics and science labs, Deck 8

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Deck 8 baseline for astrometrics and science lab references |
| Function | Stellar cartography, high-order sensor fusion, scientific modeling, anomaly interpretation |
| Retrieval keywords | astrometrics, science lab, stellar cartography, sensor analysis, anomaly, map |
| Runtime anchor | Deck 8 analytical spaces make space visually and mathematically legible |

### Location engine

Astrometrics turns the unknown into navigable shape. It is where the ship stops treating space as background and starts treating it as terrain, weather, history, hazard, and opportunity.

### Central contradiction

Astrometrics creates clarity by hiding scale. A glowing map can compress light-years into a wall, making an impossible distance feel actionable. The danger is command mistaking a beautiful model for a complete truth.

### Physical identity

Use dimmer light, wide displays, volumetric maps, sensor overlays, long observation periods, and specialists speaking in layers: raw reading, corrected reading, model confidence, and operational consequence.

### Working life

Science labs should not be generic. Biology, medical research, stellar cartography, holographic research, and sensor analysis all have different rhythms. A good science scene names what kind of knowledge is being produced.

### Pressure behavior

Astrometrics becomes dramatic when the map changes. A safe corridor closes, a signal appears older than physics permits, a nebula hides a ship, or a model becomes less certain as more data arrives.

### Scene shapes

- The map updates and the shortest route home becomes the least defensible route through contested space.
- A scientist does not contradict command directly; they changes the model scale until everyone sees the risk.
- Sensor ghosts appear in three bands, and the question becomes whether the ship is seeing an object, a reflection, or itself.

## 5.10 Crew quarters, multiple decks

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zones | Decks 2, 4, 8, 9, and 13 baseline for many quarters; captain on Deck 3 |
| Function | Private life, fatigue, off-duty relationships, memory, decompression |
| Retrieval keywords | quarters, cabin, bunk, junior officer, VIP quarters, personal space |
| Runtime anchor | Smaller than larger-class quarters; privacy is precious |

### Location engine

Quarters reveal the life a uniform compresses. They are where an officer stops being a station, where a relationship becomes private, and where a campaign can show fatigue without turning every scene into confession.

### Central contradiction

Quarters are private spaces inside a non-private machine. The ship can call at any moment. The walls carry alert tones. A personal object may sit ten meters from a life-support conduit.

### Physical identity

Use modest scale, replicator terminals, work surfaces, sleeping area, washroom references when needed, small windows for senior/officer quarters, and personal objects that reveal habits rather than exposition.

### Pressure behavior

Quarters become story spaces during insomnia, quarantine, disciplinary relief from duty, quiet celebrations, grief, secure calls, or when someone refuses to leave because the ship outside the door has become too much.

### Scene shapes

- A junior officer's room has no window, so the starfield exists only as reflected light from a terminal.
- An officer cleans a tool that does not need cleaning because the alert cycle has ended but their body has not believed it.
- A VIP quarter arrangement becomes diplomacy before anyone reaches the conference table.

## 5.11 Main engineering, Deck 11

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Deck 11 |
| Function | Warp core control, power distribution, systems integration, repair authority |
| Adjacent spaces | Chief engineer's office, open work area, upper catwalk, ladders/elevator, support rooms |
| Retrieval keywords | engineering, warp core, catwalk, chief engineer, power grid, plasma, core ejection |
| Runtime anchor | Two-level room around the warp core, with office/work area and upper catwalk |

### Location engine

Main engineering turns desire into cost. Command can ask for speed, shields, transport, weapons, landing posture, or life support priority. Engineering explains what the ship must give up, risk, overheat, isolate, or repair to make that happen.

### Central contradiction

Engineering is the most powerful room aboard and one of the least ceremonial. It has the warp core, but it also has tools, panels, exhausted specialists, and a chief engineer who knows which miracle was really a deferred failure.

### Physical identity

Use the warp core as vertical center, the monitoring area in front of it, upper catwalk, ladder or small lift access, chief engineer's office, open project space, and the distinction between console diagnosis and hands-on repair. The room should have depth and height.

### Working life

Engineering should feel staffed. People cross behind conversations carrying parts, a diagnostic crawl runs in parallel with the scene, a junior specialist waits for permission to interrupt, and someone is always watching the core or power distribution even during personal conversations.

### Pressure behavior

Under stress, engineering becomes triage. Systems are prioritized, rerouted, isolated, or sacrificed. The chief engineer should name accepted risk, temporary workaround, pending verification, and unresolved fault as different things.

### Scene shapes

- The player arrives on the lower level while the chief engineer calls down from the catwalk, and the vertical separation tells the scene who owns the room.
- The warp core's rhythm changes by a fraction, and every engineer hears it before the computer alarm catches up.
- Command asks for another thirty seconds at warp, and engineering answers with what those thirty seconds will consume.

## 5.12 Deflector control and forward engineering support, Deck 11 baseline

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Deck 11 and forward secondary-hull support areas |
| Function | Navigational deflector operations, hazard interaction, sensor/field work |
| Retrieval keywords | deflector control, navigational deflector, graviton beam, subspace field, hazard |
| Runtime anchor | The deflector is an external system with internal rooms, crews, and risk |

### Location engine

Deflector control is where the ship touches dangerous space without sending people outside. It can push, scan, clear, focus, or improvise a field interaction, but every deflector trick routes stress through hardware and crew.

### Central contradiction

The deflector can make the ship seem powerful at a distance, but its control spaces are close, technical, and vulnerable. A command to emit a field becomes someone standing beside a console as the hull sings with reflected force.

### Scene shapes

- Science proposes a deflector pulse; engineering asks how many emitters will be left for navigation afterward.
- A repair team reaches a control junction while the exterior dish is still charged, and everyone speaks more softly than the danger deserves.
- A successful beam buys the ship a corridor through debris, but leaves a smell of overheated insulation in the support room.

## 5.13 Cargo bays, Deck 4 and Deck 8 references

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zones | Cargo Bay 2 appears in Deck 4 and Deck 8 references; use package-specific placement carefully |
| Function | Stores, mission equipment, organic/environmental storage, isolation, improvised facilities |
| Retrieval keywords | cargo bay, Cargo Bay 2, supplies, stores, environmental controls, alcoves, quarantine |
| Runtime anchor | Cargo bays are adaptable volume inside a compact ship |

### Location engine

Cargo bays are where the ship keeps future options. Relief crates, sample containers, sealed equipment, mission shelters, spare parts, and emergency stores are all potential plot levers.

### Central contradiction

Cargo space feels empty until the ship needs it, then it is never enough. The same bay can be a warehouse, quarantine area, detention overflow, refugee shelter, lab annex, or repair staging area depending on the crisis.

### Physical identity

Use stacked containers, environmental controls, cargo transport pads or handling equipment, floor markings, isolation fields, sealed lockers, and the acoustic emptiness of larger open volume compared with corridors.

### Pressure behavior

Cargo bay scenes work when inventory matters: which crates were opened, which sealed container warmed, which relief supplies cannot be replicated, which living cargo needs environmental changes, which mission gear is missing.

### Scene shapes

- An evidence container hums in an environmental cradle while two departments argue whether it is cargo, patient, or weapon.
- Relief supplies fill the deck grid, and the player can see at a glance that generosity has become arithmetic.
- A cargo bay becomes temporary housing, and the ship's compactness turns humanitarian care into a layout problem.

## 5.14 Shuttlebay 1 and shuttlebay 2, Deck 10 aft

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zone | Deck 10, aft dorsal secondary hull |
| Function | Shuttle launch/recovery, auxiliary craft operations, maintenance, mission staging |
| Spaces | Shuttlebay 1 flight deck; larger maintenance hangar/shuttlebay 2 behind it |
| Retrieval keywords | shuttlebay, aft bay, shuttle approach, shuttle control, launch, hangar, flight deck, Delta Flyer-style craft |
| Runtime anchor | Aft shuttlebay, not underside saucer; approach from astern |

### Location engine

The shuttlebay is the ship's physical handshake with missions too small, dangerous, delicate, or local for the starship itself. It is where away operations gain mass: pressure suits, sample lockers, pilots, maintenance teams, security checks, medical standby, and the first smell of another atmosphere on a hull.

### Central contradiction

The shuttlebay is a threshold to openness inside one of the most controlled spaces aboard. The outer doors can expose the room to vacuum. A launch can be routine or fatal. A returning shuttle carries story back into the ship before anyone files a report.

### Physical identity

Use aft doors, approach lights, arresting field, deck markings, shuttle control station, maintenance access, larger hangar space behind the primary bay, repair cradles, cargo handling, EVA lockers, and the noise change when outer doors cycle. The room should have industrial texture compared with bridge and sickbay.

### Working life

Normal shuttlebay life includes preflight inspections, hull patching, cargo loading, EVA checks, pilot arguments with maintenance, security sweeps, and the smell of warmed composite panels after atmospheric return.

### Pressure behavior

Under combat or emergency recovery, the bay becomes a coordinated risk stack: shields, bay force fields, tractor emitters, arresting fields, flight deck clearance, medical standby, and whether the incoming craft can still obey the approach solution.

### Scene shapes

- An incoming shuttle aligns from astern while the aft bay doors open inside the nacelle-pylon frame, and the deck crew watches the arresting field come alive.
- A damaged shuttle scrapes the landing grid and the whole scene moves from flight control to medical triage in one breath.
- Shuttlebay 2 is depressurized for a launch, leaving maintenance tools locked down and everyone speaking through suit comms.
- A routine preflight becomes tense when the pilot notices a maintenance tag has been signed by someone no longer aboard.

## 5.15 Jefferies tubes and maintenance access, multiple decks

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zones | Multiple decks; Deck 7, Deck 11, Deck 15 references are useful anchors |
| Function | Repair access, covert movement, isolation, emergency routing |
| Retrieval keywords | Jefferies tube, crawlway, access panel, relay, conduit, maintenance junction |
| Runtime anchor | Tubes make the ship intimate, physical, and dangerous |

### Location engine

Jefferies tubes turn the ship from architecture into body. They expose the nerves, pipes, relays, heat, and cramped routes that let officers bypass formal spaces and meet the ship directly.

### Central contradiction

The tubes are hidden infrastructure, but in crisis they become the most important corridors aboard. A captain may give an order from the bridge, but a crew member in a tube may decide whether that order becomes possible.

### Physical identity

Use narrow access, ladders, hatch labels, hand lights, panel heat, conduit hum, awkward body position, tool tethering, and the social intimacy of two people unable to stand or look away comfortably.

### Pressure behavior

Tube scenes work under physical constraint: time, heat, radiation, decompression, pursuer proximity, loss of comms, or the need to crawl through a space that was never meant for drama and therefore feels more real.

### Scene shapes

- Two officers disagree quietly because the tube carries sound and the panel between them is hot enough to matter.
- A junior engineer knows a faster route than the officer in charge, and accepting that knowledge becomes a command test.
- The ship seals bulkheads behind the repair team, turning a shortcut into a commitment.

## 5.16 Lower-deck support spaces, Decks 12-15

### At a glance

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Zones | Decks 12-15 |

| Retrieval keywords | lower decks, environmental control, secondary command processors, plasma relay, Deck 12, Deck 15 |
| Runtime anchor | The ship has low-glamour spaces where failures become hard to reach |

### Location engine

Lower-deck spaces make the ship's sophistication accountable. The bridge can abstract them as status colors. The people sent there encounter ladders, heat, dust, access restrictions, and the fact that "noncritical" systems only stay noncritical until they fail in sequence.

### Central contradiction

### Scene shapes

# 6. Ship Systems as Story Pressure

## 6.1 Bio-neural gel packs

### System engine

Bio-neural systems make the ship fast, integrated, and strangely organic. They should be treated as a distributed nervous system, not a decorative technology noun. Gel-pack issues can affect isolated panels, replicators, transporters, holodecks, bridge stations, sensors, or shipwide processing depending on which grid is involved.

### Story use

Use gel packs when the scene needs:

- a failure that behaves less like a broken circuit and more like infection, contamination, fatigue, or cascade;
- a reason local maintenance affects multiple departments;
- a shipwide vulnerability that is advanced rather than primitive;
- a technical problem that makes medical, science, and engineering perspectives overlap.

### Avoid

Do not use gel packs as magic. They do not make the ship alive in a sentimental sense unless the campaign authors an anomaly. They are advanced biological-mechanical components with maintenance, failure modes, and supply constraints.

## 6.2 Variable-geometry nacelles

### System engine

The nacelles angle for warp, giving the class a visible mechanical transition. This is a signature external tell. A ship preparing for warp should not feel static; the nacelle posture is part of the movement language.

### Story use

Use nacelle geometry when the scene needs:

- an exterior sign of warp readiness;
- a reason pylon damage affects speed or subspace field integrity;
- a visual cue that a ship is alive with procedure before it moves;
- a way to make shuttle approach geometry specific, because the aft bay sits in visual relationship to the pylons.

### Avoid

Do not turn nacelle motion into a constant gimmick. Use it at warp transition, damage, inspection, landing/posture changes, and external approach scenes.

## 6.3 EMH and medical automation

### System engine

The Intrepid class carries Mark I EMH capability as a major 2370s medical support feature. In Directive terms, the EMH is a readiness multiplier and a continuity constraint: if medical staff are absent, incapacitated, overwhelmed, or quarantined, the ship still has an emergency medical actor, but it depends on sickbay emitters, computer state, medical database integrity, and command authorization context.

### Story use

Use EMH infrastructure when:

- sickbay casualties exceed ordinary staffing;
- a medical scene needs a sharp procedural second voice;
- the question is not "can the ship treat this" but "what does treatment require and who authorizes risk";
- holographic limits matter outside sickbay.

### Avoid

Do not make the EMH omnipresent outside authored emitter coverage. Do not import Voyager's Doctor character growth unless a campaign explicitly writes an EMH as a character.

## 6.4 Transporters

### System engine

Transporters compress mission transition into seconds while creating high trust in sensors, pattern integrity, biofilters, shields, and operator judgment.

### Story use

Transporters are strongest when they force decisions:

- beam through interference or wait;
- transport directly to sickbay or preserve quarantine;
- pull an unknown object aboard or isolate it in a cargo bay;
- risk a site-to-site extraction without a clean lock;
- hold a pattern while command debates an answer.

### Avoid

Do not use transporters to erase every logistical problem. Shields, interference, range, biohazards, power priorities, law, and ethics should matter.

## 6.5 Holodeck power and simulation

### System engine

Holodecks have power assumptions that can differ from the main grid. This lets them stay useful in some low-power stories but also makes them politically irritating: the crew can be suffering and still see a recreation system lit.

### Story use

Use holodeck power when a scene needs:

- a realistic reason recreation continues during scarcity;
- a debate about perceived fairness versus actual engineering constraints;
- a simulation needed for training or repair;
- a pressure valve for morale that command may not understand.

### Avoid

Do not make holodecks a limitless source of power, food, rooms, or plot escape.

## 6.6 Shields, phasers, torpedoes, and tractor beams

### System engine

The Intrepid class is not a pure warship, but it is a capable Starfleet vessel with phaser arrays, torpedo systems, shield grids, and tractor emitters. Tactical scenes should feel like a multi-mission ship under threat, not a battleship by default.

### Story use

Use tactical systems as spatial facts:

- aft arcs matter during shuttle recovery;
- forward deflector and torpedo geometry matter during approach;
- shield grids can fail by sector;
- tractor emitters can rescue, restrain, or complicate small-craft handling;
- torpedo inventory and special warheads should be campaign-owned, not casually invented.

### Avoid

Do not solve diplomatic or exploration scenes with weapons unless the campaign pressure supports it. Starfleet capability does not mean tactical dominance is the most interesting answer.

## 6.7 Replicators, stores, and compact logistics

### System engine

The ship is advanced but finite. Replicators, cargo stores, shuttle parts, medical supplies, gel-pack spares, and mission equipment define what generosity costs.

### Story use

Use logistics when:

- relief work must choose priorities;
- a department requests scarce parts;
- the mess hall social mood changes because food has become policy;
- a damaged shuttle consumes parts intended for a later mission;
- cargo bay inventory exposes a hidden agenda or past decision.

### Avoid

Do not turn replicators into universal plot erasers. Energy, pattern restrictions, medical ethics, classified materials, and rare components should create believable boundaries.

# 7. Movement and Scene Geography

## 7.1 Useful default routes

These routes are useful for narration and retrieval. They should not become repetitive, but they help the ship feel spatially consistent.

| Route | Typical scene use |
|---|---|
| Bridge to ready room | Public command to private judgment |
| Bridge to briefing room | Alert or report to senior-staff synthesis |
| Bridge to Deck 2 mess hall | Command leaves formality and enters crew reality |
| Bridge to Deck 5 sickbay | Command cost becomes medical fact |
| Bridge to Deck 10 shuttlebay | Mission launch or return becomes physical |
| Bridge to Deck 11 engineering | Command request becomes technical tradeoff |
| Engineering to Jefferies tube | Diagnosis becomes bodily risk |
| Transporter room to sickbay | Away-team transition to medical consequence |
| Cargo bay to shuttlebay | Supplies become mission mass |
| Astrometrics to bridge | Model becomes decision |

## 7.2 Scene transitions

A strong Intrepid-class scene transition gives at least one of:

- deck or zone change;
- sensory register change;
- duty-culture change;
- command authority change;
- physical constraint change.

Examples:

- From bridge to engineering: screen glow and command silence give way to core rhythm, tool noise, and people moving around the conversation.
- From mess hall to sickbay: informal voices give way to medical light and triage discipline.
- From shuttlebay to bridge: physical mass and hazard become status lights and decision language.
- From astrometrics to ready room: a vast map becomes one recommendation a commander must own.

# 8. Director Retrieval Guidance

## 8.1 Injection rule

When a ship area or system is relevant, inject compact ship facts in this order:

1. Hard anchor: deck, exterior placement, or system truth.
2. Physical texture: one or two details the narrator can use.
3. Operational pressure: what the area/system is for and what it costs.
4. Prohibition: only when a common wrong trope is likely.

Example for "shuttlebay":

```text
Ship anchor: Intrepid-class shuttlebay complex is aft on Deck 10 in the dorsal secondary hull. Shuttle approach is from astern toward the aft bay, not through the underside of the saucer. Shuttlebay 1 is the primary flight deck; the larger maintenance hangar/shuttlebay 2 sits behind it and can support repair or launch operations. Useful texture: approach lights, arresting field, bay force field, deck crew, maintenance cradles.
```

Example for "sickbay":

```text
Ship anchor: Sickbay is on Deck 5 with the main ward, three standard biobeds, surgical bed, CMO office, medical lab, and EMH support. Useful texture: bright medical light, ceiling sensor cluster over surgery, privacy pressure because command consequences are visible from the ward.
```

## 8.3 Over-injection guard

Do not inject every matching fact. If a player says "I go to the mess hall," the scene does not need the number of decks, the shuttlebay location, and the warp speed. It needs Deck 2 if routing matters, forward social-space texture if description matters, and maybe crew-morale pressure if the scene is social.

Use high detail when:

- the player is making a command decision about the ship;
- a generated response is likely to hallucinate a hard physical fact;
- a scene depends on exact location, route, or system behavior;
- a contradiction guard needs an explicit prohibition.

Use low detail when:

- the area is only a backdrop;
- character work is the scene's main payload;
- the same ship fact was already established in the current scene;
- the player does not need technical overhead to act.

# 9. Portrayal Principles

1. **The ship is a character only by consequence.** Do not make it sentimental by default. Make it feel alive through routines, damage, limitations, and crew knowledge.
2. **Competence is baseline.** Starfleet personnel know their work. Conflict comes from tradeoffs, incomplete information, pressure, politics, damage, and values.
3. **Physical truth beats generic drama.** If a shuttle docks, use the aft bay. If the warp core matters, use Deck 11. If sickbay matters, use Deck 5.
4. **Small scale is strength.** The Intrepid class should feel recurring and navigable. The same spaces should come back with accumulated meaning.
5. **Advanced systems create obligations.** Bio-neural packs, EMH, transporters, holodecks, and variable nacelles should create dependencies, not hand-wave them.
6. **Exterior and interior agree.** Windows, shuttlebay doors, deflector location, nacelles, and landing mechanisms should align with room descriptions.
7. **Damage has a location.** Avoid vague ship shaking when a specific deck, grid, support space, or system can carry the consequence.
8. **The ship has ordinary life.** Meals, maintenance, night watch, exercise, tool storage, private calls, and shift changes make crises matter.
9. **Voyager is reference, not inheritance.** Do not import Voyager-specific characters, events, Borg modifications, Delta Flyer history, or Delta Quadrant scarcity unless authored by the campaign.
10. **Runtime packets must be selective.** The bible is deep source; the dataset is compact truth; the prompt is situational.

# 10. Working Summary

The Intrepid class gives Directive a compact, advanced Starfleet environment where command decisions become physical consequences quickly.

The bridge turns shipwide activity into accountable command.

The ready room and briefing room convert urgency into private judgment and senior-staff synthesis.

The mess hall turns crew morale, ordinary life, and informal diplomacy into playable scenes.

Sickbay turns command cost into bodies, limits, and medical authority.

Transporter rooms turn mission transition into trust, identity risk, and biofilter judgment.

Astrometrics turns space into a model that can guide or mislead command.

Main engineering turns desire into technical debt, power tradeoffs, and repairable or irreversible risk.

The shuttlebay turns mission logistics into physical movement through the aft hull, with small craft approaching from astern rather than the saucer underside.

Jefferies tubes and lower-deck support spaces turn a sleek starship into a body with heat, cramped access, and unglamorous dependencies.

Bio-neural systems, variable nacelles, EMH support, transporters, holodecks, and landing systems are not trivia. They are the recurring ways an Intrepid-class ship makes story pressure specific.
