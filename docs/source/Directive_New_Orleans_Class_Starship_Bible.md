# Directive

## New Orleans-Class Starship Bible

**Document status:** Ship location and portrayal design baseline  
**Version:** 0.1  
**Campaign era:** Mid-2360s through 2370s baseline, adaptable by campaign  
**Primary campaign use:** New Orleans-class campaigns including the U.S.S. Aster Vale  
**Companion documents:** *Aster Vale / Unseen Border*, *Directive Intrepid-Class Starship Bible*, and *Character Bible Shaping Guide*

---

## Document Purpose

This document defines the New Orleans-class starship as a story environment rather than a list of technical trivia.

It should guide:

- Director decisions about which ship facts matter in a scene.
- Narrator descriptions of exterior approach, mission pods, internal movement, damage, patrol work, and routine frontier life.
- Ship Director and Mission Director packet construction.
- Future ship-area datasets, retrieval cards, and continuity facts.
- Contradiction guards for hard physical details, such as the class's Galaxy-family silhouette and mission/sensor pod logic.

This bible is the deep source. It should not be injected wholesale. Runtime packets should lift only the facts relevant to the current scene: one hard anchor, two sensory or operational textures, and one constraint or pressure.

The New Orleans-class baseline here is modeled from its canon and reference footprint: a mid-24th-century Starfleet frigate with Galaxy-era design language, smaller scale, phaser arrays, photon torpedoes, shields, and three prominent pods on the primary and secondary hulls. The U.S.S. Aster Vale is not the U.S.S. Kyushu or any other named reference ship. It shares class layout, mission-pod logic, and fleet-era assumptions, but its campaign owns its crew culture, frontier modifications, and command history.

# 1. Reference Grounding

## 1.1 Source posture

Use canon and reputable franchise references for hard class facts, then translate those facts into Directive-authored scene guidance. Do not copy episode dialogue, wiki prose, or reference-book prose into generated content. The useful layer for Directive is not "say what the Encyclopedia says"; it is "make the ship behave like a New Orleans-class frigate when a scene touches the ship."

Primary hard anchors for this bible:

- The New Orleans class is a Federation Starfleet frigate active by the 2360s and into the 2370s.
- The class is associated with diplomatic and tactical mission profiles.
- A New Orleans-class ship, the U.S.S. Kyushu, was present at Wolf 359 and destroyed there.
- Other named or associated ships include the U.S.S. Thomas Paine, U.S.S. Renegade, and U.S.S. Rutledge, though some associations are less direct on screen.
- The class shares design elements with the Galaxy class but is significantly smaller.
- The class carries at least three prominent pods along the primary and secondary hulls, treated here as mission/sensor pods unless a campaign overrides them.
- The class uses phaser arrays, photon torpedoes, and deflector shields.
- Reputable reconstruction work estimates the class around 340 meters long. Directive treats this as a useful scale baseline, not a canon deck plan.

Directive extrapolates interior decks, room locations, pod access, shuttle operations, crew complement, and support spaces because no complete canonical New Orleans deck plan exists. The goal is internal consistency, not pretending extrapolated details are canon.

## 1.2 Canon and adaptation boundary

Treat the following as hard class truth unless a package intentionally overrides it:

- The New Orleans class is not a Galaxy-class flagship. It shares design language, but at a smaller frigate scale.
- The three pods are class-defining exterior features. They should not be ignored in exterior descriptions or system damage.
- The class can plausibly handle diplomatic, tactical, patrol, and frontier mission profiles.
- The class predates the most advanced late-2370s designs. It should feel proven, adaptable, and mature rather than brand-new.

Treat the following as Directive-authored baseline choices:

- Deck count, area placements, and crew complement.
- The pods' default role: Directive treats the two dorsal saucer pods and one ventral engineering-hull pod as mission/sensor pods with tactical and survey utility, not shuttlebays by default.
- Main shuttlebay placement: Directive places the primary small-craft bay in the aft saucer/neck service zone, with final approach from astern and slightly above the engineering hull. It is not inside a sensor pod.
- Aster Vale's chart-validation systems, witness-protection modifications, and frontier patrol stores.

## 1.3 TNG, DS9, and VOY extrapolation posture

Use *The Next Generation* as the primary interior reference: Galaxy/Nebula-era Starfleet materials, bridge etiquette, observation-lounge culture, ready-room privacy, transporter professionalism, and sickbay authority. Scale everything down.

Use *Deep Space Nine* for frontier and war-adjacent pressure: Cardassian border memory, refugee and asylum logistics, Dominion War institutional fatigue, records custody, and the way a frigate can become the only credible authority in a disputed region.

Use *Voyager* for compact autonomy and charting practice: a smaller crew sustaining long route work, science and command arguing over partial data, and a ship's ordinary rooms accumulating emotional weight.

# 2. Class Personality

## 2.1 At a glance

| Field | Directive baseline |
|---|---|
| Class role | Mid-24th-century Starfleet frigate for patrol, diplomacy, tactical support, and mission-pod survey work |
| Story scale | Smaller than Galaxy grandeur; larger and more institutional than a scout |
| Visual identity | Galaxy-family saucer and engineering lineage, shortened neck, three mission/sensor pods |
| Social identity | Mature, procedural, frontier-capable, records-conscious |
| Dramatic engine | Old Starfleet ideals under border ambiguity |
| Central contradiction | Built from familiar flagship language, but tasked with messy frontier decisions where no flagship answer exists |
| Default tone | Capable, slightly older, modular, principled, work-worn |
| Avoid | Treating it as a Galaxy-class reskin, ignoring pods, making pods magic do-everything modules, Intrepid-style compact modernity |

## 2.2 Core engine

A New Orleans turns uncertainty into a record that command can stand behind.

It is a frigate in the Starfleet sense: capable enough to fight, formal enough to host diplomacy, small enough to patrol disputed space for long stretches, and modular enough to bring specialized sensors or mission equipment into a problem area. It is not there to awe a region. It is there to verify, escort, mediate, and hold the line until a messy fact can become accountable policy.

In scene terms, the New Orleans class rewards commanders who understand the difference between seeing, recording, publishing, and acting. The ship can detect a hidden route, identify a false border marker, prove that a colony existed, or validate a protected witness. The hard question is what Starfleet is allowed to do once the ship knows.

## 2.3 Central contradiction

The ship looks familiar enough to imply certainty and old Starfleet order. Its best stories take place where certainty is exactly what everyone is fighting over.

The Galaxy-family silhouette promises institutions, procedure, diplomacy, and competence. The pods promise specialized perception. The frigate scale promises mobility and independence. Put together, the New Orleans class is a ship that can make hidden things visible. That is useful, dangerous, and never neutral.

This contradiction should shape narration. A New Orleans can host a calm briefing under warm Starfleet light while three pods outside are collecting data that may erase a sanctuary, restore a legal route, expose a war crime, or trigger a border incident.

## 2.4 Storytelling values

**Procedural visibility.** The class should care about how facts become official. Logs, charts, sensor raw data, custody stamps, and witness protection can all matter.

**Modular perception.** The mission pods are not decoration. They give the ship a class-specific way to see, verify, and misread.

**Scaled-down Starfleet dignity.** Rooms can echo Galaxy-era ideals without being huge. The ship carries diplomatic seriousness in a smaller hull.

**Frontier accountability.** A New Orleans should often face the consequences of what it records: routes opened, asylum denied, lies exposed, evidence protected, or hidden communities endangered.

**Recoverable age.** It is not obsolete, but it has service history. Repairs, refits, older conduits, pod calibration quirks, and inherited assumptions are playable.

# 3. Exterior Bible

## 3.1 Silhouette and first read

A New Orleans reads as a smaller cousin of the Galaxy era. It has a familiar saucer, compact engineering hull, nacelles on custom pylons, and an overall Starfleet silhouette that says "official" before it says "new." The three pods break that familiarity: two ride above the saucer and one sits below the secondary hull. They make the ship look like it carries extra perception, extra bite, or extra responsibility.

For narration, describe the exterior through operational perspective:

- A shuttle pilot sees an aft saucer/neck approach, the engineering hull beneath, nacelles outboard, and sensor pods above the saucer like quiet weight.
- A border outpost sees a familiar Starfleet saucer with unfamiliar pod silhouettes and knows the ship can record more than it says aloud.
- A tactical observer sees phaser strips, torpedo arcs, and pods that may be sensors, mission hardware, or tactical support.
- A refugee or protected witness sees an older, official-looking ship that may represent safety, exposure, or bureaucracy.

## 3.2 Exterior zones

| Exterior zone | Story function | Scene detail |
|---|---|---|
| Saucer crown and bridge module | Starfleet legitimacy and command visibility | Familiar bridge shape, dorsal lights, old institutional confidence |
| Dorsal mission pods | Survey, tactical, or specialized perception | Pod calibration lights, protective shutters, maintenance seams, sensor-sweep glow |
| Aft saucer/neck service zone | Small-craft operations | Main shuttlebay doors, approach strobes, shuttle path over the engineering hull |
| Engineering hull | Power, deflector, lower mission support | Deflector glow forward, ventral pod shadow, phaser strips, service hatches |
| Ventral mission pod | Specialized field work and vulnerability | Underside sensor or tactical equipment, inspection clamps, pod damage consequences |
| Warp nacelles and pylons | Mature Galaxy-era propulsion lineage | Long nacelle glow, pylon stress, service age, reliable warp presence |
| Lateral saucer edge | Crew, labs, and escape systems | Windows, lifeboat panels, phaser arrays, evidence of scale smaller than Galaxy |

## 3.3 Shuttle approach truth

Directive baseline: New Orleans small-craft operations use an aft saucer/neck service bay, approached from astern and slightly above the engineering hull. The bay is smaller than a Galaxy-class main shuttlebay and more procedural than a ceremonial hangar.

Correct approach shape:

- The shuttle falls in behind the ship and aligns with the aft saucer/neck doors.
- Approach lights frame the bay between saucer curve, shortened neck, and engineering hull.
- Traffic control keeps the mission pods' active sensor sweeps and tractor fields out of the approach solution.
- The final approach feels official and controlled, not improvised.

Incorrect approach shape:

- A shuttle entering one of the mission pods by default.
- A massive Galaxy-class dorsal flight deck scaled up beyond the frigate.
- A forward saucer mouth.
- A ventral belly hangar that ignores the engineering hull and pod geometry.

If prose needs a dramatic external view, use the familiar saucer and unfamiliar pods together: the shuttle crossing under dorsal pod shadows, bay doors opening in the aft saucer line, and sensor-pod sweeps dimming as the approach window clears.

## 3.4 Mission pod truth

Directive baseline treats the pods as modular mission/sensor systems, not magic containers.

Use pods for:

- long-baseline sensor triangulation;
- chart validation;
- border-marker verification;
- tactical fire-control support;
- probe telemetry;
- protected-evidence scans;
- specialized mission packages installed before deployment.

Do not use pods for:

- infinite cargo capacity;
- routine crew quarters;
- surprise shuttlebays unless a campaign explicitly authors a refit;
- every specialized system at once;
- detachable escape craft by default.

Pod scenes are strongest when they create both ability and obligation. If the dorsal port pod can detect a hidden route, who is authorized to read the raw data? If the ventral pod is damaged, which blind spot appears? If the pod calibration proves one faction lied, does Starfleet publish the proof?

# 4. Interior Design Language

## 4.1 Overall feel

The interior is Galaxy-era Starfleet reduced to frigate scale. Corridors are clean, warm, and curved, but shorter. Rooms have dignity without excess. The ship has proper command spaces, sickbay, transporter rooms, science labs, cargo areas, and crew commons, but it cannot hide a whole society inside itself.

A New Orleans should feel older than an Intrepid or Steamrunner and less improvised than a Defiant. It has institutional habits: labeled evidence lockers, observation-room minutes, transporter logs, pod-calibration rituals, and officers who care whether a record will survive review.

## 4.2 Directive deck baseline

This is a runtime consistency baseline, not canonical deck evidence.

| Deck or zone | Baseline use |
|---|---|
| Deck 1 | Bridge and command interface |
| Deck 2 | Ready room, observation/briefing room, command support |
| Deck 3 | Senior quarters, diplomatic cabin, captain's office overflow |
| Decks 4-5 | Wardroom, crew commons, sickbay, medical lab |
| Deck 6 | Transporter rooms, security office, armory, brig |
| Decks 7-8 | Science labs, route records, dorsal mission-pod access trunks |
| Deck 9 | Mission ops, chart validation, pod-control room |
| Decks 10-11 | Main engineering, deflector control, lower mission support |
| Deck 12 | Cargo bays, relief stores, modular field equipment |
| Deck 13 | Aft saucer/neck shuttlebay and small-craft maintenance |
| Deck 14 | Lower support, ventral pod access, environmental, hull maintenance |

Campaigns may override names and refit details, but runtime narration should keep the class's Galaxy-family dignity, frigate compactness, pod access logic, and aft saucer small-craft placement.

## 4.3 Sensory palette

Use sensory detail as operational information.

| Sense | Baseline detail | Under pressure |
|---|---|---|
| Sound | Warmer LCARS tones, turbolift hum, pod-calibration chimes, low engineering rhythm | Pod alarms, old relay buzz, emergency seal thuds, briefing-room channel overlap |
| Light | Warm 2360s Starfleet light, amber pod displays, blue-white chart tables | Red alert wash, pod-status strobes, backup lamps, deflector glare through engineering panels |
| Touch | Smooth older consoles, thicker handrails, carpeted command decks, warm access panels | Vibration from pod sweeps, hot EPS trunks, deck shudder near pylon stress |
| Smell | Neutral air, replicator coffee, medical antiseptic, older insulation | Ozone, scorched isolinear boards, cargo dust, disinfectant after refugee intake |
| Movement | Formal bridge-to-briefing routes, lift rides through saucer, pod-access climbs | Rerouted lifts, sealed pod trunks, crowding around evidence lockers, cargo-lift delays |

## 4.4 Room-to-hull logic

The New Orleans hull has a recognizable body. Forward and dorsal command rooms should align with the saucer. Pod-control and mission ops should have physical or data-route relationships to the three pods. Engineering should feel closer to the compact secondary hull. The shuttlebay should be aft saucer/neck, not in a pod by default.

This rule matters because the class's story power comes from physical visibility. If a pod sees something, a room aboard should know which pod, what line of sight, what calibration, and what custody chain produced that knowledge.

# 5. Key Area Profiles

Each area profile uses the character-bible idea of engine, contradiction, tells, and reveal pressure, adapted for locations.

## 5.1 Bridge, Deck 1

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Deck 1, Galaxy-family bridge module scaled to frigate size |
| Function | Command, patrol decisions, pod-tasking, tactical coordination |
| Scene use | Acting command, border calls, evidence authorization, route clearance |
| Avoid | Galaxy-class luxury, Intrepid bridge geometry, pure tactical bunker tone |

### Location engine

The bridge turns what the ship sees into what Starfleet will do about it.

### Central contradiction

The room looks like old institutional confidence, but the best New Orleans scenes happen when the institution does not yet know the right answer.

### Physical identity

Use central command chairs, forward conn/ops, tactical and mission stations, aft displays for pod status and route/evidence overlays, and side consoles for communications or damage control. The bridge should be formal enough for diplomatic calls and compact enough for everyone to hear an uncomfortable report.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, pod calls interrupt command rhythm. The dorsal pod sees one thing, the ventral pod sees another, and the bridge must decide whether contradictory data becomes action, caution, or classified evidence.

### Scene shapes

- A hidden settlement appears on pod telemetry during a routine patrol.
- Tactical requests authorization while science argues the contact might be civilian.
- A legal order arrives before the ship has finished verifying the facts.

## 5.2 Ready room and observation room, Deck 2

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Adjacent to or directly below bridge |
| Function | Private command judgment and formal senior-staff synthesis |
| Scene use | Inquiry, witness protection, border policy, command transition |

### Location engine

These rooms turn discovered facts into accountable records.

### Physical identity

The ready room is smaller than a flagship captain's office but carries the same institutional grammar: desk, viewport or display, replicator, secure terminal, and personal artifacts. The observation room has a long table, wall display, and enough privacy to debate classified evidence.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, the observation room becomes a courtroom without robes. People argue not only what happened, but what the ship's log will say happened.

### Scene shapes

- A senior officer asks whether publishing a chart will protect or doom a sanctuary.
- A witness trusts the ship but not Starfleet's records.
- The player signs a log entry knowing it may survive longer than the mission.

## 5.3 Wardroom, mess, and diplomatic cabin, Decks 3-5

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Forward or mid-saucer social spaces |
| Function | Meals, morale, informal diplomacy, protected civilian meetings |
| Scene use | Trust, asylum, crew fatigue, witness conversations |

### Location engine

The wardroom turns formal Starfleet procedure into personal stakes.

### Central contradiction

It is the most humane room on a ship whose sensors can make people visible against their will.

### Physical identity

Use compact tables, replicator alcoves, older display panels, wall art or mission plaques, and a few compartments that can serve as diplomatic waiting rooms or protected-civilian spaces. It should feel less grand than Ten Forward and more official than a lower-deck mess.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, social space becomes triage for trust. Refugees, witnesses, local officials, and tired officers occupy the same finite room.

### Scene shapes

- A protected witness asks whether the pod data will be sealed.
- A local negotiator refuses the briefing room but accepts a wardroom meal.
- Crew morale cracks over whether the ship is helping or exposing people.

## 5.4 Sickbay, Decks 4-5

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Protected mid-saucer medical suite |
| Function | Treatment, forensic medicine, asylum medical exams, casualty care |
| Scene use | Body evidence, trauma, privacy, command cost |

### Location engine

Sickbay turns disputed facts into lived bodies.

### Central contradiction

Medical truth can protect a person and endanger them. A scan may prove someone survived, where they came from, or what was done to them.

### Physical identity

Use a main ward, surgical alcove, CMO office, medical lab, privacy screens, forensic evidence storage, and isolation capability. Keep it smaller than Galaxy sickbay but more formal than a scout's medical compartment.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, the doctor becomes a custodian of both care and evidence. Medical logs may need sealing, redaction, or command-level protection.

### Scene shapes

- A patient's injury proves a border crossing that a government denies.
- The doctor refuses to release a medical file to a local authority.
- A mass-casualty drill becomes real while the ship is still in a legal standoff.

### Location engine

Transporters move people. Security makes movement legible and accountable.

### Physical identity

Use standard multi-pad transporter rooms, secure operator booths, equipment lockers, interview room, compact brig, arms locker, and evidence intake. The space should have logs, seals, and procedures that matter.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, transporter status becomes political. Beaming someone aboard can be asylum, kidnapping, rescue, extradition refusal, or evidence contamination.

### Scene shapes

- A civilian requests emergency beam-out from a disputed jurisdiction.
- Security must keep a witness hidden from both guests and crew gossip.
- A transporter trace contradicts an official route record.

Mission ops is where the New Orleans turns perception into institutional risk.

### Central contradiction

# 6. Ship Systems as Story Pressure

## 6.1 Mission/sensor pods

### System engine

The pods extend what the ship can perceive and verify. They are the class's signature story system.

### Story use

Use pods when:

- a route, colony, beacon, or border marker must be verified;
- multiple sensor angles produce conflicting truth;
- a pod's power or calibration status changes what command can prove;
- a local actor fears what the ship can see.

### Avoid

Do not make pods do everything. A pod should have a current loadout, calibration, arc, maintenance state, and limitation.

## 6.2 Records, charts, and evidence custody

### System engine

New Orleans-class missions often turn on official records: what the ship knows, what it logs, who can read it, and when it becomes public.

### Story use

Use custody systems when:

- a chart exposes a hidden community;
- a medical or transporter log protects or endangers someone;
- an evidence package must survive political pressure;
- a commander must choose between secrecy and accountability.

### Avoid

Do not let records become invisible admin. Logs are story objects.

## 6.3 Tactical systems

### System engine

Phaser arrays and photon torpedoes make the frigate credible in patrol and fleet scenes.

### Story use

Use tactical systems for border warning, convoy cover, self-defense, and pressure against armed local actors. The class can fight, but it should rarely solve a political map by force.

### Avoid

Do not turn the ship into a battleship. It is a frigate with tactical authority, not a war-ending platform.

## 6.4 Older-but-proven power grid

### System engine

The ship's maturity is a strength and a risk. Systems are understood, repairable, and trusted, but modifications and pod loads create tradeoffs.

### Story use

Use power-grid pressure when:

- pod operation competes with shields, transporters, or warp;
- old conduits behave differently after refit;
- a sensor upgrade creates maintenance debt;
- a field repair requires knowledge of the ship's service history.

### Avoid

Do not make age equal incompetence. The ship is proven, not shabby.

# 7. Movement and Scene Geography

## 7.1 Useful default routes

| Route | Typical scene use |
|---|---|
| Bridge to observation room | Public command to formal consensus |
| Bridge to ready room | Order to private responsibility |
| Bridge to mission ops | Contact or route becomes evidence |
| Mission ops to pod access | Data becomes maintenance and calibration |
| Mission ops to security | Record becomes custody |
| Sickbay to security | Medical truth becomes protected evidence |
| Transporter room to brig | Movement becomes lawful detention |
| Cargo to shuttlebay | Object becomes passage |
| Engineering to ventral pod | Power tradeoff becomes physical risk |
| Wardroom to observation room | Personal trust becomes official policy |

## 7.2 Scene transitions

A strong New Orleans transition gives at least one of:

- a movement from familiar Starfleet formality to frontier ambiguity;
- a shift between public map and sealed data;
- a pod status change;
- a custody change;
- a reminder that the ship is smaller than its institutional confidence.

Examples:

- From bridge to mission ops: a clean contact report becomes a wall of competing pod returns.
- From wardroom to observation room: a personal promise becomes language that must survive review.
- From pod access to bridge: a cramped repair becomes command authority over what can be known.
- From shuttlebay to security: arrival becomes logged custody.

# 8. Director Retrieval Guidance

## 8.1 Injection rule

When a New Orleans area or system is relevant, inject compact ship facts in this order:

1. Hard anchor: class geometry, deck/zone, pod placement, exterior placement, or system truth.
2. Physical texture: one or two details the narrator can use.
3. Operational pressure: what the area/system is for and what it costs.
4. Prohibition: only when a common wrong trope is likely.

Example for "pod":

```text
Ship anchor: New Orleans-class vessels carry two dorsal saucer mission/sensor pods and one ventral secondary-hull pod. Directive treats them as specialized sensor/mission systems, not shuttlebays or infinite cargo modules by default. Use pod calibration, arc, power, custody, and maintenance limits when they matter.
```

Example for "shuttlebay":

```text
Ship anchor: Directive New Orleans baseline places the primary small-craft bay in the aft saucer/neck service zone, approached from astern and slightly above the engineering hull. Shuttles do not routinely enter the mission pods.
```

## 8.2 Keyword map

| Trigger | Inject |

## 8.3 Over-injection guard

Do not inject every matching fact. If a player says "I enter the wardroom," the scene does not need the full pod map unless the conversation is about charts, hidden routes, or evidence. It needs the room's social function and perhaps the ship's formal frontier culture.

Use high detail when:

- a scene touches pods, chart custody, asylum, legal records, or border evidence;
- a generated response is likely to put a shuttle into a mission pod;
- a command decision depends on what the ship can prove;
- contradiction guards need an explicit prohibition.

Use low detail when:

- the area is only a backdrop;
- character work is the scene's main payload;
- the same ship fact was just established;
- the player does not need technical overhead to act.

# 9. Portrayal Principles

1. **The ship makes visibility consequential.** New Orleans scenes should care about what is seen, logged, sealed, published, and acted upon.
2. **The pods matter.** They are class-defining mission/sensor systems, not decoration.
3. **Frigate scale matters.** The ship is capable and formal, but not a Galaxy-class city.
4. **Old Starfleet is not naive.** The class carries institutional ideals into regions where ideals require judgment.
5. **Evidence has custody.** Logs, charts, medical files, transporter traces, and pod data should have access rules.
6. **Tactical authority is bounded.** The ship can fight and patrol, but the strongest stories turn on proof, passage, and legitimacy.
7. **Damage has a pod, deck, or system.** Avoid generic shaking when a dorsal pod, ventral pod, pylon, deflector, or record system can carry consequence.
8. **Campaign ships own their modifications.** Aster Vale may specialize in chart validation and protected visibility, but class truth remains the common frame.
9. **Do not import another class's deck map.** Intrepid, Galaxy, Steamrunner, and Norway assumptions must not overwrite New Orleans geometry.
10. **Runtime packets must be selective.** The bible is deep source; future datasets should distill only relevant scene facts.
