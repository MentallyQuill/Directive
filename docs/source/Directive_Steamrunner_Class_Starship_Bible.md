# Directive

## Steamrunner-Class Starship Bible

**Document status:** Ship location and portrayal design baseline  
**Version:** 0.1  
**Campaign era:** 2370s baseline, adaptable by campaign  
**Primary campaign use:** Steamrunner-class campaigns including the U.S.S. Glass Harbor and U.S.S. Serein  
**Companion documents:** *Glass Harbor / Drowned Constellation*, *Serein / Black Current*, *Directive Intrepid-Class Starship Bible*, and *Character Bible Shaping Guide*

---

## Document Purpose

This document defines the Steamrunner-class starship as a story environment rather than a list of ship trivia.

It should guide:

- Director decisions about which ship facts matter in a scene.
- Narrator descriptions of exterior approach, internal movement, damage, repair, convoy work, rescue staging, and routine life.
- Ship Director and Mission Director packet construction.
- Future ship-area datasets, retrieval cards, and continuity facts.
- Contradiction guards for hard physical details, such as the class's aft deflector and non-Galaxy hull geometry.

This bible is the deep source. It should not be injected wholesale. Runtime packets should lift only the facts relevant to the current scene: one hard anchor, two sensory or operational textures, and one constraint or pressure.

The Steamrunner-class baseline here is modeled from its 2370s screen role: a compact, unconventional Starfleet vessel common in fleet deployments, Borg defense, Dominion War operations, and postwar recovery. The U.S.S. Glass Harbor and U.S.S. Serein are not the same ship. They share class layout, hull logic, and operational assumptions, but each campaign owns its crew culture, damage history, regional modifications, and shipboard nicknames.

# 1. Reference Grounding

## 1.1 Source posture

Use canon and reputable franchise references for hard class facts, then translate those facts into Directive-authored scene guidance. Do not copy episode dialogue, wiki prose, or reference-book prose into generated content. The useful layer for Directive is not "say what a reference page says"; it is "make the ship behave like a Steamrunner-class vessel when a scene touches the ship."

Primary hard anchors for this bible:

- The Steamrunner class is a Federation Starfleet class active by the early 2370s.
- It appears in late-24th-century fleet contexts: Utopia Planitia, the Battle of Sector 001, Dominion War deployments around Deep Space 9, and Earth defense against a Borg sphere in 2378.
- The class carries phaser arrays, photon torpedoes, and deflector shields typical of the period.
- The hull is visually unconventional: a near-oval or irregular saucer-like primary hull, nacelles partially embedded or closely cowled into the saucer, and a detached secondary-hull/deflector housing at the aft end.
- The navigational deflector is aft, on the detached secondary hull rather than in the forward engineering hull position used by many other Starfleet ships.
- The bridge module is inset between the forward Bussard collector/cowling structures rather than perched on a broad Galaxy-style saucer crown.
- Reference size estimates place the class around 356 meters long. Directive treats this as a useful scale baseline, not a canon deck plan.

Directive extrapolates interior decks, room locations, shuttle operations, crew complement, and support spaces because no complete canonical Steamrunner deck plan exists. The goal is internal consistency, not pretending that extrapolated details are canon.

## 1.2 Canon and adaptation boundary

Treat the following as hard class truth unless a package intentionally overrides it:

- The Steamrunner is not a Galaxy-style saucer plus neck plus forward engineering hull. It is a flatter, integrated, aft-deflector design.
- The aft deflector housing matters. Do not place the main deflector on the bow.
- The ship is smaller and denser than a Galaxy or Sovereign class but longer and more capable than a short-range scout.
- Fleet duty is part of the class identity. It can serve in patrol, escort, recovery, battle-line, convoy, and route-security scenes without feeling out of role.
- The class is visually tactical without being a pure warship. It should carry Starfleet science, medical, diplomatic, and rescue capability.

Treat the following as Directive-authored baseline choices:

- Deck count, area placements, and crew complement.
- The main flight deck placement: Directive places Steamrunner small-craft operations in an aft-lower service volume reached from astern and below, recessed into the broad rear saucer/service hull above and around the detached deflector housing. It is not a forward saucer mouth.
- Ship-specific mission labs, recovery bays, chart rooms, ordnance lockers, and refugee accommodations.
- Whether a specific Steamrunner has wartime armor, postwar sensor pallets, towing refits, field hospital modules, or extra cargo cradles.

## 1.3 TNG, DS9, and VOY extrapolation posture

Use *The Next Generation* for late-2360s Starfleet room language: bridge discipline, ready-room privacy, observation-lounge synthesis, transporter procedure, sickbay authority, and clean multi-use corridors.

Use *Deep Space Nine* for fleet pressure: war convoys, station-adjacent repairs, escort formations, casualty routing, ordnance caution, recovery law, and the political cost of Starfleet ships arriving with tactical silhouettes.

Use *Voyager* for 2370s compact-starship practice: efficient room reuse, close crew rhythms, damage-control improvisation, shuttlebay work as a physical scene, and operational dependency between science, engineering, medicine, and command.

# 2. Class Personality

## 2.1 At a glance

| Field | Directive baseline |
|---|---|
| Class role | Compact fleet escort, tactical survey, route-security, recovery, and response vessel |
| Story scale | Large enough for real departments and small-craft logistics; tight enough for recurring workspaces |
| Visual identity | Flattened irregular saucer, cowled nacelles, inset bridge, detached aft deflector housing |
| Social identity | Workmanlike, alert, convoy-aware, professionally crowded |
| Dramatic engine | Starfleet competence under contested-route pressure |
| Central contradiction | Built to look ready for battle, but most useful when it keeps people moving, found, supplied, and alive |
| Default tone | Capable, kinetic, compact, practical, alert without swagger |
| Avoid | Galaxy-class grandeur, Intrepid-like aft deck map, Defiant-style bunker minimalism, generic forward deflector shots, saucer-underbelly shuttle mouths |

## 2.2 Core engine

A Steamrunner turns a dangerous route into a working problem.

It is not a palace, city, or ceremonial flagship. It is the ship Starfleet sends when a region needs escort, sensor coverage, convoy judgment, salvage security, shuttle coordination, tactical credibility, and enough science to make uncertainty actionable. It can fight, but its best scenes are about making movement possible: clearing lanes, holding a perimeter, recovering survivors, verifying charts, towing damaged hulls, and deciding which risk is allowed to enter a convoy.

In scene terms, the Steamrunner class rewards officers who think in arcs, windows, custody, clearance, and timing. Can the ship keep shields angled while a shuttle comes home? Can it tow one wreck without losing tractor coverage on another? Can it prove a route safe enough for civilian traffic without turning the region into a Starfleet checkpoint?

## 2.3 Central contradiction

The ship looks like a weapon from the outside and acts like infrastructure when it is doing its best work.

The flattened hull, embedded nacelles, aft deflector housing, and fleet-war history make the class read as blunt and tactical. But in Directive play, the Steamrunner is strongest as a mobile public utility under stress. It keeps routes honest, makes recovery legal, gives civilians a known place to come home to, and turns raw sensor data into permission for people to move.

This contradiction should shape narration. A Steamrunner can arrive with shield grids bright and torpedo tubes ready, then spend the next three sessions adjudicating salvage custody, patching a trawler's life support, and holding a grieving family in a mess hall because the ship is the only stable authority within four light-years.

## 2.4 Storytelling values

**Kinetic competence.** Steamrunner scenes should move. People cross decks with cases, tractor teams update load estimates, shuttle crews argue over launch windows, and tactical officers think about arcs while science officers think about false positives.

**Route morality.** The class is excellent for stories about who gets safe passage, whose charts are trusted, which wrecks can be touched, and whether protection becomes control.

**Compact departments.** A Steamrunner has enough departments for ensemble play, but not enough room for anonymity. If recovery operations run for days, the same engineers, pilots, doctors, and security officers keep reappearing.

**Unconventional hull truth.** The ship's body is not generic. Aft deflector placement, cowled nacelles, and the broad flattened hull should affect exterior shots, damage reports, and shuttle approaches.

**Recoverable wear.** The ship should take specific operational damage: tractor emitter stress, aft deflector restrictions, cowling heat, sensor blind spots, cargo-door misalignment, shield-grid gaps, and shuttle-deck overuse.

# 3. Exterior Bible

## 3.1 Silhouette and first read

A Steamrunner reads as a compact slab of Starfleet intent. The hull is wide, flattened, and irregular rather than a clean circular saucer. The nacelles sit close to, or partially within, the primary hull through heavy cowlings. The bridge is visually tucked into the forward structure rather than raised as a proud crown. The deflector sits aft in a detached housing between the rear structures, making the class immediately different from most familiar Starfleet silhouettes.

For narration, describe the exterior through operational perspective:

- A shuttle pilot sees the wide rear hull, cowled nacelles, aft deflector hazard zone, recovery strobes, and a recessed flight-deck approach rather than a broad saucer belly.
- A convoy captain sees a low, hard Starfleet profile crossing a route ahead of them, shield arcs becoming visible as the ship takes station.
- A salvage team sees tractor lock emitters, aft service lights, cargo doors, and deflector restrictions that determine whether a wreck can be moved.
- A diplomatic arrival sees a vessel whose tactical posture may reassure allies and alarm communities that fear seizure.

## 3.2 Exterior zones

| Exterior zone | Story function | Scene detail |
|---|---|---|
| Forward cowling and inset bridge | Command identity and fast-read vulnerability | Bridge tucked between forward structures; alert lights reflected along the cowlings |
| Broad primary hull | Crew, labs, mission work, shield grid | Long window runs, phaser strips, access hatches, sensor pallets, scars from patrol work |
| Cowled nacelle roots | Warp identity and thermal vulnerability | Bussard glow close to inhabited hull; cowling heat and pylon stress visible during maneuvers |
| Aft detached deflector housing | Navigation, field work, hazard pressure | Deflector pulses from the rear; aft field arcs matter for towing and shuttle recovery |
| Aft-lower flight-deck recess | Small-craft launch and recovery | Approach lights, bay force field, traffic-control strobes, shuttle silhouettes against the rear hull |
| Ventral service panels | Cargo, repair, and recovery work | Cargo locks, tractor maintenance panels, emergency umbilicals, hull clamps |
| Lateral shield and phaser arcs | Convoy defense | Shield shimmer over the flattened hull; phaser arrays sweeping wide approach lanes |

## 3.3 Shuttle approach truth

Directive baseline: Steamrunner small-craft operations use an aft-lower service volume in the broad rear hull, with final approach from astern and below. The flight deck is recessed and operationally tied to cargo handling, recovery triage, and aft tractor coverage.

Correct approach shape:

- The shuttle falls behind and below the Steamrunner, then aligns with aft-lower approach lights.
- Flight control watches the aft deflector status because deflector activity and shuttle recovery share the ship's stern geometry.
- Tractor guidance may catch the shuttle late, especially during combat or storm conditions.
- The bay force field and cargo locks become part of the same physical scene when the ship is staging rescue or salvage.

Incorrect approach shape:

- A wide forward mouth in the primary hull.
- A Galaxy-style main shuttlebay across the dorsal saucer crown.
- A routine shuttle door placed directly in the active deflector face.
- A generic belly hangar with no relationship to aft field arcs or the flattened hull.

If prose needs a dramatic external view, use stern geometry: deflector glow dimming to permit recovery, shield edges dropping around the bay, cowled nacelles framing the shuttle's line, and the flight deck crew visible behind force-field shimmer.

## 3.4 Fleet, convoy, and recovery posture

A Steamrunner is visually at home beside civilian traffic, damaged freighters, and war-line formations. It should rarely enter a region as a purely scenic object. Its arrival changes movement rights.

Useful exterior postures:

- **Escort station:** off a convoy's flank, keeping sensors and phasers on the route edge.
- **Recovery hover:** bow angled away while aft tractors, cargo locks, and shuttle deck handle survivors or wreckage.
- **Barrier posture:** shields extended or angled while smaller craft pass through a controlled corridor.
- **Survey sprint:** low warp or impulse sweeps along a route while probes and shuttle teams check hazards.
- **Battle-line hold:** compact silhouette near larger ships, taking a defined tactical arc rather than dominating the whole field.

# 4. Interior Design Language

## 4.1 Overall feel

The Steamrunner interior is late-24th-century Starfleet with a practical, fleet-service edge. It should feel clean and professional, but not expansive. Corridors are efficient, workspaces are close to their support rooms, and the ship's traffic patterns favor fast transitions between bridge, mission ops, shuttlebay, cargo, sickbay, and engineering.

Compared with an Intrepid-class ship, the Steamrunner should feel broader and flatter. Compared with a Galaxy-class ship, it should feel less luxurious and less socially spacious. Compared with a Defiant-class ship, it should still feel like a full Starfleet vessel with science labs, diplomacy rooms, medical care, transporter procedure, and ordinary crew life.

## 4.2 Directive deck baseline

This is a runtime consistency baseline, not canonical deck evidence.

| Deck or zone | Baseline use |
|---|---|
| Deck 1 | Inset bridge, ready room, command support |
| Deck 2 | Briefing room, command offices, senior watch support |
| Decks 3-4 | Senior quarters, crew commons, wardroom, department offices |
| Deck 5 | Sickbay, medical lab, quarantine rooms |
| Deck 6 | Transporter rooms, security office, armory, brig |
| Decks 7-8 | Science labs, route-analysis rooms, mission ops, chart custody |
| Decks 9-10 | Cargo handling, relief staging, salvage processing, field equipment |
| Decks 11-12 | Main engineering, power transfer, warp support, aft deflector control |
| Deck 13 | Aft-lower flight deck and small-craft maintenance |
| Decks 14-16 | Lower mechanical, environmental, hull-access, and maintenance spaces |

Campaigns may override room names and refit details, but runtime narration should keep the class's aft service concentration, compact traffic, and broad flattened hull logic.

## 4.3 Sensory palette

Use sensory detail as operational information.

| Sense | Baseline detail | Under pressure |
|---|---|---|
| Sound | Shorter corridor reverberation, LCARS chirps, distant tractor relays, shuttle-deck calls | Tractor load alarms, cargo-lock impacts, aft deflector cycling, shield harmonics |
| Light | Working Starfleet light, amber cargo bays, blue-white sensor displays, flight-deck strobes | Red alert strips, bay force-field shimmer, emergency worklamps, shield flicker through windows |
| Touch | Broad deck plates, handrails near cargo and flight spaces, warm panels near nacelle cowlings | Vibration through the flattened hull, hot cowling access, static from stressed tractor systems |
| Smell | Recycled air, cargo straps, coolant, replicator food, shuttle lubricants | Scorched insulation, antiseptic triage, burned deuterium residue, wet evacuation blankets |
| Movement | Fast bridge-to-mission-ops routes, short turbolift rides, cargo lifts, bay gantries | Rerouted lifts, sealed aft sections, crowded triage corridors, Jefferies-tube crawls |

## 4.4 Room-to-hull logic

The Steamrunner hull is broad and low. Rooms should respect that body.

Forward command spaces and high-priority sensor rooms should look over or into the forward cowling geometry. Aft service rooms should be physically near deflector control, cargo, tractor, and flight operations. The ship should not suddenly grow a tall hotel-like saucer, a forward engineering neck, or a grand dorsal main shuttlebay unless a specific refit document authorizes it.

# 5. Key Area Profiles

Each area profile uses the character-bible idea of engine, contradiction, tells, and reveal pressure, adapted for locations.

## 5.1 Bridge, Deck 1

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Deck 1, inset forward command module between cowling structures |
| Function | Command, tactical coordination, route decisions, fleet communication |
| Scene use | Acting-command pressure, convoy clearance, recovery prioritization, battle arcs |
| Avoid | Vast flagship bridge, cramped Defiant bunker, Intrepid bridge layout by default |

### Location engine

The Steamrunner bridge turns contested movement into accountable command. It is where route maps, tactical arcs, shuttle windows, tractor loads, and civilian demands become one order.

### Central contradiction

The bridge feels protected because it is inset into the hull, but it is emotionally exposed. Everyone can see when a commander chooses which ship gets the tractor beam, which route remains closed, or which signal is trusted.

### Physical identity

Use a compact late-2370s Starfleet bridge: central command chairs, forward conn/ops sightline, tactical and mission-status stations, aft displays for route and convoy overlays, and side consoles for communications and damage control. It should feel flatter and more integrated than a Galaxy bridge.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, the bridge becomes a traffic-control room. Voices shorten. Conn reports relative velocity. Tactical names arcs. Ops tracks power and damage. Mission ops or science pushes uncertainty into the command conversation.

### Scene shapes

- A convoy clearance scene where every safe route has a political cost.
- A shuttle recovery scene where bridge timing must protect both the bay and the ship.
- A salvage custody argument conducted while tractor load numbers change in real time.
- A battle-line scene where the Steamrunner holds one flank rather than winning the whole battle alone.

## 5.2 Ready room and briefing room, Decks 1-2

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Adjacent to bridge or directly below it in command support |
| Function | Private command judgment and senior-staff synthesis |
| Scene use | Acting-captain transition, witness interviews, route policy, inquiry preparation |

### Location engine

These rooms convert operational noise into decisions a commander must personally own.

### Physical identity

Keep them compact. A ready room may have a narrow viewport or forward-facing display wall, a desk, a replicator, a small case of personal objects, and route-status access. The briefing room is more utilitarian than ceremonial: table, wall displays, secure evidence cabinet, emergency update channel.

### Pressure behavior

Private scenes should not become detached from the ship. A conversation about law may be interrupted by a cargo-bay injury, a deflector status change, or a civilian captain asking for clearance.

### Scene shapes

- Captain missing or incapacitated; the player receives command in a room built for decisions, not comfort.
- Senior staff debate whether chart publication will save lives or expose a sanctuary.
- Recovery law and tactical necessity collide over one wreck, one survivor, or one sealed manifest.

## 5.3 Wardroom, mess, and crew commons, Decks 3-4

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Mid-forward primary hull, close enough to command spaces for recurring use |
| Function | Meals, morale, informal diplomacy, survivor holding, crew rumor pressure |
| Scene use | Civilian reassurance, crew fatigue, command rapport, post-mission aftermath |

### Location engine

The wardroom turns route policy into ordinary life. It is where officers see whether their decisions made people safer, hungrier, angrier, or stranded.

### Central contradiction

It is a casual space on a ship that rarely feels off duty. A meal can become a testimony, a survivor intake, or a political negotiation before anyone finishes their coffee.

### Physical identity

Use compact tables, replicator alcoves, low display panels, route-status chatter, and windows or display walls that can show the region under discussion. It should not become a Ten Forward duplicate; it is a working mess with enough dignity for guests.

### Pressure behavior

Crowding changes the room first. Evacuees, salvage witnesses, escort crews, or displaced families make ordinary crew routines visibly costly.

### Scene shapes

- A survivor recognizes a Starfleet officer and asks why the route was closed.
- Two departments relax until a convoy alarm turns the commons into a staging room.
- A civilian leader accepts tea but refuses Starfleet's map terms.

## 5.4 Sickbay and medical isolation, Deck 5

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Deck 5, mid-hull, protected from flight-deck exposure |
| Function | Medical treatment, quarantine, casualty sorting, survivor stabilization |
| Scene use | Recovery cost, contamination, triage ethics, command consequence |

### Location engine

Steamrunner sickbay turns the ship's public mission into bodies, infection risk, and triage math.

### Central contradiction

The ship can look tactical from outside, but sickbay often decides the mission's moral shape. Who receives a biobed, who waits, and who must be transported elsewhere are command consequences.

### Physical identity

Use a main ward with several biobeds, surgical bay, CMO office, compact lab, decon room, isolation rooms, and overflow protocols into nearby compartments. The medical suite should be close enough to transporter and cargo routes for casualty flow, but not so exposed that shuttle accidents spill directly into primary care.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, sickbay expands into corridors. Privacy collapses. Medical authority interrupts command convenience. Biofilters, quarantine fields, and decontamination logs become plot-bearing objects.

### Scene shapes

- A shuttle brings survivors contaminated by an unknown field condition.
- A wounded captain forces command succession before the player is emotionally ready.
- The doctor refuses a transport because the pattern data is not clean enough.

## 5.5 Transporter rooms, security, and armory, Deck 6

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Deck 6, central enough for bridge, sickbay, cargo, and security routing |
| Function | Personnel transfer, evidence custody, tactical deployment, prisoner handling |
| Scene use | Boarding risk, salvage law, refugee movement, armed response |

### Location engine

Transporters turn intent into irreversible movement. Security turns that movement into accountability.

### Physical identity

Use six-pad transporter rooms, operator booths, adjacent equipment lockers, secured arms issue, a compact brig, interview room, and evidence storage. Keep armory detail controlled; Starfleet accountability matters more than weapon glamour.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, the area becomes procedural. Names are checked, biofilters log exceptions, security escorts mark custody, and a single transporter lock can become a legal or ethical decision.

### Scene shapes

- A salvage team demands beam-in access before ownership is settled.
- A survivor refuses transport because the last beam-out killed someone.
- Security catches a route marker hidden in personal effects.

## 5.6 Mission ops, route analysis, and science labs, Decks 7-8

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Decks 7-8, broad primary hull near sensor trunks and command access |
| Function | Route validation, hazard analysis, survey science, evidence reconstruction |
| Scene use | Charts, wreck emergence, anomalies, sanctuary detection, false certainty |

### Location engine

Mission ops is where the Steamrunner earns its right to say "safe enough."

### Central contradiction

The room produces clear maps from dirty data. Its danger is confidence that looks more stable than the route actually is.

### Physical identity

Use wall-scale route displays, sensor-fusion tables, probe telemetry, sealed evidence readers, science benches, and secure chart custody. For Glass Harbor, this space can favor hydrographic and gravitic route modeling. For Serein, it can favor wreck-emergence prediction, custody logs, and ordnance hazard overlays.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, mission ops becomes adversarial in a useful way. Science argues uncertainty, ops argues traffic, tactical argues exposure, and command must decide when the model is good enough.

### Scene shapes

- A route looks clear until one sensor mode disagrees.
- A chart that saves a convoy would also expose a hidden community.
- Wreckage appears where the model said no mass could survive.

## 5.7 Cargo, salvage, and recovery holds, Decks 9-10

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Decks 9-10, aft-central service hull |
| Function | Cargo, relief pallets, salvage sorting, survivor property, field equipment |
| Scene use | Physical evidence, scarcity, repair parts, disaster logistics |

### Location engine

Cargo holds make abstract rescue material. Every policy choice becomes mass, volume, inventory, locks, and labels.

### Central contradiction

The holds are mundane until they become the most politically important rooms aboard: who owns a crate, a seed vault, a beacon, a manifest, or a sealed coffin can redirect an entire campaign.

### Physical identity

Use pallet rails, modular containers, environmental cages, salvage tables, temporary bunks, cargo lifts, wall-mounted tractor controls, and hazard tape. The space should be large enough for small-craft parts and evacuation supplies but not infinite.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, cargo volume runs out before compassion does. Security, medical, engineering, and command all need the same square meters for different reasons.

### Scene shapes

- A recovered container forces legal custody decisions.
- A cargo bay becomes a temporary family shelter.
- An engineering team strips noncritical stores to repair a tractor emitter.

## 5.8 Main engineering and aft deflector control, Decks 11-12

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Decks 11-12, aft service concentration near power transfer and deflector trunk |
| Function | Warp power, impulse support, tractor load, aft deflector coordination |
| Scene use | Power tradeoffs, damage control, towing, field pulses, repair triage |

### Location engine

Steamrunner engineering turns route promises into physics.

### Central contradiction

The ship looks broad and simple from outside, but its aft systems are crowded with dependencies. A command to tow, shield, beam, and recover shuttles at once becomes an engineering argument almost immediately.

### Physical identity

Use a compact two-level core room or vertical core bay, power-transfer consoles, engineering office, repair bench, aft deflector control alcove, tractor-load displays, and access trunks toward nacelle cowlings. The aft deflector is a character in engineering scenes because it shares space and power with recovery tasks.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, engineering speaks in costs. Deflector activity may prevent shuttle recovery. Tractor load may starve shields. Cowling heat may limit warp. Repair teams may have to crawl into spaces never meant for long conversations.

### Scene shapes

- The chief engineer has to shut down the aft deflector before a damaged shuttle can cross the approach line.
- A tow job threatens the same power grid needed for medical quarantine.
- A cowling access point overheats while the ship holds station in a storm.

## 5.9 Aft-lower flight deck, Deck 13

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Deck 13, aft-lower service volume above/around the detached deflector housing |
| Function | Shuttle launch, recovery, maintenance, field teams, emergency intake |
| Scene use | Away-team launch, return under fire, survivor intake, repair pressure |

### Location engine

The flight deck turns missions into physical arrivals and departures.

### Central contradiction

It is the ship's most kinetic workspace, but it depends on stillness: stable force fields, clean approach arcs, locked cargo, secured patients, and disciplined timing.

### Physical identity

Use a recessed bay, force-field threshold, approach strobes, maintenance cradles, small-craft parts, cargo tie-downs, deck-control booth, and adjacent ready lockers. The space should feel smaller than a starbase hangar and busier than a ceremonial shuttle bay.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, the bay becomes a choke point. Pilots, medics, engineers, security, and cargo handlers all need the same deck at once.

### Scene shapes

- A shuttle returns with one nacelle failing while the aft deflector has not fully powered down.
- Evacuees enter through the bay and immediately overload medical and security routing.
- A damaged shuttle consumes the parts reserved for a later rescue.

## 5.10 Jefferies tubes and lower support, Decks 14-16

### At a glance

| Field | Baseline |
|---|---|
| Location | Multi-deck maintenance network, densest aft and around nacelle cowlings |
| Function | Repair access, environmental support, shield-grid service, emergency routing |
| Scene use | Physical risk, hidden damage, private confession, systems mystery |

### Location engine

Maintenance access turns the sleek hull into a body with heat, vibration, narrow passages, and unglamorous labor.

### Central contradiction

The ship's public authority depends on people crawling through spaces no civilian will ever see.

### Physical identity

Use ladder tubes, crawlways near cowling roots, warm conduits, shield-grid panels, environmental valves, and emergency access into cargo or engineering spaces.

### Pressure behavior

Under pressure, Jefferies tubes become alternate routes when lifts are sealed and force fields divide the ship. They are useful, but slow, hot, and dangerous.

### Scene shapes

- A junior engineer identifies damage by vibration before a diagnostic confirms it.
- Two officers crawl toward a cowling relay while a convoy waits for clearance.
- A private argument happens in a place where both people have to keep working.

# 6. Ship Systems as Story Pressure

## 6.1 Aft deflector and field geometry

### System engine

The Steamrunner's aft deflector makes the class physically distinctive and narratively useful. Field work happens through the stern, so towing, hazard scans, shuttle recovery, and escape vectors can interfere with each other.

### Story use

Use the aft deflector when:

- a route scan requires the ship to point or hold station in an inconvenient posture;
- a shuttle recovery must wait for deflector power-down;
- a tractor tow distorts sensor readings;
- the ship's most important system is behind it rather than ahead of it.

### Avoid

Do not casually move the main deflector to the bow for generic starship prose.

## 6.2 Tractor, towing, and convoy control

### System engine

The class is ideal for tractor-and-escort pressure. It can secure one wreck, guide one convoy, or hold one dangerous line, but it cannot physically solve every local movement problem at once.

### Story use

Good Steamrunner tractor scenes involve load, drift, anchor points, shield geometry, and legal custody. The question is often not "can the ship tow it," but "what becomes exposed while it does."

### Avoid

Do not make tractors magic hands. Mass, spin, hull integrity, shields, and field interference matter.

## 6.3 Tactical systems

### System engine

Phaser arrays and photon torpedoes give the Steamrunner credible threat presence. That credibility should pressure diplomacy as much as combat.

### Story use

Use tactical systems to show:

- route denial;
- convoy cover;
- warning shots with political cost;
- defensive screens around shuttles or civilians;
- postwar discomfort when Starfleet arrives looking armed.

### Avoid

Do not turn the class into a Defiant replacement. It is combat-capable, not a tiny battleship.

## 6.4 Sensors, route maps, and chart custody

### System engine

The Steamrunner's most Directive-useful system is not a weapon. It is the ability to make contested space legible enough for command action.

### Story use

Use sensor and chart systems when:

- a route needs verification;
- a sanctuary may be exposed by good data;
- a wreck or emergence point contradicts the model;
- a local community disputes Starfleet's right to publish a chart.

### Avoid

Do not make scans omniscient. Good sensors still produce uncertainty, false confidence, political exposure, and blind spots.

## 6.5 Small-craft and cargo integration

### System engine

Flight deck, cargo, transporter, and medical routing are interdependent. Steamrunner rescue scenes should feel like a chain, not isolated rooms.

### Story use

Use this system when:

- survivors arrive by shuttle and overflow medical;
- cargo locks must stay open while shields are stressed;
- a field team needs a shuttle while cargo has priority;
- a recovery operation forces security to track people and property together.

### Avoid

Do not let every compartment absorb every crisis. The ship has volume limits.

# 7. Movement and Scene Geography

## 7.1 Useful default routes

| Route | Typical scene use |
|---|---|
| Bridge to ready room | Public command to private ownership |
| Bridge to briefing room | Tactical or route data to senior-staff synthesis |

## 7.2 Scene transitions

A strong Steamrunner transition gives at least one of:

- a change from map to mass;
- a change from route authority to human consequence;
- an aft/forward hull orientation;
- a tactical arc or shield-state change;
- a reminder that recovery, cargo, medical, and security share traffic.

Examples:

- From bridge to mission ops: command silence gives way to overlapping sensor models and contested routes.
- From mission ops to cargo: clean chart lines become labeled crates, straps, and property claims.
- From flight deck to sickbay: engine noise and bay strobes become medical light and triage pressure.
- From engineering to Jefferies access: power estimates become hot conduits and the smell of insulation.

## 8.3 Over-injection guard

Do not inject every matching fact. If a player says "I go to the wardroom," the scene does not need the aft deflector, class length, and Dominion War history. It needs the room's social use, crowding pressure if relevant, and perhaps the ship's route-security culture.

Use high detail when:

- the player is making a command decision about movement, rescue, combat, or custody;
- a generated response is likely to hallucinate a generic forward deflector or saucer shuttlebay;
- a scene depends on exact route, deck, exterior placement, or system tradeoff;
- contradiction guards need a clear prohibition.

Use low detail when:

- the area is only a backdrop;
- character work is the scene's main payload;
- the same ship fact was just established;
- the player does not need technical overhead to act.

# 9. Portrayal Principles

1. **The ship is infrastructure under pressure.** Make the Steamrunner feel most alive when it keeps routes, wrecks, shuttles, and people moving.
2. **Combat credibility is not the whole identity.** Tactical presence should support escort, rescue, and route security.
3. **Aft geometry matters.** The deflector is aft; small-craft recovery is an aft-lower operation in Directive baseline.
4. **Movement creates politics.** Safe passage, chart custody, salvage rights, and rescue priority are class-native story pressure.
5. **Compact does not mean empty.** The ship has departments, labs, medical care, crew life, and diplomacy, but the same spaces recur.
6. **Damage has a job location.** Name tractor emitters, cowling access, aft deflector control, cargo locks, flight deck, or shield arcs.
7. **Starfleet competence is baseline.** Conflict comes from tradeoffs, incomplete data, limited volume, politics, and moral cost.
8. **Do not import another class's deck map.** Intrepid, Galaxy, Defiant, and Sovereign assumptions must not overwrite Steamrunner geometry.
9. **Campaign ships own their modifications.** Glass Harbor and Serein can differ, but class truth remains the common frame.
10. **Runtime packets must be selective.** The bible is deep source; future datasets should distill only relevant scene facts.
