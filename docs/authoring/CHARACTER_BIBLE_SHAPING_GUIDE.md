# Character Bible Shaping Guide

This guide defines the target depth and structure for Directive senior-staff character bibles.

The reference standard is the Breckenridge / Ashes of Peace senior staff bible at `docs/source/Directive_Breckenridge_Senior_Staff_Character_Bible.md`. Other campaign bibles should be raised toward that depth before playtest promotion. The goal is not longer text for its own sake. The goal is enough authored personality, history, voice, and relationship pressure for the runtime crew dataset to produce distinct people instead of interchangeable Starfleet roles.

## Core Standard

A Directive character bible should make each major NPC playable from four angles:

- as a competent professional with a concrete job;
- as a person with habits, contradictions, warmth, and blind spots;
- as a relationship partner for the player command character;
- as a source of campaign pressure, dissent, trust, and development.

The bible is the deep source. It is not injected wholesale. It should give authors and dataset builders enough material to create compact runtime cards, voice capsules, reveal gates, relationship dynamics, and scene guidance.

## Reference Shape

The Ashes bible works because it does more than list traits. For each major officer it defines:

- public role and service-record facts;
- causal history that explains behavior without excusing it;
- central strength and central flaw;
- values, stress behavior, humor, and blind spots;
- relationship triggers with the player;
- relationships with the rest of the senior staff;
- long-term character pressures;
- reveal ladder;
- voice guide and example dialogue;
- ensemble dynamics and coalition rules.

New and revised campaign bibles should use the same design ambition, even when their exact section names differ.

## Document Spine

A mature senior-staff bible should contain these sections.

## 1. Shared Starting Context

Define the command culture before the player meaningfully changes it.

Include:

- ship or station history that affects the crew now;
- why this crew is together;
- which officers already know each other;
- recent trauma, success, failure, refit, transfer, political pressure, or mission strain;
- what the player changes by arriving, taking command, or accepting a role;
- what each major officer immediately wants from the player.

The starting context should create a living ensemble. The player should join an existing command culture, not a blank roster waiting to be assigned personalities.

## 2. Major Officer Profiles

Each major senior officer should receive a full profile. For a playtest-ready campaign, target roughly 1,500 to 3,000 words per major officer when the character is expected to recur throughout the campaign. Draft packages can be shorter, but every missing category should be treated as unfinished authoring work.

Use this structure.

### At A Glance

Use a compact table or bullet list:

- full name;
- species;
- age or age frame when relevant;
- birthplace or formative origin;
- rank and billet;
- service background;
- time aboard;
- public reputation;
- central strength;
- central flaw;
- campaign function.

This is not the whole character. It is the index.

### Role In The Ensemble

State why this character exists in the campaign.

Good roles test command questions:

- who names cost when others chase speed;
- who challenges evidence when politics wants certainty;
- who protects people through procedure;
- who sees informal obligations before command does;
- who can execute an ugly order without becoming ugly.

Avoid roles that are only approval meters, quest dispensers, or genre stereotypes.

### Formative History

Write history that still acts on the present.

Keep:

- professional experiences that shaped judgment;
- family, culture, mentor, or institutional background that affects current behavior;
- one or two defining successes;
- one or two costly failures;
- how they interpret those events now.

Cut trivia that cannot affect play.

### Personality

Define the person through engine, contradiction, and behavior.

Required fields:

- **Core engine:** what the character is really doing in the room.
- **Central contradiction:** the tension that keeps them from becoming a type.
- **Default demeanor:** what the player sees first.
- **Values:** what they believe even when it costs them.
- **Strengths:** what they can do under pressure.
- **Blind spots:** where competence misleads them.
- **Stress behavior:** how their voice, posture, judgment, and habits change.
- **Warmth and humor:** how humanity appears.
- **Physical tells:** distinctive gestures, posture, working habits, or room behavior.

Physical tells matter. Do not give every officer the same folded hands, narrowed eyes, quiet voice, or unreadable expression.

### Personal Life And Habits

Include enough ordinary life to prevent characters from existing only in briefings.

Useful details:

- room or workspace objects;
- drink, food, music, reading, exercise, spiritual, or craft habits;
- correspondence and off-duty rituals;
- friendships, family, partnership, or chosen solitude;
- what relaxes them and what they pretend does not.

These details should be playable, not decorative. They should give the narrator grounded actions and private-scene texture.

### Relationship With The Player

Define what the officer needs from the player and what changes trust.

Include:

- what they initially want;
- what they fear the player will do;
- what earns professional confidence;
- what earns integrity trust;
- what earns personal rapport;
- what lowers each;
- what high trust looks like;
- what disagreement looks like without breaking the relationship.

Use concrete triggers. "Likes decisive commanders" is too weak. "Gains confidence when the commander names uncertainty, sets abort criteria, and protects sound advisers after a failed plan" is usable.

### Relationships With Senior Staff

Map the ensemble, not just the player axis.

Include:

- established trust;
- useful friction;
- personal discomfort;
- professional dependencies;
- what each pair can disagree about without becoming enemies;
- which relationships change under pressure.

The crew should be able to generate scenes when the player is not the emotional center.

### Long-Term Character Pressures

List playable arcs or recurring pressures.

Good pressures are questions:

- When does proper process become delayed courage?
- Can ambition become responsibility before it becomes performance?
- Can protected dissent stay rigorous instead of becoming fixation?
- When does care become control?
- What debt is command allowed to carry forward?

These are not guaranteed arcs. They are pressure lines the campaign can activate.

### Reveal Ladder

Separate what the player can know from what the Director may use.

Use at least:

- **Service record:** public or official facts.
- **Ordinary professional conversation:** things the player can learn through normal work.
- **High trust or crisis disclosure:** personal history, fears, failures, correspondence, unresolved grief.
- **Never automatic:** truths that should not appear unless campaign state explicitly earns them.

The reveal ladder is how the bible stays rich without leaking hidden history in the first scene.

### Voice Guide

A voice guide should be useful under prompt budget pressure. It must include more than adjectives.

Required:

- sentence rhythm;
- diction;
- question style;
- interruption or correction style;
- humor style;
- how warmth appears;
- how stress changes the voice;
- what the character never sounds like;
- example line shapes.

Example line shapes are syntax and posture examples, not catchphrases. They should teach how the character forms a line without requiring exact reuse.

### Example Line Shapes

Provide 8 to 12 line shapes per major officer.

The set should cover:

- command pressure;
- correction;
- disagreement;
- warmth;
- humor;
- private trust;
- crisis;
- ordinary work.

Do not include only severe lines. If every example is a rebuke, the runtime will write a cold character even when the bible says they are warm.

For Whitaker, the useful mix is:

```text
"I have six assessments. I need one recommendation."
"That is tidy. I am asking whether it is true."
"I would rather have your honest caveat than a clean report I cannot use."
"You are settling into the chair, Commander. Do not mistake discomfort for failure."
"I asked for your judgment because I wanted your judgment. Not a diplomatic version of mine."
"Come in. I have coffee, tea, and three reports pretending not to contradict each other."
```

That range teaches precision and warmth together.

## 3. Ensemble Dynamics

After the individual profiles, define the crew as a working group.

Required:

- starting relationship map;
- what each officer initially wants from the player;
- likely coalitions by situation;
- who speaks in which kinds of scenes;
- who should stay silent when another officer is the right speaker;
- how command disagreements end;
- how personal relationships complicate but do not replace professional duty.

This section prevents every meeting from becoming seven officers taking equal turns stating moral positions.

## 4. Portrayal Principles

Every bible should close with authoring rules.

Recommended principles:

- Competence is baseline.
- No character is a command-style mascot.
- Approval is not trust.
- History explains behavior without excusing it.
- Deep history is earned.
- Characters have work and goals outside the player.
- Voice consistency is not repetition.
- Characters can be relaxed, funny, tired, petty, generous, or ordinary.
- Professional disagreement ends when command requires execution.
- The player shapes the crew over time.

## 5. Working Summary

End with one compact line per major officer.

The line should state the command question they bring into the campaign:

```text
Mara Whitaker gives the ship principled strategic direction and tests whether institutions remain worthy of trust.
Hadrik Bronn tests whether a command decision can withstand experience, hostility, and system failure.
```

These lines help dataset authors verify that the ensemble is not duplicating the same function.

## Depth Targets

Use these as practical targets, not hard limits.

| Character class | Source depth | Runtime expectation |
|---|---:|---|
| Major captain or player-counterpart officer | 2,000-3,500 words | Full six-card dataset plus voice capsule |
| Recurring senior officer | 1,500-3,000 words | Full six-card dataset plus voice capsule |
| Supporting bridge or department figure | 400-1,000 words | Profile, voice capsule, relationship hook |
| Recurring civilian, faction, or antagonist | 600-1,500 words | Profile, pressure role, voice capsule, reveal gates as needed |
| Minor named character | 100-300 words | Identity, function, one tell, one line-shape rule |

The Breckenridge bible is the target for major senior staff. It is acceptable for draft packages to start lower, but the playtest bar should require major officers to feel comparably authored.

## Raising Existing Campaigns

Upgrade existing non-Ashes bibles in this order:

1. Confirm the campaign command frame and starting ensemble.
2. Expand each senior officer profile to include engine, contradiction, stress behavior, warmth, tells, player relationship, crew relationships, and reveal ladder.
3. Add 8 to 12 example line shapes per officer with a mix of pressure and warmth.
4. Add ensemble dynamics and coalition rules.
5. Convert the bible into richer crew dataset cards.
6. Verify prompt-context tests use the compact runtime cards, not the whole bible.

The bible pass and dataset pass should be linked but separate. The bible can be expansive. The dataset must be selective, gated, and prompt-budget aware.
